Not All Cans Are Created Equal

If you walk down the canned food aisle at any grocery store you'll see hundreds of options. Most of them are absolutely terrible for survival stockpiling. Canned green beans? 70 calories. Canned peas? 240 calories for the entire can. You'd need to eat 15 cans a day just to not starve. How is that sustainable?

But some canned foods are absolute workhorses. Some of them are high calorie, high protein, ridiculously long shelf life, and they taste good enough that your family won't revolt on day three. Those are the ones worth buying in bulk whenever you can.

I've been stockpiling canned goods for years in our NJ house and at the cabin in PA and I've learned that the best survival food is food people will actually eat. It really doesn't matter how many calories per ounce something has if your seven year old won't touch it.

So here's my honest ranked list that I've spent time and time again going through. Calories, protein, shelf life, and the taste/practicality factor that nobody else seems to rank.

The Heavy Hitters: Best Canned Foods for Survival

1. Spam (and Generic Luncheon Meat)

I know. Spam has a reputation and personally it makes me cringe, but just look at the numbers.

One 12 oz can of Spam has 1,080 calories and 36g of protein, which is basically almost half your daily calories in a single can. It lasts 3-5 years on the shelf (and honestly way longer, there are reports of people eating Spam from the 1990s and being fine). It needs zero preparation and you can eat it cold right out of the can, you savage, you!

It's salty and fatty. And those are features, not bugs, when you're burning 3,000+ calories a day in an emergency situation. Your body needs sodium and fat. Spam delivers both of those.

My kids eat it fried with rice. My wife eats it in fried rice (there's a pattern here). I eat it on crackers straight from the can. It's versatile and it's cheap, about $3-4 per can. I don't eat it all the time, just from time and time again so I adjust to it, but for me I'll pretty much eat anything knowing that its going to serve a purpose and that purpose is sustainability.

Buy generic lunch meat if Spam is too expensive. Same stuff and half the price. Lunch meat is something I rarely eat. Not because I don't like it, but because it's very highly processed.

2. Canned Chili (With Beans)

It doesn't matter if it's Hormel or store brand. A 15 oz can of chili with beans gives you roughly 500-600 calories, 25-30g of protein, and a meal that tastes like actual food. It's warm, filling, and you can eat it heated or room temperature... and heck, you can even make some homemade bread with the flour you have stored to make it super comfort food.

The beans add fiber and complex carbs that keep you feeling full. This is one of the few canned foods that's actually kind of, sort of, complete a meal on its own. Pair it with crackers or rice and you've got a solid 800-calorie dinner.

I keep at least two cases (24 cans) in the pantry at all times. Shelf life is 3-5 years. Probably longer and if I'm working a long day and my wife had a busy day and she couldn't make dinner, for me it's an easy meal.

3. Canned Chicken

A 12.5 oz can of chunk chicken breast gives you about 350 calories and 60-70g of protein. That protein number is insane for the weight. It's pre-cooked, shelf stable for 3-5 years, and you can toss it in anything from rice, wraps, pasta, or just eat it straight with your favorite hot sauce.

I like the Kirkland (Costco) brand. I can get a six-pack for around $15. That's 2,100 calories and 360g of protein for fifteen bucks. Hard to beat that!

4. Canned Beef Stew

Dinty Moore or store brand has about 800 calories per can, decent protein, and it's an actual hot meal. Open the can, heat it up on your camp stove or eat room temperature, feed a person. Done.

My kids will voluntarily eat this - that alone puts it in the top five.

5. Canned Tuna

Ah yes, the classic. A 5 oz can of tuna in water is only about 120 calories, which is low, but it packs 25-30g of protein. Tuna in oil bumps that to about 180 calories. I stock the oil packed version for survival purposes because every calorie counts. I normally don't eat the tuna with oil, so when it's time to rotate stock I'll just donate it to a local food shelter.

You can mash it up with a packet of mayo (those single-serve packs from any fast food place), put it on crackers or a tortilla and it's a solid meal for pennies on the dollar.

It has a shelf life of 3-5 years. Some sources say 10+ years. It's one of those foods that basically lasts forever as long as the can isn't damaged.

6. Canned Salmon

Similar to tuna but with more calories and omega-3s. A 14.75 oz can of pink salmon runs about 600 calories and 80g of protein and it's pricier than tuna but nutritionally superior. Bones are soft and edible (vitamins) they're a calcium source, but some people get squeamish about that. I'm not.

7. Canned Beans (Black, Kidney, Pinto)

A 15 oz can of black beans is about 350 calories, 21g of protein, and packed with fiber. They're dirt cheap and often under a dollar per can. They can be mixed with rice and they form a complete protein that covers all your amino acids.

They're not the most exciting food and you may experience some side effects (if you know what I mean), but rice and beans has kept civilizations alive for thousands of years. There's a reason for that.

8. Canned Pasta (Chef Boyardee)

I'm not pretending this is health food. It's probably the worst thing you can eat, but a 15 oz can of Beefaroni is 440 calories. Kids will eat it without being asked twice, and it costs about a dollar. In a survival situation, a dollar per can of food that your kid inhales happily is worth more than the fanciest freeze-dried meal that sits uneaten.

It's shelf life is 2-3 years, which isn't the longest, but it rotates easily because, again, kids eat it voluntarily.

9. Canned Soup (Chunky/Hearty Varieties)

Skip the condensed broth based soups, because they're mostly water and sodium. Go for the chunky/hearty varieties with meat and vegetables. Campbell's Chunky Sirloin Burger soup is about 400 calories per can with decent protein. Progresso does similar ones.

Soup is underrated as survival food. It's warm, it hydrates (partially because there's water in it), and it requires zero effort. Just heat and eat. I can tell you that I have eaten many cans of chicken noodle soup up at the cabin that have been through frozen Winters at or around zero degrees and Summers that have reached the 90's. I went through an older kitchen cabinet with my brother in law, Adam, and we found canned soup that was over 5 years old and opened it and cooked it just to try it. Of course we did the smell test first. There was a little separation of things, if that even makes sense, but we didn't get sick and it actually smelled and tasted just fine.

10. Canned Fruit in Heavy Syrup

Peaches, pears, mixed fruit all are the heavy syrup versions specifically. The syrup is sugar water, which means extra calories. A 15 oz can of peaches in heavy syrup is about 300 calories. More importantly, it tastes GOOD. In a week of eating chili and Spam, a can of sweet peaches feels like dessert – no lie. It's straight up morale boosting goodness. Don't underestimate it.

Also the syrup is drinkable sugar water. In an emergency, calories are calories and you need the most of what you can get.

How Much to Stock

Here's my rule of thumb for canned goods as part of a broader food storage plan:

2 weeks worth for "getting started." That's roughly 60-80 cans for a family of four, mixed across the categories above. That sounds like a lot but it's about 4-5 cases of canned goods, which easily stack on top of each other and does not take up that much space. This can easily fit on a storage shelf.

3 months is the sweet spot for serious preparedness. At that point you're talking 200+ cans, but you're also supplementing this with dry goods (rice, beans, pasta), freeze-dried food, and other shelf stable stuff.

Use the Food Storage Calculator to get your actual numbers – it's FREE to use. It factors in your family size and dietary needs so you don't over buy in one category and under buy in another.

Storage Tips That Actually Matter

Cool, dark, and dry. Same as everything else in food storage. A pantry works. A basement shelf works. A closet works. A root cellar works. A hot garage in July does not work because heat accelerates degradation and can cause cans to swell (I wouldn't risk it)

FIFO - first in, first out. When you buy new cans, put them behind the old ones. Eat the old ones first. This is not complicated but people still mess it up and end up with ancient cans buried behind fresh ones. You can use a sharpie to write on the bottom or top of the can to keep track.

Watch for damage. Dented cans are usually fine as long as the dent is small and the can isn't leaking, swollen, or rusted. Bulging cans you just have to throw them out immediately. That's botulism territory and botulism will kill you faster than any emergency scenario.

Again, write the purchase date on each can with a Sharpie. Yeah, cans have "best by" dates, but those are often stamped in tiny unreadable codes. A big Sharpie date means you can glance at your shelf and instantly know what is the oldest.

The "Canned Food Only" Mistake

Don't build your entire food storage around cans, they are heavy. A single can of beef stew weighs a pound. A month of canned food for a family of four weighs 200+ lbs. You can't put that in a bug out bag. You can barely fit it all on a shelf.

Canned food is best as the eat first layer of your storage plan. The stuff you grab during a 3-7 day power outage when the media is blaring fear. Behind that, you want lightweight dry goods (rice, beans, oats, pasta) in long term storage and freeze-dried meals as the deep reserve.

Think of it as three layers:

  • Layer 1 (Days 1-7): Canned goods + pantry staples. No cooking required or simple heating.

  • Layer 2 (Days 7-30): Bulk dry goods - rice, beans, pasta, oats. Requires cooking.

  • Layer 3 (Months 1-12+): Long-term storage - sealed mylar bags of grains, freeze-dried meals, specialty items.

Our rice storage guide covers Layer 2, and the Food Storage Calculator helps you plan across all three layers.

What About "Survival" Branded Canned Food?

Companies like Augason Farms and others sell "survival" branded canned food at premium prices. Some of it is decent. Most of it is stuff you can buy at Walmart for a third of the price, so keep your eyes peeled.

A #10 can of freeze dried chicken from a survival brand runs $35-50. A 6 pack of canned chicken from Costco is $15. The freeze dried version lasts longer (25 years vs 5 years) but costs 4-5x more per serving. For most people, regular grocery store canned goods with a good rotation system is the better play for you and your wallet/purse.

Save the survival branded stuff for the deep long term storage where the 25-year shelf life actually matters and you can properly store it. For your working stockpile, just buy what you normally eat, buy more of it, and rotate through it.

The Shopping Strategy

Don't try to build a 3-month stockpile in one shopping trip. You'll spend $300-400 and your spouse will question your sanity and if your spouse isn't quite on board with you yet... give it time, they will be when you show them you have the goods during the next disaster or power outage.

Instead: buy an extra 5-10 cans every grocery run. Whatever's on sale (look for those can-can sales). Just buy a little more than you need each week. In two months, you'll have a solid 2-week supply without feeling the financial hit. In six months, you're at a month. In a year, you're in great shape.

Watch for sales. Canned goods go on sale constantly, especially around Thanksgiving and the start of hurricane season. You should take this opportunity to stock up when they're cheap. I've gotten cases of chili for under $1/can during clearance sales.

And don't forget the can opener. I keep three of them. One in the kitchen drawer, one in the emergency supplies, and one in the bug out bag and if my wife is reading this a fourth that is an unbeknownst location (only because I know if she finds it, it'll be lost forever) Because opening 30 cans of food with a pocket knife is technically possible but absolutely miserable and you can risk cutting yourself on the knife or the can.

Bottom Line

Canned food is the backbone of short-term emergency food storage. It's affordable, it's shelf-stable, it requires minimal preparation, and if you choose wisely it's food your family already eats.

Start with the top five on this list. Build a two-week supply. Rotate through it. Then expand from there. It's not glamorous and it's not Instagram worthy. However, when the lights go out and the stores are closed, that shelf full of Spam and chili is going to look like the smartest investment you ever made.
