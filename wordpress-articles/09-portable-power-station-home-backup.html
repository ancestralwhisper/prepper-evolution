Everyone Buys the Wrong Size

I see it all the time. Someone buys a 300Wh power station because the price was right, plugs in their fridge during a power outage, and it dies in 2 hours. Or they buy a massive 5,000Wh unit for $3,000 and all they ever run is a phone charger and a lamp.

The right size power station for home backup depends on exactly one thing: what do you need to keep running and for how long?

Not what you'd LIKE to run. Not your whole house. Just the stuff that actually matters when the grid goes down. Once you figure that out, the sizing is just math. And I'm going to walk you through that math right now because it's simpler than everyone makes it sound.

Step 1: List Your Must-Run Devices

Grab a piece of paper. Write down what you absolutely need to keep running during a power outage. For most families, it looks something like this:

  • Refrigerator (so food doesn't spoil)
  • Phone charging (communication and information)
  • WiFi router + modem (internet for updates and communication)
  • A few LED lights (so you can see)
  • Maybe a fan in summer or a small heater (though electric heaters are power hogs)
  • Medical devices — CPAP, nebulizer, oxygen concentrator (if applicable)

Notice what's NOT on that list. TV. Microwave. Hair dryer. Dishwasher. Air conditioner. Those are convenience items that draw huge power. During an outage, you're in conservation mode. Everything that isn't keeping food cold, people safe, or devices charged gets unplugged.

Step 2: Know Your Wattages

Every device has a wattage rating. Some are printed on the device, some you need to look up. Here's a quick reference for the common stuff:

Device Running Watts Hours/Day Daily Wh
Refrigerator 100-150W (avg) 8-10 (compressor cycles on/off) 800-1,500
Phone charger 10-20W 2-3 30-60
WiFi router + modem 10-20W 24 240-480
LED lamp/light 5-15W 6-8 40-120
Laptop 50-65W 4-6 200-390
Box fan 50-75W 8-12 400-900
CPAP machine 30-60W 8 240-480
TV (LED, 50") 80-120W 4 320-480
Space heater 750-1,500W Don't Don't

Yes, I put "don't" for the space heater. A 1,500W space heater will drain a 2,000Wh power station in about 80 minutes. It's the single worst thing you can plug into a battery. Use blankets, dress in layers, or get a propane indoor-safe heater like a Mr. Buddy if heat is your concern.

Step 3: Do the Math

Add up your daily Wh needs. For a typical family running the basics:

  • Fridge: 1,000 Wh
  • 2 phones charging: 60 Wh
  • Router: 360 Wh
  • 3 LED lights: 240 Wh
  • Laptop: 250 Wh

Total: roughly 1,900 Wh per day.

So a 2,000Wh power station gets you through about one day on that load. If the outage lasts longer, you need to recharge — either from solar panels, a generator, or your car (though car charging is slow and burns gas).

Here's the thing most people miss: a power station alone is only half the solution. Without a way to recharge it, you just have one day of power and then a really expensive paperweight. Solar panels or a small generator for recharging is what turns a power station from a one-day buffer into an indefinite solution.

The Size Tiers — What Each Range Gets You

Small: 300-500Wh ($150-400)

Good for: Phone/device charging, LED lights, router for a few hours.

Not good for: Anything with a compressor (fridge, AC) or high draw. Will NOT run your fridge overnight.

Best for: Apartment dwellers with limited needs, or as a secondary/bedroom unit alongside a larger primary station.

Medium: 1,000-1,500Wh ($500-1,000)

Good for: Running a fridge for 8-12 hours, plus device charging and lights. This is the "survive one night" tier.

Not good for: Multi-day outages without solar recharging.

Best for: Areas with occasional short outages (6-12 hours). Budget-conscious buyers who'll add solar panels later.

Large: 2,000-3,000Wh ($1,000-2,000)

Good for: Running a fridge for 20-30 hours plus all essential devices. With 400W+ of solar panels, can sustain your basics indefinitely in good weather.

Not good for: Whole-house backup or heavy loads like AC, dryers, etc.

Best for: Most homeowners. This is the sweet spot for home backup. It's what I run — the EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra at 3,072Wh keeps my family of five running through multi-day outages with solar recharging.

Extra Large: 4,000Wh+ ($2,000-4,000+)

Good for: Extended whole-home backup, multiple high-draw devices, medical equipment that can't go offline.

Best for: People with critical medical devices, large families, or areas with frequent extended outages. Also good if you want to run a transfer switch to power specific home circuits directly.

What About Surge Watts?

This trips people up. A refrigerator runs at about 100-150W on average. But when the compressor kicks on, it surges to 400-800W for a split second. If your power station can't handle that surge, it'll trip the overload protection and shut off.

This is why a 500W power station often can't run a fridge even though the average draw is well under 500W — the startup surge exceeds the station's capacity.

Rule of thumb: your power station's surge rating needs to be at least 2x the startup draw of your heaviest device. For a standard fridge, that means you want at least 1,500-2,000W surge capacity. Most power stations in the 1,000Wh+ range handle this fine.

Solar Panels: The Missing Half

I'm going to say it again because it's that important: a power station without a recharging plan is just a one-day buffer. Solar panels turn it into a sustainable system.

For home backup, here's what I recommend based on station size:

  • 1,000Wh station: 100-200W of panels. Recharges in 5-10 hours of sun.
  • 2,000Wh station: 200-400W of panels. Recharges in 5-10 hours of sun.
  • 3,000Wh station: 400-600W of panels. Recharges in 5-8 hours of sun.

Portable panels fold up and store in a closet. When the power goes out, you set them up on your deck, driveway, or yard. Point them south. Plug them in. Free electricity.

Living in an apartment? A balcony-mounted panel or a panel in a window works. It's less efficient than outdoor placement but it's something. Our Solar Power Calculator factors in apartment constraints if you tell it your living situation.

The UPS Function: Why It Matters

Better power stations have a UPS (uninterruptible power supply) mode. You plug the station into the wall, plug your fridge/router/etc. into the station, and during normal operation, power passes through from the wall. When the grid fails, the station switches to battery automatically — so fast that connected devices don't notice.

The EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra does this in under 10 milliseconds. That's fast enough for computers, medical devices, and networking equipment. If you have a CPAP that you cannot have interrupted, this feature is worth the price difference alone.

Without UPS mode, you have to physically unplug your devices from the wall and plug them into the power station during an outage. It's not hard, but it means your fridge and router go down for however long it takes you to notice the power's out and get set up. At 3 AM, that could be hours.

What About Whole-House Backup?

Some power stations can connect to your home's electrical panel through a transfer switch. EcoFlow makes a Smart Home Panel for this. It lets you select which circuits the power station feeds — fridge circuit, bedroom circuit, office circuit, whatever.

This is the most seamless setup because everything just works when the power goes out. No moving plugs around. But it requires electrician installation, the equipment isn't cheap, and you need a seriously large power station or multiple stations to handle whole circuits.

For most people, just plugging critical devices directly into the power station is simpler, cheaper, and works perfectly well. The transfer switch approach makes sense if you're in an area with frequent outages and you're tired of managing extension cords every time the lights go out.

How Long Will It Actually Last?

Real numbers from my DELTA 3 Ultra (3,072Wh) with my family of five:

  • Fridge + router + phone charging + some lights = about 28-32 hours on a full charge
  • Add laptop and TV = about 18-22 hours
  • With 440W solar generating ~1,500-2,000Wh per clear day, the fridge and basics run indefinitely
  • On cloudy days, solar generates about 500-800Wh — need to ration or supplement

On a cloudy winter day with limited solar, I get about 1.5 days of basic operation before needing another charging source. That's where a small generator or even car charging becomes the backup to the backup.

Don't Overthink It

A 2,000Wh power station with a 200W solar panel covers about 80% of home backup scenarios for about $1,000-1,500 total. It runs your fridge, keeps your phones alive, and keeps your router on so you can get weather updates and communicate.

Is that everything? No. Will you wish you had air conditioning? Probably. Will you wish you had a hot water heater? Maybe. But the stuff that actually matters — food preservation, communication, light, and medical devices — it handles all of that silently and indefinitely with solar.

That's a pretty good deal for something that fits on a shelf in your garage and requires zero maintenance until you need it.
