So iKamper dropped the Skycamp 4.0. Or announced it, anyway. As of right now you can't actually buy one yet â€” their site says April, a European retailer is showing late May, and nobody has one in hand to review. But the spec sheet is out there and I've been digging through everything I can find.

I own the Skycamp 3.0. Well, owned. Sold it with the Jeep during COVID, which I'm still a little salty about if I'm being honest. But I spent two years sleeping in that thing in the North East. So when iKamper announces a new generation I pay attention. And there's some genuinely interesting stuff happening with this one.

But there's also some stuff that feels like it got pulled straight from the DLX and repackaged. Let me break it all down.

Three Sizes Now. That's the Big Move.

The 3.0 came in one size. Four-person. They had the Mini for two people but that was basically a different product. The 4.0 does it differently â€” three models under one roof. Duo, Trio, and Quad.

The Trio is the interesting one. Three-person RTT is kind of a weird spot that nobody really serves well right now. Most couples with one kid end up in a four-person tent and swimming in extra space they're paying a premium for. Or they cram into a two-person with a toddler between them and nobody sleeps. A three-person with a tighter footprint actually makes sense for a lot of families. Three kids is a different conversation but my oldest is getting to the age where he doesn't want to sleep in a tent with mom and dad anyway.

The Duo and Trio also have a narrower footprint than the 3.0 equivalents. That matters if you're running a mid-size truck or a shorter roof rack. The 3.0 is wide. Really wide. On my Sierra it would have worked fine but I've seen guys with Tacomas where the tent hangs over the edges of the rack and it looks sketchy.

BreathWeave Fabric â€” This Actually Matters

Anyone who's slept in a hardshell RTT in cold weather knows the condensation problem. You wake up and the inside of the shell is dripping. Your sleeping bag is damp. Everything feels clammy. It's the single most common complaint about hardshell tents and iKamper's 3.0 wasn't immune to it.

The 4.0 uses what they're calling BreathWeave. It's a condensation-resistant fabric that's supposed to handle moisture without the heavy waterproof coatings that trap humidity inside the tent. I haven't touched it so I can't tell you if it actually works, but the concept is sound. Condensation happens when warm moist air (your breath, your body heat) hits a cold surface (the shell). If the fabric can breathe and manage moisture before it condenses on the shell, that's a real improvement. Not a gimmick.

For reference, the 3.0 used a PFC-free 75D ripstop rainfly. Solid fabric, good waterproofing, but condensation was still an issue on cold mornings. If BreathWeave solves even 60% of that problem it's worth the upgrade on its own.

ThermaSeal Cork Floor Is Now Standard

This one's a trickle-down from the DLX. The Skycamp DLX had a cork-topped insulated floor and people loved it. Warm to the touch, naturally antimicrobial, decent insulation. But the DLX started at like $5,100 so most people never got to experience it.

The 4.0 puts the ThermaSeal cork floor in all three models as standard. Good move. Cork doesn't feel like stepping onto a cold piece of aluminum at 5 AM, which is what the bare floor of most hardshell tents feels like. Small thing that makes a big difference in comfort.

Built-In LED Lighting and Pass-Throughs

Integrated LED interior lighting. Sounds minor until you've fumbled around in the dark trying to find a headlamp while your wife is telling you to stop stepping on things. Every RTT should have this. The fact that it took until generation 4 is more embarrassing than impressive, but better late than never.

The electrical and duct pass-throughs are more interesting to me. Being able to run a power cable or a small AC hose through a purpose-built port instead of leaving the door cracked? That's a real quality-of-life thing. I run an EcoFlow WAVE 3 for cooling and right now the hose goes through a partially zipped door. A dedicated pass-through would clean that up a lot.

Side note: I found a solution for the port on my OVS Bushveld 4 hardshell RTT, which doesn't have a dedicated hose port at the moment. I've been in contact with a guy who makes them. As soon as I get it and physically hold it to determine if it is good enough to install on my tent and give it a thumbs up I'll be writing an article on that too. So be on the lookout for that.

Optional Air Mattress System

The 3.0 comes with a 9-zone high-density foam mattress that's honestly pretty good. 2.6 inches thick, comfortable enough for a weekend. But after 4-5 nights in a row you start feeling the platform underneath. I'd probably start to feel it after 2 nights to be honest.

The 4.0 offers an optional air mattress system. I'm torn on this. Air mattresses in RTTs can be amazing for comfort but they add a failure point. You're one puncture away from sleeping on a deflated bag on top of a hard platform 5 feet off the ground. In a tent that costs $4,000+, I'd want to know that the air mattress is seriously durable before I trusted it over proven foam.

That said, the foam mattress is still standard. The air mattress is an add-on. So you're not losing anything, just gaining an option. Fair enough.

What Didn't Change (or What They Haven't Told Us)

Here's what bugs me. The shell is still a clamshell pop-up hardshell, which is fine â€” that's the Skycamp's whole identity. But iKamper hasn't published weight specs yet. The 3.0 weighs 163 lbs. If the 4.0 Quad is heavier than that because of the cork floor, LED system, and pass-through hardware, that matters. An extra 15-20 lbs changes your payload math, especially on a mid-size truck that's already tight on GVWR.

No pricing yet either. The 3.0 runs $3,895 direct, more at retailers. The DLX is $5,100+. My guess is the 4.0 Quad lands somewhere around $4,200-$4,800 based on the feature set being a middle ground between the 3.0 and DLX. But that's me speculating, not iKamper announcing.

And honestly? I want to know about the ladder. The 3.0's ladder is fine but it's not great in tight camp spaces. A telescoping or side-mount option would've been a welcome change. Haven't seen anything about that yet.

Should You Wait for the 4.0 or Buy the 3.0 Now?

Depends on your timeline.

If you're building out a rig right now and plan to camp this spring, buy the 3.0. It's a proven tent. Two generations of refinement. Known weight, known fit, known quality. You can find deals on 3.0s right now because dealers know the 4.0 is coming and they need to move inventory. I've seen them under $3,500 at some retailers.

If you're not in a rush and you specifically care about the condensation issue or you want the Trio size, wait. The BreathWeave fabric alone could be worth it for anyone who camps in cold or humid conditions regularly. And the Trio fills a gap that nobody else is filling right now.
