Your Bug Out Bag Food Is Probably Wrong

I'm just going to be straight with you. If your bug out bag food plan is "I'll figure it out when I need it" or "I threw some granola bars in there two years ago," you're setting yourself up to fail. And if you've got three kids like I do, you're setting your family up to fail.

Bug out bag food has exactly three jobs: be calorie dense, be lightweight, and be something people will actually eat under stress. That last one is way more important than most preppers think.

I've spent a lot of time dialing in what goes in my bag and what gets cut. Some of it came from mutual aid trips where I watched people try to choke down stuff they'd never eaten before while exhausted and dehydrated. Some came from trial runs at the cabin in PA where my kids flat out refused things I thought were fine.

Here's what I know works.

The Calorie Math Nobody Wants to Do

When you're humping a pack on foot, which is what a bug out scenario might look like and you're burning 3,000 to 4,000 calories on a normal day, more if it's cold and even more if you're carrying a heavy bag. Kids burn less but they also can't carry as much, so there's a good chance you're probably hauling their food too.

For a 72-hour bag (which is the minimum, honestly you should plan for longer, but that's a different article about 14-day kits), you need roughly 9,000 to 12,000 calories per person. That sounds like a lot until you realize a single jar of peanut butter has about 2,600 calories and suddenly the math isn't that scary.

The goal here is calories per ounce. You want the most energy for the least weight, because every ounce in your bag is an ounce on your back, and after mile three, you feel every single one.

You want to test that theory out? Take a glass and fill it with water. It's just a measly little glass of water, right? Hold it, walk around with it, but don't put it down. Everything seems fine at first, no sweat and you think you can hold that glass of water all day. Over time you will start to realize your arm is getting a little tense. After more time it's going to start to bother you holding it. Carry it around for a few hours and you're going to be ready to toss that glass of water because it's become so overwhelming for you to hold. Now apply that theory to your pack.

The A-List: What Actually Goes in My Bag

Peanut Butter (or Almond Butter)

This is the king of bug out food. 190 calories per ounce. Protein, fat, doesn't need cooking, and doesn't need refrigeration. Kids eat it without complaining too. I carry the individual squeeze packets because jars are heavy and messy, but honestly a 12 oz jar works fine too.

Justin's makes individual almond butter packets that are around 200 calories each and weigh nothing. Throw ten of those in your bag and you've got 2,000 calories that take up less space than a paperback book.

Trail Mix and Nuts

These are calorie dense and are shelf stable for months. They give you the right mix of fats, protein, and carbs. I'm partial to mixes heavy on cashews and almonds and light on the raisins (they're mostly sugar and don't give you much sustained energy). M&Ms in trail mix aren't just for fun they're fast sugar when you're bonking on a trail. Chocolate melts though, so think about your climate for that time of year. One of my favorite mixes is from Target called “Monster Trail Mix”. It's got the perfect balance of sweet, salty, crunchy, it just tastes really good and it has roughly 180 calories per ¼ cup.

Energy/Protein Bars

CLIF Builders bars - 280 calories, 20g protein, and they actually taste pretty decent. My oldest will eat these voluntarily, which is the real test. They last about a year before the texture gets weird, so rotate them frequently.

ProBars, RXBARs, whatever, you pick what you like and will actually eat. Don't buy something you've never tried just because a YouTube prepper recommended it.

Freeze-Dried Meals

If you have a way to boil water and you should, Mountain House meals are the gold standard. The 3 Day emergency supply gives you 18 servings at roughly 1,700 calories per day paired with a 30 year shelf life. Just add boiling water, wait 10 minutes and enjoy.

The biscuits and gravy is legitimately pretty darn good. The beef stroganoff is fine. The chicken fried rice is... edible. But here's the real deal, they all taste like a hot meal when you've been hiking for 8 hours in the rain, and that psychological boost is worth its weight in gold, especially when you can get a hot meal fairly quickly.

Downsides: they require water (about 2 cups per pouch) and heat. If you're in a scenario where you can't make fire or run a stove, these are dead weight.

Tortillas

This is my sleeper pick. Mission flour tortillas last for weeks at room temperature, they're flat so they pack perfectly, and they turn peanut butter or tuna packets into an actual meal. About 140 calories each and I pack 6-8 of them in a ziplock baggy.

Like everything, they do eventually mold so rotate them out.

Emergency Calorie Bars

Datrex 3600 calorie bars are Coast Guard approved and designed precisely for this situation. A one bar package is 3,600 calories, which is a full day's energy. They don't make you thirsty (which matters when water is limited), they're shelf stable for 5 years, and they taste like a slightly bland shortbread cookie.

Not exciting food, but I do like them and they're reliable. I keep one in each of our bags as the "if everything else fails" option.

Tuna/Chicken Packets

The foil pouch kind, not cans. StarKist and Bumble Bee both make them. I like the Wild Skipjack one's from Trader Joe's. About 100 calories and 16g protein per pouch. They weigh a fraction of a can and you don't need a can opener. Pair with a tortilla and you've got a quick protein packed meal to keep you sustained.

Honey Packets

Honey packets sound weird, but honey is pure fast energy, it literally never expires, and individual packets are tiny. When your blood sugar crashes at mile six, a honey packet gets you moving again in minutes. I grab a handful from Wawa whenever I'm there. Free calories. I actually like to use honey to sweeten my coffee.

The B-List: Solid But Situational

Instant Oatmeal

This is good if you have hot water. It provides decent calories and the kids like the flavored packets. The issue is they take up space and requires cooking, which bumps it down from the A-list.

Jerky

One of my absolute favorites. It's packed with protein, tastes good, and it's lightweight. But it's expensive per calorie, it makes you thirsty (not great when water is a concern), and the salt content is up there. I carry some because I like it, not because it's optimal.

Dried Fruit

Fast sugar, some vitamins and shelf stable. Mango slices, banana chips, or raisins. They're not calorie dense enough to be your main food, but a good supplement.

Instant Coffee

Not food, technically. But if you're a coffee person and you're bugging out, caffeine withdrawal headaches on top of everything else is miserable. Starbucks VIA packets weigh nothing. Throw three in the bag and for me, there's nothing like a cup of coffee to start the day.

The Skip List: What NOT to Pack

Canned food. It's way too heavy. A 15 oz can of chili is like 350 calories but weighs almost a pound. That's terrible math for a carry bag. Save the canned stuff for your home stockpile and check our breakdown of best canned food for survival for that.

MREs. Military MREs are awesome but they weigh about 1.5 lbs each, which is 4.5 lbs for a day. For a car kit or a vehicle bug out bag? Yeah, this will work. For a bag you're carrying on foot? Skip it or only pack components, not full meals.

Raw rice and beans. I know, I know, "but the calories per dollar!" Yeah, and the cooking time is 20-45 minutes with a pot, fuel, and water. This isn't a pantry, it's a go bag and you have to be able to move quick, rest and get a meal quick and be back on the move.

Don't pack anything that you have not personally tried yourself or your family. That weird survival bar your buddy recommended? Eat one first. If you don't like it at home on a normal day, you won't like it when you're stressed, tired, and your adrenaline is crashing.

Packing It: A Real 72-Hour Food Kit

Here's what I actually have in my bag right now for myself (Bug Out Bag Calculator helps with this and you can adjust it for your family needs):

  • 1x Datrex 3600 bar (~18 oz, 3,600 cal) emergency backup

  • 1 Small bag of beef jerky (2.2 oz 70 cal)

  • 6x CLIF Builders bars (~14 oz, 1,680 cal)

  • 8x peanut butter packets (~8 oz, 1,600 cal)

  • 2x Mountain House pouches (~10 oz, 1,200 cal)

  • 4x tortillas (~6 oz, 560 cal)

  • 2x tuna packets (~5 oz, 200 cal)

  • 1x bag trail mix (~8 oz, 1,300 cal)

  • 3x Starbucks VIA packets (~negligible weight)

  • Assorted honey packets

Total weight: roughly 4.7 lbs. Total calories: ~10,000+. That's over 3,300 calories per day for three days, which is plenty for a moderately active bug out on foot. And nothing in there requires cooking except the Mountain House pouches, which are nice-to-have, not need-to-have.

Rotation - The Part Everyone Forgets

Bug out bag food isn't a "set it and forget it" thing. Every six months I pull everything out and eat what's close to expiring (family trail snack day), and replace it fresh. Takes 10 minutes and costs maybe $25.

The Datrex bars and Mountain House meals can stay for years. Everything else should rotate twice a year. You can set a reminder in your phone. I use the daylight saving time as my switch trigger so when the clocks change, I check the bags.

What About the Kids?

I'm going to be real, getting three kids to eat survival food is harder than the actual survival part sometimes. So I cheat. I pack stuff they already eat at home. Goldfish crackers (yes, seriously), fruit snacks, those little apple sauce pouches. Are they optimal for calorie density? No. Will my nine year old actually eat them without a meltdown? Yup.

A hungry kid who refuses to eat is worse than a slightly heavier bag, so pack some comfort food. It weighs a few ounces and buys you hours of peace and you get to keep your sanity.

Final Thought

The best bug out bag food is food you'll eat, food that doesn't weigh a ton, and food that's actually in your bag, not food you're planning to buy "someday." Go put this together today. It'll cost you less than a dinner out and it'll sit there quietly until you need it and you have something to look forward to in a few months.

Now forget about it and go practice something that matters, like actually knowing where you'd go if you had to leave like right now.
