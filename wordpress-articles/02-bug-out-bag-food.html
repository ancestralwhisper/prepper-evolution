Your Bug Out Bag Food Is Probably Wrong

I'm just going to be blunt. If your bug out bag food plan is "I'll figure it out when I need it" or "I threw some granola bars in there two years ago," you're setting yourself up to fail. And if you've got kids — like I do, three of them — you're setting your family up to fail.

Bug out bag food has exactly three jobs: be calorie-dense, be lightweight, and be something people will actually eat under stress. That last one? Way more important than most preppers think.

I've spent a lot of time dialing in what goes in my bag and what gets cut. Some of it came from mutual aid deployments where I watched people try to choke down stuff they'd never eaten before while exhausted and dehydrated. Some came from trial runs at the cabin in PA where my kids flat-out refused things I thought were fine.

Here's what I know works.

The Calorie Math Nobody Wants to Do

When you're humping a pack on foot — which is what a bug out scenario might look like — you're burning 3,000 to 4,000 calories a day. More if it's cold. More if you're carrying a heavy bag. Kids burn less but they also can't carry as much, so you're probably hauling their food too.

For a 72-hour bag (which is the minimum — honestly you should plan for longer, but that's a different article about 14-day kits), you need roughly 9,000 to 12,000 calories per person. That sounds like a lot until you realize a single jar of peanut butter has about 2,600 calories. Suddenly the math isn't that scary.

The goal is calories per ounce. You want the most energy for the least weight. Because every ounce in your bag is an ounce on your back, and after mile three, you feel every single one.

The A-List: What Actually Goes in My Bag

Peanut Butter (or Almond Butter)

King of bug out food. 190 calories per ounce. Protein, fat, doesn't need cooking, doesn't need refrigeration. Kids eat it without complaining. I carry the individual squeeze packets because jars are heavy and messy, but honestly a 12-oz jar works fine too.

Justin's makes individual almond butter packets that are around 200 calories each and weigh nothing. Throw ten of those in your bag and you've got 2,000 calories that take up less space than a paperback.

Trail Mix and Nuts

Calorie-dense. Shelf-stable for months. Mix of fats, protein, and carbs. I'm partial to mixes heavy on cashews and almonds, light on the raisins (they're mostly sugar and don't give you much sustained energy). M&Ms in trail mix aren't just for fun — they're fast sugar when you're bonking on a trail. Chocolate melts though, so think about your climate.

Roughly 160-175 calories per ounce depending on the mix.

Energy/Protein Bars

CLIF Builders bars — 280 calories, 20g protein, and they actually taste good. My oldest will eat these voluntarily, which is the real test. They last about a year before the texture gets weird, so rotate them.

ProBars, RXBARs, whatever — pick what you like and will actually eat. Don't buy something you've never tried just because a YouTube prepper recommended it.

Freeze-Dried Meals

If you have a way to boil water — and you should — Mountain House meals are the gold standard. The 3-Day Emergency Supply gives you 18 servings at roughly 1,700 calories per day. 30-year shelf life. Just add boiling water, wait 10 minutes.

The biscuits and gravy is legitimately good. The beef stroganoff is fine. The chicken fried rice is... edible. But here's the thing — they all taste like a hot meal when you've been hiking for 8 hours in the rain, and that psychological boost is worth its weight in gold.

Downsides: they require water (about 2 cups per pouch) and heat. If you're in a scenario where you can't make fire or run a stove, these are dead weight.

Tortillas

This is my sleeper pick. Mission flour tortillas last weeks at room temperature, they're flat so they pack perfectly, and they turn peanut butter or tuna packets into an actual meal. About 140 calories each. Pack 6-8 of them in a ziplock.

They do eventually mold, so rotate every couple months.

Emergency Calorie Bars

Datrex 3600 calorie bars are Coast Guard approved and designed for exactly this situation. One bar package is 3,600 calories — that's a full day's energy. They don't make you thirsty (which matters when water is limited), they're shelf stable for 5 years, and they taste like a slightly bland shortbread cookie.

Not exciting food. But reliable food. I keep one in each of our bags as the "if everything else fails" option.

Tuna/Chicken Packets

The foil pouch kind, not cans. StarKist and Bumble Bee both make them. About 100 calories and 16g protein per pouch. They weigh a fraction of a can and you don't need a can opener. Pair with a tortilla and you've got a meal.

Honey Packets

Sounds weird. But honey is pure fast energy, it literally never expires, and individual packets are tiny. When your blood sugar crashes at mile six, a honey packet gets you moving again in minutes. I grab a handful from Chick-fil-A whenever I'm there. Free calories.

The B-List: Solid But Situational

Instant Oatmeal

Good if you have hot water. Decent calories. Kids like the flavored packets. But it takes up space and requires cooking, which bumps it down from the A-list.

Jerky

High protein, tastes good, lightweight. But it's expensive per calorie, it makes you thirsty (not great when water is a concern), and the salt content is pretty wild. I carry some because I like it, not because it's optimal.

Dried Fruit

Fast sugar, some vitamins, shelf stable. Mango slices, banana chips, raisins. Not calorie-dense enough to be your main food, but a good supplement.

Instant Coffee

Not food, technically. But if you're a coffee person and you're bugging out, caffeine withdrawal headaches on top of everything else is miserable. Starbucks VIA packets weigh nothing. Throw three in the bag.

The Skip List: What NOT to Pack

Canned food. Too heavy. Period. A 15 oz can of chili is like 350 calories but weighs almost a pound. That's terrible math for a carry bag. Save the canned stuff for your home stockpile — and check our breakdown of best canned food for survival for that.

MREs. Military MREs are awesome but they weigh about 1.5 lbs each. That's 4.5 lbs for a day. For a car kit or a vehicle bug out bag? Sure. For a bag you're carrying on foot? Skip it or only pack components, not full meals.

Raw rice and beans. I know, I know — "but the calories per dollar!" Yeah, and the cooking time is 20-45 minutes with a pot, fuel, and water. This isn't a pantry, it's a go bag.

Anything you haven't tried. Seriously. That weird survival bar your buddy recommended? Eat one first. If you don't like it at home on a normal day, you won't like it when you're stressed, tired, and your adrenaline is crashing.

Packing It: A Real 72-Hour Food Kit

Here's what I actually have in my bag right now for myself (adjust for family — the Bug Out Bag Calculator helps with this):

  • 1x Datrex 3600 bar (~18 oz, 3,600 cal) — emergency backup
  • 6x CLIF Builders bars (~14 oz, 1,680 cal)
  • 8x peanut butter packets (~8 oz, 1,600 cal)
  • 2x Mountain House pouches (~10 oz, 1,200 cal)
  • 4x tortillas (~6 oz, 560 cal)
  • 2x tuna packets (~5 oz, 200 cal)
  • 1x bag trail mix (~8 oz, 1,300 cal)
  • 3x Starbucks VIA packets (~negligible weight)
  • Assorted honey packets

Total weight: roughly 4.5 lbs. Total calories: ~10,000+. That's over 3,300 calories per day for three days, which is plenty for a moderately active bug out on foot. And nothing in there requires cooking except the Mountain House pouches, which are nice-to-have, not need-to-have.

Rotation — The Part Everyone Forgets

Bug out bag food isn't a "set it and forget it" thing. Every six months, I pull everything out, eat what's close to expiring (family trail snack day), and replace it fresh. Takes 30 minutes and costs maybe $25.

The Datrex bars and Mountain House meals can stay for years. Everything else should rotate twice a year. Put a reminder in your phone. I use the daylight saving time switch as my trigger — when the clocks change, I check the bags.

What About the Kids?

I'm going to be honest — getting three kids to eat survival food is harder than the actual survival part sometimes. So I cheat. I pack stuff they already eat at home. Goldfish crackers (yes, seriously), fruit snacks, those little apple sauce pouches. Are they optimal for calorie density? No. Will my seven-year-old actually eat them without a meltdown? Yes.

A hungry kid who refuses to eat is worse than a slightly heavier bag. Pack some comfort food. It weighs a few ounces and buys you hours of peace.

Final Thought

The best bug out bag food is food you'll eat, food that doesn't weigh a ton, and food that's actually in your bag — not food you're planning to buy "someday." Go put this together today. It'll cost you less than a dinner out and it'll sit there quietly until you need it.

Then forget about it and go practice something that matters, like actually knowing where you'd go if you had to leave.
