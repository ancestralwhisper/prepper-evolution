Overlanding Isn't What Instagram Thinks It Is

If you scroll through overlanding content on social media, you'd think it requires a $90,000 truck, $15,000 in mods, and a full-time travel lifestyle. That's not overlanding. That's a marketing campaign.

Overlanding is vehicle-based, self-reliant travel where the journey is the point. That's it. You're using your vehicle to get to remote places, you're carrying everything you need to be self-sufficient, and the destination is secondary to the experience of getting there.

It's not off-roading (though off-road driving can be part of it). Off-roading is about the driving challenge — rock crawling, mud bogging, technical trails. Overlanding is about the travel. Going somewhere, camping, exploring, moving on. Think less "conquer the obstacle" and more "find the most remote campsite possible and cook dinner under stars nobody else can see."

I've been doing some version of this for years — before I even knew the word "overlanding." Started with a minivan and a tent, honestly. My actual overlanding journey went from there to a Jeep Rubicon with an iKamper rooftop tent, then a Honda Accord (don't ask — COVID was weird), then a 2023 Canyon that had problems so I sold it, and now I'm building out a 2025 GMC Sierra 1500. Every vehicle taught me something different about what matters and what doesn't.

Overlanding vs Camping vs Off-Roading

People mix these up constantly and it drives me nuts.

Car camping: You drive your car to a developed campground. There's a fire ring, probably a bathroom, maybe neighbors 20 feet away blasting country music from a Bluetooth speaker. Nothing wrong with it — I did it for years — but it's not overlanding.

Off-roading: The vehicle IS the activity. You're trying to get through tough terrain — rocks, mud, sand, water crossings. It's a sport. Some overlanders off-road as part of their trips, but plenty of overlanding happens on gravel forest service roads that a Subaru could handle.

Overlanding: The travel IS the activity. You're moving through terrain — maybe paved, maybe not — with everything you need onboard. You camp where you end up. You stay as long as you want. You move on when you're ready. There's a self-reliance element that separates it from just driving around — you're carrying your shelter, food, water, and tools.

I read an article that described it as "the intersection of off-roading, camping, and road-tripping" and honestly that's pretty good. Take those three things, blend them together, add self-sufficiency, and that's overlanding.

You Don't Need an Expensive Truck

This is the biggest barrier I see keeping people out. They think they need to drop fifty grand on a vehicle before they can start. Nonsense.

Some of the best overlanding vehicles are used Toyota 4Runners, Jeep Cherokees (the old XJs), and even stock pickup trucks. A buddy of mine overlands in a 2010 Toyota Tacoma with 180,000 miles on it. He's been from Utah to Baja and back. Multiple times. Stock suspension. All-terrain tires. A rooftop tent. That's it.

Here's what your vehicle actually needs:

  • Reliable: This is number one. You're going to remote places. A breakdown on a forest road in Nevada is very different from a breakdown on the highway. Your vehicle needs to be mechanically solid.
  • Ground clearance: Enough to handle rough gravel roads without scraping. Most trucks and SUVs are fine stock. You don't need a 6-inch lift.
  • 4WD or AWD: Strongly recommended. Not strictly required for easy trails and forest roads, but once you want to go to the good spots, you'll want low range.
  • Cargo space: You're carrying shelter, food, water, cooking gear, and tools. You need room for it.
  • Good tires: This is the single best mod you can make. A set of quality all-terrain tires (BFG KO2, Falken Wildpeak, Toyo Open Country) transforms what a vehicle can handle.

My Sierra is a full-size 1500 with 4WD. It's not a hardcore rock crawler, but it goes everywhere I need it to. Forest service roads, fire roads, two-track trails in Utah and Nevada, mountain roads in PA and West Virginia. Most overlanding terrain isn't technical — it's just remote.

The Gear That Actually Matters

Here's where people go wrong. They start buying gear before they've even gone on a trip. Then they end up with $5,000 of stuff they've never used and no idea what they actually need.

Start with the bare minimum. Go on trips. Figure out what you're missing. Buy that. Repeat.

Sleep System

You need somewhere to sleep. Options from cheapest to most expensive:

  • Ground tent: $50-200. A regular camping tent works fine. Throw it in the truck bed or set it up next to the vehicle. Nothing wrong with this — I started here.
  • Truck bed setup: $100-500. A truck bed platform with a mattress pad or sleeping bag. Add a truck cap or a tent that goes over the bed (Napier Backroadz makes decent ones).
  • Rooftop tent (RTT): $800-3,000+. This is where I am now. I run an OVS HD Bushveld RTT. It pops up in 60 seconds, gets you off the ground, and is genuinely comfortable. But it adds weight up high (which affects vehicle dynamics — the Vehicle Profile tool shows you exactly how) and it costs real money.

My advice? If you have a truck, start with a ground tent or a truck bed setup. If you love overlanding after 5-10 trips, then invest in an RTT. Don't blow $2,000 on a rooftop tent before you've spent a single night on a forest road.

Kitchen Setup

Cooking outdoors is half the fun. Minimum viable kitchen:

  • A camp stove (propane single-burner or the classic Coleman 2-burner)
  • A pot, a pan, and a spatula
  • Paper plates and plastic utensils (or real ones if you're fancy)
  • A cooler with ice (or a 12V fridge if you've got the budget and the power — but that's a future upgrade)
  • A water container — 5-7 gallon jerry can or Reliance Aqua-Tainer
  • A table — even a cheap folding table from Walmart changes the cooking experience

I've cooked full steak dinners at 9,000 feet in Utah on a $40 camp stove. You don't need a $400 slide-out kitchen for your first year. Or second. Or ever, honestly — some people love fancy setups and that's cool, but a propane stove and a cutting board gets the same food in your belly.

Power

You need to charge your phone, run lights, maybe power a fan or a fridge. My setup is the EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra with two 220W solar panels, and it's wildly overkill for most people starting out. But it keeps everything running indefinitely, which matters when you're out for 4-5 days.

Starting out, all you really need is a portable power bank for your phone and maybe some battery-powered LED lanterns. As you get deeper into it, a mid-size power station (300-500Wh) with a small solar panel covers everything for weekend trips.

Recovery Gear

If you go off pavement, eventually you'll get stuck. It's not if, it's when. Basic recovery kit:

  • A kinetic recovery rope (not a tow strap — kinetic ropes stretch and provide gentle pulls that won't snap your frame)
  • A set of soft shackles
  • A shovel (folding or full-size)
  • Traction boards (MaxTrax or generic — they work on sand, mud, and snow)
  • A tire plug kit
  • A portable air compressor (airing down for trails and airing back up for highway)

I've used every one of these items. Some of them multiple times in a single trip. The traction boards have saved me in soft sand in Nevada and mud in West Virginia. The tire plug kit has saved me twice. This gear is not optional if you're going off pavement.

Where to Go (Starting Out)

National Forests are your best friend. They have thousands of miles of forest service roads that are open to vehicles, and dispersed camping is free on most national forest land. You just drive until you find a spot, pull off, and camp. No reservations, no fees, no neighbors.

BLM (Bureau of Land Management) land works the same way. Mostly in the western states — Utah, Nevada, Arizona, Oregon. Massive expanses of public land where you can camp for up to 14 days for free.

For me, being based in NJ, the closest good overlanding is in Pennsylvania. The Pocono Mountains and surrounding areas have decent forest roads. Then when I can get away for longer trips, Utah and Nevada are the promised land. Moab, the Paiute Trail system, Nevada's Basin and Range — incredible terrain with virtually nobody around.

Some resources for finding trails and camp spots:

  • onX Offroad app — shows land ownership, trails, and overlays. Best $30/year you'll spend.
  • iOverlander app — community-sourced camp spots with reviews
  • Gaia GPS — detailed topo maps with trail overlays
  • Forest Service motor vehicle use maps (MVUMs) — free downloads from the Forest Service website. These show exactly which roads are open to vehicles.

The Mindset: Self-Reliance Is the Point

Here's where overlanding connects to preparedness in a way that car camping doesn't.

When you're overland, you carry everything you need. Water, food, fuel, shelter, tools, first aid. If something breaks, you fix it or work around it. If weather changes, you adapt. If you can't find a campsite, you make one. There's no running to the camp store. There's no calling AAA from a national forest road in the middle of Nevada.

This self-reliance muscle is the same one you use in emergency preparedness. Every overlanding trip teaches you something — how much water you actually use, how to manage power, how to fix things with limited tools, how to stay comfortable in uncomfortable conditions. It's practical skills building disguised as a vacation.

My kids have learned more about problem-solving, outdoor skills, and self-sufficiency on overlanding trips than from any book or class. And they think we're just camping. Which we are. Sort of.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Buying too much gear before your first trip. Go with what you have. A tent, a sleeping bag, a cooler, a stove. That's enough. Figure out what you need from experience, not from YouTube recommendations.

Going too hard too fast. Don't pick a hardcore 4x4 trail for your first trip. Find a well-maintained forest service road with some dispersed camping. Learn your vehicle's limits on easy terrain before you push them on hard terrain.

Not telling anyone where you're going. Leave a trip plan with someone who's not going with you. Where you're headed, what route, when you expect to be back. Cell service is spotty to nonexistent in overlanding territory.

Ignoring the weight. Overlanding gear adds up fast. RTT, water, fuel, food, tools, recovery gear — you can easily add 500-800 lbs to your vehicle. Know your payload capacity. Know what the extra weight does to your handling, braking, and fuel economy. The Vehicle Profile tool shows you exactly how different loads affect your specific vehicle.

Forgetting to air down. On gravel, sand, and rough terrain, airing your tires down to 18-25 PSI (from your normal 35-40) dramatically improves traction and ride comfort. Just remember to air back up before you hit pavement. Running aired-down tires at highway speed is a great way to blow a sidewall.

Just Start

The best overlanding rig is the one you already have. The best overlanding trip is the first one you take. Everything else — the upgrades, the gear, the fancy builds — comes later. Or doesn't. Some people overland for years in a stock vehicle with a ground tent and have a better time than the guy in the $150,000 build who's afraid to scratch it.

Pack the truck. Fill the cooler. Grab a map. Pick a forest road. Drive until you find a spot. Camp. Wake up. Keep going or go home.

That's overlanding. Everything else is just accessories.
