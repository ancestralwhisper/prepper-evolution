Overlanding Isn't What Instagram Thinks It Is

If you scroll through overlanding content on social media, you'd think it requires a $90,000 truck, $15,000 in mods, and a full-time travel lifestyle. That's not overlanding. That's just a marketing campaign.

Overlanding is all vehicle based and self reliant travel where the journey is the point. That's it. You're using your vehicle to get to places whether it be remote or not. You're carrying everything you need to be self-sufficient, and the destination is secondary to the experience of getting there.

It's not off roading (but off road driving can be part of it). Off roading is about the driving challenge like rock crawling, mud bogging, technical trails. Overlanding is about the travel and getting out and going somewhere, camping, exploring, moving on to the next location. Think of it less like "conquer the obstacle" and more like "find the most remote campsite possible and cook dinner under stars nobody else can see." Everybody in the city is drowned out with light pollution, hindering the real view of mother nature.

I've been doing some variation of this for years before I even knew the word "overlanding." I started with a minivan and a tent, honestly. My actual overlanding journey went from there to a 2009 Jeep Rubicon with an iKamper rooftop tent, then a Honda Accord (don't ask, COVID was weird), then a 2023 Canyon that gave me so many problems so I traded it in, and now I'm building out a 2025 GMC Sierra 1500. Every vehicle taught me something different about what matters and what doesn't. I actually started building out my Canyon to go overlanding and learned a lesson which couldn't be taught anywhere. Mid size truck fitment of parts and accessories are totally different on a full size truck. I thought I was going to be able to transfer all of the stuff I bought from my mid size to the full size. Whelp, I was wrong, so I wound up selling it for way less than I paid for it. That hurt.

Overlanding vs Camping vs Off-Roading

People mix these up constantly and it drives me nuts.

Car camping: You jump in and drive your car to a developed campground. There's a fire ring, probably a bathroom, maybe neighbors 20 feet away blasting country music from a bluetooth speaker. Nothing wrong with it and I did it for years, but it's not overlanding. It's just camping.

Off-roading: The vehicle IS the activity. You're trying to get through tough terrain like rocks, mud, sand, water crossings. It's a sport and I often get out to do this on UTV's. Some overlanders off road as part of their trips, but plenty of overlanding happens on gravel forest service roads that a Subaru could handle.

Overlanding: The is where the travel IS the activity. You're moving through terrain that may be paved, maybe not with everything you need onboard. You camp where you end up and stay as long as you want. You move on when you're ready to go. There's a self reliance element that separates it from just driving around, because you're carrying your shelter, food, water, and tools. Literally you have on you everything you need to survive and thrive.

I read an article that described it as "the intersection of off roading, camping, and road-tripping" and honestly that's pretty good. Take those three things, blend them together, add self sufficiency, and that's overlanding.

You Don't Need an Expensive Truck

This is the biggest barrier I see keeping people from just doing it and getting out. They think they need to drop fifty grand on a vehicle before they can start. This is the furthest thing from the truth.

Some of the best overlanding vehicles are used Toyota 4Runners, Jeep Cherokees (the old XJs), and even stock pickup trucks. A buddy of mine overlands in a 2010 Toyota Tacoma with 180,000 miles on it. He's been from Utah to Baja and back multiple times. Everhthing on his truck is stock too. All-terrain tires. A rooftop tent. That's it.

Here's what your vehicle actually needs:

  • Reliability: This is number one. You're going to remote places where you may not see another person. A breakdown on a forest road in Nevada is very different from a breakdown on the highway. Your vehicle needs to be mechanically solid.

  • Ground clearance: Enough to handle rough gravel roads without scraping. Most trucks and SUVs are fine stock. You don't need a 6-inch lift.

  • 4WD or AWD: Strongly recommended. Not strictly required for easy trails and forest roads, but once you want to go to the good spots, you'll want low range.

  • Cargo space: You're carrying shelter, food, water, cooking gear, and tools. You need room for all of these things.

  • Good tires: This is the single best mod you can make. A set of quality all-terrain tires (BFG KO2, Falken Wildpeak, Toyo Open Country) transforms what a vehicle can handle.

My Sierra is a full-size 1500 with 4WD. It's not a hardcore rock crawler, but it goes everywhere I need it to, plus it's my daily commuter back and forth to work. Forest service roads, fire roads, two track trails in Utah and Nevada, mountain roads in PA and West Virginia. Most overlanding terrain isn't technical, it's just remote. I like remote.

The Gear That Actually Matters

Here's where people can spend money that's not necessary. They start buying gear before they've even gone on a trip. Then they end up with $5,000 of stuff they've never used and no idea what they actually need.

Start with the bare minimum. Get out and go on trips. Figure out what you're missing, then buy what you need as you go. Repeat. As you get out more you're inventory of items will grow. You may even come across somebody else that didn't pack something that you might have or vice versa.

Sleep System

Sleep is extremely important and you need somewhere to sleep. Options from cheapest to most expensive:

  • Ground tent: $50-200. A regular camping tent works perfectly fine. Throw it in the truck bed or set it up next to the vehicle. Nothing wrong with this at all, I started at this point.

  • Truck bed setup: $100-500. A truck bed platform with a mattress pad or sleeping bag. Add a truck cap or a tent that goes over the bed (Napier Backroadz makes decent ones).

  • Rooftop tent (RTT): $800-3,000+. This is where I am now. I run an OVS HD Bushveld RTT. It pops up in 60 seconds, gets you off the ground from critters, and is genuinely comfortable. But it adds weight up high (which affects vehicle dynamics, so I created the Vehicle Profile tool which shows you exactly how) and it costs real money to get an RTT.

My advice to you? If you have a truck or a car, start with a ground tent or a truck bed setup. If you love overlanding after 5-10 trips, then invest in an RTT. Don't blow two grand on a rooftop tent before you've spent a single night on a forest road.

Kitchen Setup

Cooking outdoors is half the fun. Minimum viable kitchen:

  • A camp stove (propane single-burner or the classic Coleman 2-burner)

  • A pot, a pan, and a spatula

  • Paper plates and plastic utensils (or real ones if you're fancy). I use titanium utensils so I can wash and reuse them.

  • A cooler with ice (or a 12V fridge if you've got the budget and the power, but that's a future upgrade)

  • A water container. A 5-7 gallon jerry can or Reliance Aqua-Tainer

  • Some type of table. Even a cheap folding table from Walmart changes the cooking experience

I've cooked full steak dinners in Utah on a $40 camp stove. You don't need a $400 slide out kitchen for your first year, or second, or ever. Honestly, some people love fancy setups and that's all well and good, but a propane stove and a cutting board gets the same food in your belly.

Power

You need to charge your phone, run lights, and maybe power a fan or a fridge. My setup is the EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra with three 220W solar panels, and it's wildly overkill for most people starting out. But it keeps everything running indefinitely, which matters when you're out for 4-5 days.

Starting out, all you really need is a portable power bank for your phone and maybe some battery powered LED lanterns. As you get deeper more experience, a mid-size power station (300-500Wh) with a small solar panel covers everything for weekend trips.

Recovery Gear

If you go off pavement, eventually you'll get stuck. It's not a matter of if, it's only a matter of when. Basic recovery kit:

  • A kinetic recovery rope (not a tow strap. Kinetic ropes stretch and provide gentle pulls that won't snap your frame)

  • A set of soft shackles

  • A shovel (folding or full-size)

  • Traction boards (MaxTrax or generic brands. They work on sand, mud, and snow)

  • A tire plug kit

  • A portable air compressor (airing down for trails and airing back up for the highway)

I've used every one of these items. Some of them multiple times in a single trip. The traction boards have saved me in soft sand in Nevada and mud in West Virginia. The tire plug kit has saved me twice. This gear is not optional if you're going off the beaten path.

Where to Go (Starting Out)

National Forests are your best friend here. They have thousands of miles of forest service roads that are open to vehicles and dispersed camping is free on most national forest land. You just drive until you find a spot, pull off, and set up camp. No reservations, no fees, no neighbors. Just you and mother nature.

BLM (Bureau of Land Management) land works the same way. Mostly in the western states like Utah, Nevada, Arizona, and Oregon. Massive expanses of public land where you can camp for up to 14 days for free.

For me, being based in NJ, the closest good overlanding is in Pennsylvania. The Pocono Mountains and surrounding areas have decent forest roads. Then when I can get away for longer trips, Utah and Nevada are the promised land. Moab, the Paiute Trail system, Nevada's Basin and Range have incredible terrain with virtually nobody around.

Some resources for finding trails and camp spots:

  • onX Offroad app - shows land ownership, trails, and overlays. Best $30/year you'll spend.

  • iOverlander app – community sourced camp spots with reviews

  • Gaia GPS - detailed topo maps with trail overlays

  • Forest Service motor vehicle use maps (MVUMs) - free downloads from the Forest Service website. These show exactly which roads are open to vehicles. Check before you go and plan in advance.

The Mindset: Self-Reliance Is the Point

Here's where overlanding connects to preparedness in a way that car camping simply does not.

When you're overland, you carry everything you need. Water, food, fuel, shelter, tools, and first aid. If something breaks, you fix it or work around it. If weather changes, you adapt. If you can't find a campsite, you make one. There's no running out to the camp store. There's no calling AAA from a national forest road in the middle of Nevada. It's just you out there and perhaps some people you may run across that have the same mindset you do.

This self-reliance muscle is the same one you use in emergency preparedness. Every overlanding trip teaches you something different. How much water you actually use, how to manage power, how to fix things with limited tools, how to stay comfortable in uncomfortable conditions. It's practical skills building disguised as a vacation. Sometimes it involved actual work, where other times things go incredibly smooth. I try to look at it like this... are you going to remember 25 good trips or are you going to remember that trip where you got stuck in the mud and had to figure out a way to get out?

My kids have learned more about problem solving, outdoor skills, and self-sufficiency on overlanding trips than from any book or class. And they think we're just camping, which we kind of are.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Buying too much gear before your first trip. Go with what you have. A tent, a sleeping bag, a cooler, a stove. That's plenty. Figure out what you need from experience, not from YouTube recommendations with somebody telling you what you need. If your a gadget person and see something really cool that you just have to buy, I get it. I'm not guilty of doing that myself or anything – hey, don't judge me.

Going too hard too fast. Don't pick a hardcore 4x4 trail for your first trip. Just find a well maintained forest service road with some dispersed camping and start with that. Learn your vehicle's limits on easy terrain before you push them on hard terrain. Get used to going out and get some basic skills under your belt and build yourself up as you go.

Not telling anyone where you're going. Leave a trip plan with someone who's not going with you. Where you're headed, what route, when you expect to be back. Cell service is spotty to nonexistent in overlanding territory. Download offline maps for the area you are going to be in before you go.

Ignoring the weight. Overlanding gear adds up fast. RTT, water, fuel, food, tools, recovery gear, which you can easily add 500-800 lbs to your vehicle very fast. Know your payload capacity. Know what the extra weight does to your handling, braking, and fuel economy. The Vehicle Profile tool shows you exactly how different loads affect your specific vehicle, so check it out and work up an idea of what you're capable of.

Forgetting to air down. On gravel, sand, and rough terrain, airing your tires down to 15-25 PSI (from your normal 35-40) dramatically improves traction and ride comfort. Just remember to air back up before you hit the pavement. Running aired down tires at highway speed is a great way to blow a sidewall and I'd personally rather see you spend that money on some new gear.

Just Start

The best overlanding rig is the one you already have. The best overlanding trip is the first one you take. Everything else with all the upgrades, the gear, the fancy builds either comes later or not at all. Some people overland for years in a stock vehicle with a ground tent and have a better time than the guy in the $150,000 build who's afraid to scratch it. Yeah, I have a newer truck, but my mindset is it's a truck and I use it as a truck.

So pack the truck, fill the cooler and grab a map. Pick a forest road, drive until you find a spot and set up camp. Wake up, pack up and keep going or go home.

That's overlanding. Everything else is just accessories and an ego.
