This Isn't Fiction — I've Watched Pieces of It Happen

I'm not spinning some Red Dawn fantasy. I do mutual aid electrical work — California, Texas, Florida, Maine. I've rolled into towns where gas stations are dead, grocery shelves are picked clean, and people are just standing in their driveways refreshing their phones like that's going to bring the cell service back.

Same thing every time.

Not all at once, though. That's what gets people. It creeps. They keep thinking power's coming back in an hour. Then a day. Then three days. And by the time it hits them that nobody's riding in to save them — not anytime soon, at least — they've already burned through whatever little cushion they had. Gone. And now they're starting from zero with no store to run to.

So what I'm laying out here is a realistic 7-day scenario built on stuff that has actually happened. Just not all stacked on top of each other in the same place at the same time. Water systems failing post-hurricane. Fuel chains breaking after a refinery hit. Cell towers going silent because the backup gennies ran dry. I've personally watched every single one of these. This article asks the uncomfortable question: what if they all hit at once?

If you've already run through our SHTF Simulator, some of this will feel familiar. But this goes deeper — day by day, what actually breaks, what that means for your family, and what you should've had in place before day one even started.

Day 1-2: The Water Goes Away

Here's what most people don't get about municipal water: it runs on electricity. The pumps, the treatment plants, all of it. Needs power. When the grid drops, most systems kick over to backup generators. Those generators run on diesel. And diesel — well, there's only so much of it sitting around.

Day one, you notice the pressure dropping. Shower feels weak. Toilet takes forever to fill. Most people shrug. "Probably maintenance." By end of day one, what's coming out of the tap looks brownish. That's sediment getting kicked up from the pressure swings. Not going to kill you immediately, but it's a big fat warning sign that things are heading sideways.

Day two, the trickle stops.

I was in a small town outside Houston after a nasty storm season — water treatment plant lost power for something like 36 hours. Boil-water advisory went out fast, which sounds helpful until you think about it for two seconds. The advisory assumes you have a working stove. When the grid's down? You do not have a working stove. So you've got an advisory telling you to do something you can't do. Classic.

A family of five — that's my situation, three kids — goes through water way faster than people realize. Drinking, cooking, washing hands, flushing toilets. Roughly a gallon per person per day just for drinking. Tack on basic hygiene and cooking and you're closer to two. For my house, that's ten gallons a day. Minimum. So let me ask you something: how many days of water do you have stored right now? Actually think about it.

If you don't know the answer, go run the Water Storage Calculator. Plug in your actual household. The number it spits out is the only number that matters here.

The other thing about day two — saw this firsthand in Florida — people absolutely mob the stores. Cases of water vanish in hours. And the stores aren't getting resupplied because the trucks aren't running. Which brings us to the fun part.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Water storage: Minimum 14 gallons per person (2 gal/day x 7 days). Family of 5, that's 70 gallons. I use a mix of 7-gallon Aqua-Bricks and two 55-gallon drums at the cabin in PA.
  • Water filtration: Gravity-fed filter like the Proone, or a Sawyer Squeeze for backup. We did a whole water filter breakdown on these.
  • Water purification: Aquamira Water Treatment Drops — lightweight, long shelf life, dead simple.
  • WaterBOB: 65-gallon bathtub bladder. Fill this the SECOND you hear something bad is coming. Under $40 and it buys your family days of water. Honestly one of the best dollar-per-gallon investments out there.
  • Cooking without power: Camp stove with fuel. I keep a Camp Chef Everest at home — same one I use at the cabin.

Day 3: The Supply Chain Breaks

In our scenario, physical sabotage takes out a bridge and a rail switching station. Sounds dramatic until you look at how thin the supply chain actually is. Most metro areas carry about three days of food inventory at any given time. That's not some prepper talking point — that's straight from the American Trucking Associations. Three days. Under normal demand. When people are panic-buying? Way less.

Diesel stops flowing, trucks stop moving. Trucks stop, grocery stores go dark. Gas stations can't pump because — surprise — their pumps need electricity too. Even the ones with fuel sitting in their underground tanks are just useless metal boxes.

I was doing storm restoration up in Northern California and watched a gas station owner try to jury-rig a generator to his pumps so he could sell fuel. Fire department shut him down. No permits, fire hazard, the whole thing. He had 8,000 gallons of gas underground and couldn't move a single drop. Meanwhile, cars were lined up a mile deep at the one station that still had power. Absolute madness.

Day three is when things shift from "this is annoying" to "oh, this is serious." The stuff you didn't buy is now the stuff you can't get. Period. That jar of peanut butter you kept meaning to replace? Gone. The extra case of canned chicken? Should've grabbed it last Tuesday.

This is exactly why I've gotten kind of obsessive about rotating stock. Not hoarding — rotating. Big difference. You buy what you already eat, you buy more of it, and you use the oldest stuff first. The Food Storage Calculator will tell you how much you actually need based on your family size and how long you want to hold out. And the Deadstock tool tracks what's expiring so you're not throwing money away on cans that went bad six months ago in the back of your pantry. I've been guilty of that one myself.

Day three is also when your neighbors start knocking. Some of them you're happy to help — that's community, that's what we do. But some of them are going to be desperate, and desperate people make bad calls. Not the day for that conversation, though. That comes on day five.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Food storage: 2-4 weeks minimum for your household. Canned goods, freeze-dried meals, rice, beans — stuff your family will actually eat. Nobody's touching that quinoa during a crisis, trust me. Use the Food Storage Calculator for your number.
  • Fuel: 5-10 gallons of stabilized gasoline (STA-BIL keeps it good for 24 months). Approved containers. Away from the house.
  • Rotation tracking: Run the Deadstock tool right now. Today. Input what you have. It'll show you what's about to expire and where your gaps are.
  • Cash: $500-1,000 in small bills. ATMs don't work without power. Card readers don't work without internet. Cash is the only thing that still spends — at least for the first few days.
  • Manual can opener: I'm half laughing typing this, but I've literally met people who have 200 cans stockpiled and no way to open them.

Day 4: Communications Go Dark

Cell towers have battery backups. Most last 4-8 hours. Some have generators that push that to maybe 24-72 hours. But those generators need fuel, and we already covered that fuel isn't flowing anymore.

By day four, your phone is a flashlight with a camera. That's it.

And honestly? I think this is the day that messes with people the most. Not the water. Not the food. The silence. You can't call your parents to check on them. Can't text your brother to see if his kids are alright. Can't pull up the news to figure out if help is coming or how big this thing actually is. You're just... sitting there. Not knowing.

AM radio becomes everything. If you have one. And if anyone's still broadcasting. In a widespread event, even AM stations might go dark when their backup power dies. What you'll probably pick up is a mix of official emergency broadcasts and ham radio operators filling in gaps — some of it accurate, some of it absolute garbage. Sorting signal from noise becomes a survival skill all by itself.

At my cabin in PA, cell service is already trash on a good day. So I keep a Midland GXT1000VP4 radio set that my brother-in-law Adam and I use when we're out on the trails. Range is limited, but for staying in contact within a few miles? Works fine. Anything beyond that, you're looking at amateur HAM radio — and yeah, you need a license, but the test is honestly pretty easy and the Technician license gets you on repeaters that can reach across your whole region. Our emergency comms roundup covers all of it.

The isolation is the real enemy on day four. When you can't verify information, your brain fills in the blanks. And it never fills them in with good news. Does it ever? Having a communication plan with your family BEFORE this happens is worth more than any piece of gear in your garage. Where do you meet if you can't reach each other? Who's the out-of-state contact? Pro tip: long distance calls sometimes get through when local networks are jammed — having an uncle in Ohio as your relay point is a legit strategy that actually works.

Power for your devices matters too. Phone might be useless for calls, but it still has your maps, your documents, photos of important papers. Keeping it charged means solar or a battery bank. Our Solar Power Calculator helps you figure out what size setup you need, and the portable power station guide covers the hardware side. I run an EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra with two 220W panels — enough to keep critical devices charged and run small appliances. Sort of my insurance policy against going completely dark.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • AM/FM/NOAA radio: Hand-crank with solar backup. Midland ER310 does all three and charges via USB, solar, or crank. Just get one.
  • Two-way radios: At minimum, a set of FRS/GMRS radios for your immediate family and close neighbors.
  • Communication plan: Written down. On paper. Not in a notes app on a dead phone. Rally point, out-of-state contact, backup meeting times. Everyone in the family gets a copy.
  • Solar charging: Even a small Anker 625 solar panel keeps phones and radios alive. Run the Solar Power Calculator to size it right.
  • Paper maps. Your state, your county, your region. GPS is dead. Paper doesn't need a battery.
  • Important docs: Copies of IDs, insurance, medical info — waterproof bag in your bug-out bag.

Day 5: You Are Your Own First Responder

This is the day I hope never comes. Coordinated arson hits suburban industrial parks. Maybe it's opportunistic. Maybe it's planned. Doesn't matter why — what matters is the fire department can't get to all of them, you dial 911 and get a busy signal, and you realize that safety net you've leaned on your entire life has some pretty big holes in it.

I want to be careful here because this is where prepper content usually goes completely off the rails. I'm not going to tell you to go full compound mode and set up fighting positions around your cul-de-sac. Come on. That's not real life for most of us.

What IS real: somebody needs to be awake at your house at all times. Sounds intense until you realize it just means you and your spouse — or your neighbor, or your buddy — take shifts. One sleeps, one stays up. You're not patrolling the perimeter. You're just paying attention. Porch light on if you've got solar power. Motion-activated lights are worth their weight. Know what's normal on your street so you notice when something isn't.

I'll tell you what I saw after Hurricane Michael down in the Florida panhandle. The neighborhoods where people actually knew each other? Did fine. Shared food. Watched each other's houses. Moved debris together. The neighborhoods where everyone was a stranger to each other? Those got ugly fast. Theft. Arguments over generators. One guy tried to siphon fuel from a downed power company truck while my crew was working the lines fifty feet away. Broad daylight. Didn't even look embarrassed about it.

The prep for day five isn't a bunker. It's relationships. Talk to your neighbors now — the ones you'd trust to watch your kids. That's your team. And quietly make sure you can handle the ones who show up with bad intentions.

Our home security on a budget piece covers the practical stuff — motion lights, door reinforcement, camera systems that run on solar. None of it costs a fortune.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Motion-activated lights: Solar-powered, all entry points. Ring Solar Floodlight works without Wi-Fi for basic motion detection and light.
  • Door reinforcement: Door Armor MAX kit — reinforces frame, hinges, strike plate. Thirty minutes to install and now your door isn't getting kicked in.
  • Neighborhood relationships: No product link for this one. Go introduce yourself to the people on your street. Bring over some eggs or a six-pack. That $12 is going to pay off more than any gadget you own.
  • Fire extinguishers: One per floor, one in the kitchen, one in the garage. Kidde FA110 multipurpose — I keep four in my house.
  • Watch schedule template: 4-hour shifts work best. Write it out. 10pm-2am, 2am-6am, 6am-10am. Rotate nightly so nobody burns out.

Day 6: The Hygiene Wall

Nobody wants to talk about this day. So let's talk about it.

No running water for close to a week means toilets stopped flushing days ago. Municipal sewer, septic, doesn't matter — none of it's working right. Storm drains are becoming open sewage channels. The smell is very, very real. And the pathogens are even more real.

Waterborne diseases are the number one killer in grid-down scenarios worldwide. Not violence. Not starvation. Dysentery, cholera, hepatitis A — stuff we don't even think about in the U.S. because our water treatment infrastructure handles it for us. Quietly. In the background. Every single day. Take that away and we're dealing with the same risks that hit developing countries constantly.

If someone in your family catches a stomach bug on day six, that's not just uncomfortable — it's dangerous. Vomiting and diarrhea burn through your water supply, which accelerates dehydration, which creates a cascading problem you cannot outrun. Kids and elderly family members get hit hardest. A basic stomach bug that a healthy adult could muscle through becomes life-threatening for a nine-year-old who's already not drinking enough. And I've got three kids, so believe me, this is the scenario that keeps me up at night.

Sanitation basics — you need a way to deal with human waste that doesn't contaminate your water supply or your living space. A five-gallon bucket with a snap-on toilet seat and heavy-duty trash bags. Not glamorous. Works. Line the bag with kitty litter or enzyme-based waste treatment powder. Seal each bag, store it away from where you live and where your water is. Got yard space and no nearby wells? Dig a latrine.

Hand hygiene is your frontline defense here. Wet Ones antibacterial wipes work short-term, but what you really want is a hand-washing station — five-gallon jug with a spigot and soap. Yes, you'll feel like you're "wasting" stored water. You're not. You're preventing the illness that would cost three times that water in recovery. Trust me on this one.

Medical supplies get real important right about now. Our first aid essentials guide covers the full kit, but on day six specifically you need: oral rehydration salts (or the DIY recipe — 6 teaspoons sugar, half teaspoon salt, 1 liter clean water), anti-diarrheal meds, disposable gloves, bleach for water purification (8 drops unscented bleach per gallon, let stand 30 minutes), and enough N95 masks to handle the smell and airborne stuff near waste areas.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Emergency toilet: Luggable Loo 5-gallon bucket toilet seat + heavy-duty 8-gallon trash bags + enzyme waste treatment bags.
  • Sanitation supplies: Bleach (unscented, regular), hand soap, antibacterial wipes, disposable gloves, N95 masks. Buy in bulk — all of it's cheap right now and priceless later.
  • Oral rehydration salts: DripDrop ORS packets — easier and more accurate than mixing your own. Toss a box in your first aid kit.
  • First aid kit: Not the $20 drugstore thing with 4 band-aids and a pamphlet. A real one. Our first aid essentials guide breaks down what should actually be in it.
  • Medications: 30-day supply of every prescription med for everyone in your household. Anti-diarrheal, pain relievers, antihistamines, electrolyte powder. Rotate these — they expire.
  • Trash bags: Way more than you think you need. Waste, waterproofing, carrying stuff, makeshift rain cover — you'll use them for everything. Get contractor-grade 3-mil bags.

Day 7: The Fork in the Road

One week. Seven days without reliable water, food resupply, communication, or emergency services.

By now, one of two things is happening. Either recovery has started — you're hearing convoys, seeing utility trucks staging, catching fragments of news that the National Guard is moving in — or it hasn't. And if it hasn't? You need to get real honest with yourself about your situation.

How much water do you have left? How much food? Is everyone healthy, or is someone getting sick? Is your neighborhood holding together or is it coming apart?

Day seven is the decision point. Stay or go. And this is where having a plan matters more than anything sitting in your garage.

If recovery's coming, you shelter in place. Ride it out. You've got your supplies, your security watch is running, keep your head down and wait. Most scenarios end right here. The cavalry shows up, the grid flickers back on, life returns to some version of normal over the next few weeks. That's the realistic outcome for the vast majority of infrastructure failures. Not sexy. Just true.

But if recovery is NOT coming — if the scope of this thing is bigger than your region can handle — you need to already know where you're going, how you're getting there, and what's coming with you. For me, that's the cabin in PA. I know the route. Driven it a hundred times in my Sierra. I know which roads jam up and which back roads actually work. I know how much fuel it takes and I keep enough on hand to get there without stopping.

Your bug-out bag should have been packed since day zero. Not thrown together in a panic at hour 168. Our bug-out bag checklist is built for real families, not solo wilderness survival types. It accounts for kids, medications, documents, and the fact that you're probably driving — not rucking through the woods like some kind of action movie.

But here's what I really want you to walk away with: day seven doesn't determine your outcome. Day negative-thirty does. The work you put in a month before this started — stocking water, building food supply, setting up comms, getting to know your neighbors, tracking your stuff so nothing expires — that's what determines whether day seven is uncomfortable or catastrophic. The gap between those two outcomes is enormous, and it gets decided long before anything bad happens.

The Honest Truth About All of This

I've been doing mutual aid electrical work long enough to know that infrastructure is both tougher and more fragile than people think. Tougher because there are thousands of dedicated people — lineworkers, water plant operators, first responders — who work insane hours to get things back online. I've been one of those people. I've slept in my truck for four days straight to get power restored to a hospital. You do not complain about overtime on those calls.

But fragile because the systems are all tangled up together in ways most people never think about. Power goes out, water goes with it. Fuel stops, food stops. Communications fail, and trust fails right behind them. Each domino knocks over the next one, and by day three you're living in a different world than you were on day one.

Good news: you don't need a bunker. You do not need $50,000 in gear. You need water storage, food rotation, a way to communicate, basic security, and sanitation supplies. That's the real list. Everything else on top of that is optimization. Nice to have, not need to have.

Most people reading this have gaps. That's fine — I still have gaps. The difference between being ready and not being ready isn't perfection. It's knowing where your holes are and filling them one paycheck at a time. Nobody goes from zero to fully prepped in a weekend. That's not how this works.
