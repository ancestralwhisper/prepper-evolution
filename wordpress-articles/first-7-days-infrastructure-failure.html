This Isn't Fiction — I've Watched Pieces of It Happen

I'm not going to spin you some Red Dawn fantasy here. I've done mutual aid electrical work in California, Texas, Florida, and Maine. I've pulled into towns where the gas stations are dark, the grocery store shelves are bare, and people are standing in their driveways looking at their phones like maybe if they refresh hard enough, service will come back.

Every time, the same thing happens. Not all at once. Slowly. And that slow part is what gets people. They keep thinking things will come back online in an hour. Then a day. Then three days. And by the time they realize nobody is coming — not soon, anyway — they've burned through whatever cushion they had.

What I'm laying out here is a realistic 7-day scenario built on things that have actually happened — just not all at the same time, in the same place. Water systems failing after a hurricane. Fuel supply chains breaking after a refinery hit. Cell towers going dark because the backup generators ran dry. I've seen every one of these individually. This article asks: what if they stacked up?

If you've run through our SHTF Simulator, some of this will feel familiar. But this goes deeper. Day by day. What breaks, what that means for your family, and what you need to already have in place before day one.

Day 1-2: The Water Goes Away

Here's what most people don't understand about municipal water: it runs on electricity. The pumps that push water through the system, the treatment plants that keep it safe — all of it needs power. When the grid drops, most systems have backup generators. Those generators run on diesel. And diesel is a finite thing.

Day one, you probably notice the pressure dropping. The shower feels weak. The toilet takes a little longer to fill. Most people shrug it off. "Must be work on the line." By the end of day one, what comes out of the tap has a brownish tint to it. That's sediment getting kicked up as pressure fluctuates. It's not immediately dangerous, but it's a signal.

Day two, the trickle stops.

I was in a small town outside Houston after a bad storm season, and the water treatment plant lost power for about 36 hours. The boil-water advisory went out fast, but people didn't have power to boil with. Think about that for a second. The advisory assumes you have a working stove. When the grid is down, you don't.

A family of five — which is what I've got — goes through water faster than most people realize. Drinking, cooking, washing hands, flushing toilets. You're looking at roughly a gallon per person per day for drinking alone. Add in basic hygiene and cooking, and you're closer to two. For my family, that's ten gallons a day minimum. The other thing about day two — and I saw this firsthand in Florida — is that people start hitting the stores. Hard. Cases of water disappear in hours. And the stores aren't getting resupplied because trucks aren't running. Which brings us to day three.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Water storage: Minimum 14 gallons per person (2 gal/day x 7 days). For a family of 5, that's 70 gallons. I use a mix of 7-gallon Aqua-Bricks and two 55-gallon drums at the cabin.
  • Water filtration: A gravity-fed filter like the Proone or a Sawyer Squeeze for backup. See our water filter breakdown.
  • Water purification: Aquamira Water Treatment Drops — lightweight, long shelf life, dead simple to use.
  • WaterBOB: 65-gallon bathtub bladder — fill this the second you hear something bad is coming. Costs under $40 and buys your family days.
  • Cooking without power: Camp stove with fuel. I keep a Camp Chef Everest at home — same burner I use at the cabin.

Day 3: The Supply Chain Breaks

In our scenario, physical sabotage takes out a bridge and a rail switching station. Sounds dramatic until you look at how thin the supply chain actually is. Most metro areas have about three days of food inventory at any given time. That's not some prepper stat — that's straight from the American Trucking Associations. Three days. And that's under normal demand.

When diesel stops flowing, trucks stop moving. When trucks stop, grocery stores go dark. Gas stations can't pump because their pumps need electricity too. Even the ones with fuel in their underground tanks are just sitting there useless.

I was doing storm restoration in Northern California, and I watched a gas station owner try to rig up a generator to run his pumps so he could sell fuel. Local fire department shut him down — no permits, fire hazard, all that. He had 8,000 gallons of gas underground and couldn't move a drop of it. Meanwhile, people were lined up for a mile at the one station that still had power.

Day three is when things shift from inconvenient to serious. The stuff you didn't buy is now the stuff you can't get. That jar of peanut butter you've been meaning to replace? Gone. The extra case of canned chicken? Should've grabbed it last week.

This is exactly why I've become obsessive about rotating stock. Not hoarding — rotating. There's a difference. You buy what you already eat, you buy more of it, and you use the oldest first. The Food Storage Calculator will tell you how much you actually need based on your family size and how long you want to hold out. And the Deadstock tool tracks what's expiring so you're not throwing money away on stuff that goes bad in the back of your pantry.

Day three is also when your neighbors start knocking. Some of them you're happy to help — that's what community is. But some of them are going to be desperate, and desperate people make bad decisions. This isn't the day for that conversation, though. That comes on day five.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Food storage: 2-4 weeks minimum for your household. Canned goods, freeze-dried meals, rice, beans — stuff your family will actually eat. Use the Food Storage Calculator for your number.
  • Fuel: 5-10 gallons of stabilized gasoline (STA-BIL keeps it good for 24 months). Store in approved containers away from your house.
  • Rotation tracking: Run the Deadstock tool right now. Input what you have. It'll show you what's about to expire and what gaps you're carrying.
  • Cash: $500-1,000 in small bills. ATMs don't work without power. Card readers don't work without internet. Cash is the only thing that still spends.
  • Manual can opener: I'm laughing typing this, but I've met people with 200 cans and no way to open them.

Day 4: Communications Go Dark

Cell towers have battery backups. Most of them last 4-8 hours. Some have generators that can push that to 24-72 hours. But those generators need fuel, and we already established that fuel isn't flowing.

By day four, your phone is a flashlight with a camera. That's it.

I think this is the day that messes with people the most. Not the water. Not the food. The silence. You can't call your parents to check on them. You can't text your brother to see if his kids are okay. You can't check the news to find out if help is coming or how far this thing has spread.

AM radio becomes everything. If you have one. And if anyone's still broadcasting. In a widespread event, even AM stations may go dark if their backup power gives out. What you'll probably hear is a mix of official emergency broadcasts and people on amateur radio filling in gaps — some accurate, some not. Sorting signal from noise becomes its own survival skill.

At my cabin in PA, cell service is already garbage on a good day. So I've got a Midland GXT1000VP4 radio set that my brother-in-law Adam and I use when we're out on the trails. Range is limited, but for keeping in contact within a few miles, they work. For anything beyond that, you're looking at amateur (HAM) radio — and yeah, you need a license, but the test is easy and the Technician license gets you on repeaters that can reach across your whole region. Check out our emergency comms roundup for the full breakdown.

The isolation is the real enemy here. When you can't verify information, your brain fills in the gaps — and it never fills them in with good news. Having a communication plan with your family BEFORE this happens is more valuable than any piece of gear. Where do you meet if you can't reach each other? Who's the out-of-state contact? (Pro tip: long distance calls sometimes get through when local networks are jammed — having an uncle in Ohio as your relay point is a real strategy.)

Power for your devices matters too. Your phone might be useless for calls, but it still has your maps, your documents, your photos of important papers. Keeping it charged means having solar or a battery bank. Our Solar Power Calculator will help you figure out what size setup you need — and our portable power station guide covers the hardware. I'm running an EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra with two 220W panels, and that's enough to keep critical devices charged and run small appliances.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • AM/FM/NOAA radio: Hand-crank with solar backup. Midland ER310 does all three and charges via USB, solar, or hand crank.
  • Two-way radios: At minimum, a set of FRS/GMRS radios for your immediate family and close neighbors.
  • Communication plan: Written down. On paper. Rally point, out-of-state contact, backup meeting times. Everyone in the family should have a copy.
  • Solar charging: Even a small Anker 625 solar panel keeps phones and radios alive. Run the Solar Power Calculator to size your setup right.
  • Paper maps: Your state, your county, your region. When GPS is dead, paper is king.
  • Important docs: Copies of IDs, insurance policies, medical info — in a waterproof bag in your bug-out bag.

Day 5: You Are Your Own First Responder

This is the day I hope never comes. Coordinated arson hits suburban industrial parks. Maybe it's opportunistic. Maybe it's planned. Doesn't matter why — what matters is the fire department can't get to all of them, 911 gives you a busy signal, and you realize that the safety net you've relied on your entire life has holes in it.

I want to be careful here because this is where prepper content usually goes off the rails. I'm not going to tell you to go full compound mode and set up fighting positions around your cul-de-sac. That's not real life for most of us.

What IS real: someone needs to be awake at your house at all times. A 24-hour security watch sounds intense until you realize it just means you and your spouse (or your neighbor, or your buddy) take shifts. One sleeps, one stays up. You're not patrolling — you're just paying attention. Porch light on if you've got solar power. Motion-activated lights are worth their weight in gold. Know what's normal on your street so you notice what isn't.

I'll tell you what I saw after Hurricane Michael in the Florida panhandle: the neighborhoods where people knew each other did fine. They shared food. They watched each other's houses. They moved debris together. The neighborhoods where everyone was a stranger to each other? Those got ugly faster. Theft. Arguments over generators. One guy tried to siphon fuel from a downed power company truck while my crew was working on the lines fifty feet away.

The prep for day five isn't a bunker. It's relationships. Talk to your neighbors now. The ones you'd trust to watch your kids — that's your team. And quietly make sure you can handle the ones who come with bad intentions.

Our home security on a budget piece covers the practical stuff — motion lights, door reinforcement, camera systems that work on solar. None of it requires a huge investment.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Motion-activated lights: Solar-powered, placed at all entry points. Ring Solar Floodlight works without Wi-Fi for basic motion detection and light.
  • Door reinforcement: Door Armor MAX kit — reinforces the frame, hinges, and strike plate. Takes 30 minutes to install.
  • Neighborhood relationships: No product link for this one. Go introduce yourself to the people on your street. Bring over some extra eggs or a six-pack. That $12 investment pays off more than any gadget.
  • Fire extinguishers: One per floor, one in the kitchen, one in the garage. Kidde FA110 multipurpose — I keep four in my house.
  • Watch schedule template: 4-hour shifts work best. Write it out. Who's on from 10pm-2am, 2am-6am, 6am-10am. Rotate nightly so nobody burns out.

Day 6: The Hygiene Wall

This is the day nobody wants to talk about, so I'm going to talk about it.

No running water for nearly a week means toilets stopped flushing days ago. Whatever system your neighborhood has — municipal sewer, septic, doesn't matter — it's not working right. Storm drains are becoming open sewage channels. The smell is real. More importantly, the pathogens are real.

Waterborne diseases are the number one killer in grid-down scenarios worldwide. Not violence. Not starvation. Dysentery, cholera, hepatitis A — things we don't even think about in the U.S. because our water treatment infrastructure handles it. Take that infrastructure away, and we're back to the same risks that developing countries deal with daily.

If someone in your family gets a stomach bug on day six, that's not just uncomfortable — it's dangerous. Vomiting and diarrhea accelerate dehydration, which burns through your water supplies faster, which creates a cascading problem you can't outrun. Kids and elderly family members are the most vulnerable. A basic stomach bug that a healthy adult could handle becomes life-threatening for a nine-year-old who's already not drinking enough water.

Sanitation basics: you need a way to deal with human waste that doesn't contaminate your water supply or your living space. A five-gallon bucket with a snap-on toilet seat and heavy-duty trash bags works. It's not glamorous, but it works. Line the bag with kitty litter or enzyme-based waste treatment powder. Seal each bag and store away from your living area and water sources. Dig a latrine if you have yard space and no nearby wells.

Hand hygiene is the frontline defense. Wet Ones antibacterial wipes are fine for short-term, but what you really want is a hand-washing station — a five-gallon jug with a spigot and some soap. Use your stored water for this. Yes, it feels like you're "wasting" water. You're not. You're preventing the illness that would cost you three times that water.

Medical supplies become critical right now. Our first aid essentials guide covers the full kit, but on day six specifically, you need: oral rehydration salts (or the DIY recipe — 6 teaspoons sugar, half teaspoon salt, 1 liter clean water), anti-diarrheal meds, disposable gloves, bleach for water purification (8 drops unscented bleach per gallon, let stand 30 minutes), and enough N95 masks to deal with the smell and airborne particles near any waste areas.

What You Need In Place Before This Happens

  • Emergency toilet: Luggable Loo 5-gallon bucket toilet seat + heavy-duty 8-gallon trash bags + enzyme waste treatment bags.
  • Sanitation supplies: Bleach (unscented, regular), hand soap, antibacterial wipes, disposable gloves, N95 masks. Buy these in bulk — they're cheap now and priceless later.
  • Oral rehydration salts: DripDrop ORS packets — easier and more accurate than mixing your own. Keep a box in your first aid kit.
  • First aid kit: Not a $20 drugstore kit. A real one. See our first aid essentials guide for what should actually be in it.
  • Medications: 30-day supply of any prescription meds for everyone in the household. Anti-diarrheal, pain relievers, antihistamines, electrolyte powder. Rotate these — they expire.
  • Trash bags: Way more than you think. You'll use them for waste, for waterproofing, for carrying, for everything. Get contractor-grade 3-mil bags.

Day 7: The Fork in the Road

One week. Seven days without reliable water, food resupply, communication, or emergency services.

By now, one of two things is happening. Either recovery has started — you're hearing convoys, seeing utility trucks staging, getting fragments of news that the National Guard is moving in — or it hasn't. And if it hasn't, you need to be honest with yourself about your situation.

How much water do you have left? How much food? Is everyone in your family healthy, or is someone getting sick? Is your neighborhood holding together, or is it falling apart?

Day seven is the decision point. Do you stay or do you go? And this is where having a plan matters more than anything in your garage.

If recovery is coming, you shelter in place. You ride it out. You've got your supplies, your security watch is running, and you keep your head down and wait. Most scenarios end here. The cavalry comes, the grid comes back, and life returns to some version of normal over the next few weeks. This is the realistic outcome for the vast majority of infrastructure failures.

If recovery is NOT coming — if the scope of this thing is bigger than your region can handle — you need to already know where you're going, how you're getting there, and what you're taking with you. For me, that's the cabin in PA. I know the route. I've driven it a hundred times. I know which roads get jammed and which back roads work. I know how much fuel it takes and I keep enough on hand.

Your bug-out bag should have been packed since day zero. Not thrown together in a panic on day seven. Our bug-out bag checklist is built for real families, not solo wilderness survivors. It accounts for kids, medications, documents, and the fact that you're probably driving, not hiking through the woods.

But here's what I really want you to take away from this: day seven doesn't determine your outcome. Day negative-thirty does. The work you put in a month before — stocking water, building your food supply, setting up a communication plan, getting to know your neighbors, tracking your supplies so nothing expires on you — that's what determines whether day seven is uncomfortable or catastrophic.

The Honest Truth About All of This

I've been doing mutual aid electrical work long enough to know that infrastructure is both tougher and more fragile than people think. Tougher because there are thousands of dedicated people — lineworkers, water plant operators, first responders — who work insane hours to get things back online. I've been one of those people. I've slept in my truck for four days straight to get power restored to a hospital.

But fragile because the systems are interconnected in ways most people never consider. Power goes out, and water goes with it. Fuel stops, and food stops. Communications fail, and trust fails. Each domino hits the next one, and by day three you're in a different world than you were on day one.

The good news: you don't need a bunker. You don't need $50,000 in gear. You need water storage, food rotation, a way to communicate, basic security, and sanitation supplies. That's the real list. Everything else is optimization.

Most people reading this have gaps. That's fine — I still have gaps. The difference between being ready and not being ready isn't perfection. It's awareness. Knowing what your gaps are and working on them, one paycheck at a time.
