I've Used Both. I Have Both. Here's the Truth.

So you're trying to figure out if you should go the power station route or the generator route. This debate drives me crazy because the answer is always "it depends" and nobody ever explains what it depends ON. So let me cut through the noise.

I run a power station setup right now with the EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra with three 220W solar panels. It's my primary home backup and that handles everything I need. But I'm also actively researching tri-fuel generators because there are a couple of scenarios where a power station won't cut it. And I'll tell you exactly what those are.

I'm a lineman for the power company. I've been on mutual aid trips in California, Texas, Florida, Maine, Long Island, New York and New Jersey. I've seen people's generator setups fail because they didn't understand fuel consumption. I've seen people's power stations die because they oversized their loads. I've seen $3,000 worth of equipment become a paperweight because someone didn't read the manual.

Let's make sure you do not become any of those folks.

What a Portable Power Station Actually Is

A portable power station is basically a big battery with an inverter. You plug it in and charge it up from wall power, solar panels, or a car charging outlet and then it puts out clean AC power through standard household outlets. There is no gas, no emissions, no noise, and zero maintenance. Easy Peasy.

The more up to date ones use LiFePO4 (lithium iron phosphate) batteries that last 3,000-5,000 charge cycles. If you charge that power station for cycle per day, that's 8-13 YEARS of daily use. They're fairly heavy but portable. I own the DELTA 3 Ultra and it weighs about 60 lbs. You can pick it up and move it, but you're not casually tossing it around. To help with that, EcoFlow actually put a retractable handle on it similar to your luggage and a set of wheels to make it easy to move around.

They put out clean, stable power known as “pure sine wave” that's safe for sensitive electronics like medical devices, computers, and routers. There are no dirty power spikes and no voltage fluctuations. The end product is just clean, quiet electricity.

What a Generator Actually Is

A generator uses fuel and burns gas, propane, natural gas, or a combination (tri-fuel) to spin a motor that generates electricity. The Honda EU2200i is the gold standard for portable generators: 2,200W, whisper-quiet for a generator (48-57 dB), fuel efficient, and bulletproof reliable. I see the telco and the cable guys use these quite a bit on storms. They actually have a small rack that gets bolted to the utility pole and the generator sits on a shelf. They typically lock it to the pole with a metal chain so nobody steals it.





On an ice storm up in Blairestown, NJ we were scoping out a job that led up to an old fire tower on the top of a mountain. We didn't have the access to the trail because our bucket trucks were too big and the access road was really muddy, however an engineer from the local radio station showed up with a tracked UTV. He had to go to the top of the mountain to install one of those Honda generators to power an antenna. He gave us a lift up the mountain while he was going up there anyway.



Generators produce power continuously as long as you feed them fuel. There isn't a battery to deplete. No solar panels needed. Just gas and oil. That's both their greatest strength and their biggest weakness, depending on your situation.

When a Power Station Wins

Short to Medium Outages (1-3 Days)

This is where power stations dominate the field. If your area gets that occasional 1-3 day outage from storms or grid issues, a power station handles that beautifully. All you need to do is charge it up when you know weather is coming, and it'll run your fridge, charge your phones, power your router, and run some lights for 24-48 hours depending on the size that you have.

With solar panels, you extend that indefinitely as long as you get a few hours of decent sunlight, you can top off the battery everyday and keep cycling. My DELTA 3 Ultra with 660W of solar panels can maintain my fridge and basic electronics pretty much forever during a summer outage.

Apartment or Condo Living

There really is no way you can run a generator in an apartment. Period. No balcony operation, no running extension cords out the window, none of it, unless you are on the first floor unit and your association doesn't have a problem with it. Carbon monoxide doesn't care about your lease terms and it'll kill you. A power station is the truly one of the ONLY option for apartment dwellers.

Quiet Operation Required

Most generators are extremely loud. Even the Honda EU2200i, which is considered "whisper quiet" for a generator, runs at 48-57 dB. That's as loud as a normal conversation between two peolple. At 2 AM during a power outage, your neighbors will hear it. In some HOAs, they'll fine you for it. In a grid down security situation, noise is a signal that says "this house has power and stuff” and if there is no other noise around people will be attracted to you like a moth to a flame.

Power stations run at 25-35 dB under normal loads. That's quiet enough to bring into a library. Nobody knows you have power unless you tell them and loose lips sink ships.

Sensitive Electronics

If you're running CPAP machines, medical equipment, computers, or anything with sensitive circuitry, a power station's pure sine wave output is cleaner than grid power. Most generators claim "clean power" but still have more THD (total harmonic distortion) than a good inverter battery does. For medical devices, this matters.

When a Generator Wins

Extended Outages (5+ Days)

Here's where the scale starts to shift a bit more in favor of the generator. A power station has a fixed amount of energy. My DELTA 3 Ultra has 3,072Wh. Running a full-size fridge (about 100-150W average draw), that's roughly 20-30 hours per charge. With 660W of solar panels and good sun light, I can sustainably run maybe 3-4 kWh per day.

Ah, but what about those cloudy days? What about winter when I'm getting 3 hours of weak sun if I'm lucky? What about running the fridge AND a space heater AND charging devices? The math falls apart fast.

A generator running on straight propane can burn through 5+ days without any solar dependency. A twenty gallon propane tank runs a 2000W generator for about 40 hours. Stack three tanks and you've got 120 hours or five days of continuous power. No sun required.

Heavy Loads

Do you need to run a well pump? A sump pump during a flood? A space heater in winter? A window AC unit in summer? These are 1,000-1,500W continuous loads that will drain a battery in a few hours. A generator just keeps running.

And if you're running a whole-house transfer switch setup where the generator powers your home's circuits directly you are getting into generator territory. Power stations can do this too (EcoFlow has a smart home panel) and they do have whole home power solutions from 12kWh to 36kWh, but the sustained output you need for multiple circuits running simultaneously usually exceeds what battery power can reasonably handle for extended periods of time. With that type of EcoFlow whole home battery backup they still recommended to pair it with a generator for unlimited runtime.

Baby, It's Cold Outside

LiFePO4 batteries don't charge below 32F. They can discharge, but a built in safery mechanism won't allow it to accept a charge. So if your power station is in an unheated garage in January in New Jersey and the power goes out, you can drain the battery but you can't recharge it from solar until the ambient temperature comes up or you bring it inside.

Generators don't care about temperature. Gas might gel in extreme cold, but propane works fine to -40F.

The Hybrid Approach (What I've Got)

I think it's the smart play for most people if you choose to go this route:

Get a power station + solar for everyday use and short outages and a generator for extended backup when solar isn't enough.

The power station handles 90% of scenarios silently and cleanly. The generator sits in the garage with a few propane tanks for that other 10% of the week long winter ice storm, the extended grid failure, the serious stuff.

I'm looking at tri-fuel generators specifically because they can run on gasoline, propane, OR natural gas. If my natural gas line is still working during an outage (which it usually is, gas and electric are different systems), I can run the generator indefinitely without storing any fuel. Propane for backup if the gas line goes down and gasoline as a last resort.

The Hutch Mountain tri-fuel conversion kit for the Honda EU2200i is on my short list. Takes the most reliable inverter generator on the market and gives it total fuel flexibility.

Sizing: How to Figure Out What You Need

This is where most people go wrong. They either buy too small and can't run what they need, or they buy too big and waste money.

Here's the quick and dirty calculation:

List everything you'd want to run during an outage. Not everything in your house, just the stuff that matters. For most families, that's:

  • Refrigerator: 100-150W average (400-800W startup surge)

  • Phone charging: 10-20W per phone

  • WiFi router: 10-15W

  • LED lights: 5-15W each

  • Laptop: 50-65W

  • Fan: 30-75W

  • CPAP: 30-60W

Add up the total average watts and you will get your continuous load. Most families land somewhere between 200-500W for basic comfort and this is a conservative number if you are very disciplined. Now multiply that by hours to get watt-hours you need per day.

300W average x 24 hours = 7,200Wh per day.

A 2,000Wh power station covers about 6-7 hours at that rate. With 400W of solar generating maybe 1,200-1,600Wh per day (depending on sun), you're in a deficit. Either reduce loads, get a bigger battery, add more solar, or accept that a generator fills the gap.

The Money Question

People always ask "which is cheaper?" and the answer is - compared to what?

Upfront, yes a basic generator is cheaper. You can get a reliable 2000W inverter generator for $500-1,000. A comparable power station is $800-2,000, plus $200-400 for solar panels.

But generators have ongoing costs. Gasoline (which gets expensive if you're running daily), oil changes, maintenance, fuel stabilizer. Gas also starts to go bad in 6-12 months if you don't stabilize it and store it properly. I've seen so many people pull start a generator during a hurricane and it won't fire because the gas has been sitting in the tank since the last hurricane and the carburetor is all gummed up.

Power stations have zero operating cost if you're charging from solar. Zero. Sunlight is free and the math shifts in the power station's favor over time, especially if you're using it regularly while camping, tailgating, job sites, or whatever.

Safety: This Is Not Optional Reading

Generator carbon monoxide poisoning kills about 80 people per year in the US. During the hurricane season, emergency rooms fill up with CO poisoning cases because people run generators in garages, basements, or too close to open windows not realizing they need to vent the exhaust outside. Even if it's outside it has to be in an open area. Right before Hurricane Sandy hit I bought a Generac gas generator and had it about 25 feet outside my house in the corner of where the fence met. I ran it for a while to test to see how it would do and I went back into my house. A few minutes later I could smell the exhaust fumes from inside my house because the airflow wasn't adequate enough and the exhaust seemed to be pooling in that area, so I had to move it.

If you buy a generator: run it OUTSIDE, at least 25 feet from any window, door, or vent. Get a generator with a CO sensor (most new Honda and Champion models have them). Get battery operated CO detectors for your house if you don't already have them. Yes, I know they're not cheap, but would you rather spend the money or spend your time 6 feet under the dirt?

This is the single biggest argument for power stations. You can run them on your kitchen counter. No CO risk. No fire risk (LiFePO4 doesn't burn like older lithium chemistries). No gas to spill.

My Recommendation

If you're starting from zero and can only buy one thing:

Apartment/condo: Power station without a question. 1,000-2,000Wh range. Add a solar panel when budget allows as you can always add that on later.

House, short outages common: Power station with solar panels. It covers 90% of scenarios, zero maintenance, zero fuel cost.

House, long outages common: Generator first (propane or tri-fuel), then add a power station for quiet overnight use and sensitive electronics.

House, want the best of both: Power station + solar for daily use, generator for extended backup. This is where I'm going and I think it's the right answer for most homeowners in areas with serious weather.
