Yeah, There's Stuff Growing in There

If you've owned a hydration bladder for more than a few months and haven't cleaned it, I've got bad news. Open it up. Look inside. See those dark spots? That film? That slight smell that's somewhere between a wet towel and a science experiment?

That's mold. Or algae. Or biofilm. Possibly all three.

And you've been drinking through it.

Look, I'm not judging. I've been there. After a long day hiking the Pine Barrens or a trail ride in Utah, the last thing you want to do is thoroughly clean your hydration bladder. So it goes in a bag or a closet while still damp, sits there for two weeks, and next time you pull it out it's hosting its own ecosystem.

Here's the thing — this is completely fixable. And with the right habits, completely preventable. I'm going to walk you through both.

The Quick Clean (After Every Use)

This takes 5 minutes. Maybe less. If you do this every time you use your bladder, you will almost never need the deep clean process below.

  • Dump all remaining water immediately after your trip. Don't let it sit.
  • Fill the bladder halfway with warm water. Give it a good shake. Drain it through the hose and bite valve to flush the entire system.
  • Repeat once more.
  • Open the bladder as wide as possible and hang it upside down to dry. This is the most important part — air circulation inside the bladder while drying. More on drying later because it matters a LOT.

That's it. Five minutes. Do it every single time and you're ahead of 90% of people.

The Deep Clean: Baking Soda Method

This is my go-to for regular deep cleaning or mild funk. It's gentle, non-toxic, cheap, and it works. Do this once a month if you're using your bladder regularly, or anytime you notice a taste or smell.

What You Need

  • 2 tablespoons of baking soda
  • Warm water (not hot — hot water can damage some bladder materials)
  • A bladder cleaning brush or a long bottle brush

Process

Mix the baking soda with about a cup of warm water and shake until dissolved. Pour it into the bladder. Fill the rest of the bladder with warm water. Shake it around. Let it sit for 30 minutes.

While it soaks, pinch the bite valve open and let some of the solution flow through the drinking tube. You want to treat the tube too — stuff grows in there just as easily as in the main reservoir.

After 30 minutes, use a long brush to scrub the inside surfaces. Pay attention to the corners and the edges near the opening — that's where mold likes to set up camp. If your bladder has a wide mouth (most modern ones do — CamelBak Crux, Osprey Hydraulics, etc.), you can get your whole hand in there with a sponge.

Drain, rinse twice with clean water, flush the tube, and dry completely. Done.

The Nuclear Option: Bleach Method

For when things have gotten bad. Like, visible mold growth bad. Black spots bad. "I forgot about this bladder in my gear closet for 6 months" bad.

I'm going to be real — if your bladder is REALLY moldy, you should probably just replace it. They're $25-40 and your health is worth more than saving twenty bucks. But if the mold is surface-level and the bladder is otherwise in good shape, bleach will kill everything.

What You Need

  • 1/4 teaspoon of unscented household bleach (regular Clorox — not the splashless or scented kind)
  • 1 liter of water

Process

Mix the bleach into the water. Pour it into the bladder. Fill the rest with water if needed. Let some flow through the drinking tube and bite valve.

Let it soak for 30 minutes. No more than that — extended bleach exposure can degrade the bladder material.

After soaking, dump it out, and this is the critical part — rinse thoroughly. I'm talking 3-4 full rinses with clean water. Run water through the tube multiple times. You do NOT want residual bleach taste in your next drink. Nobody wants a hint of pool water on a trail.

Some people are nervous about bleach. Fair enough. A quarter teaspoon per liter is a very mild solution — it's actually similar to the concentration water treatment plants use. But if it makes you uncomfortable, the baking soda method handles most situations.

Cleaning Tablets: The Easy Button

CamelBak cleaning tablets are designed specifically for hydration bladders. Drop one in, fill with water, wait 30 minutes, rinse, done. They're a combination of baking soda and citric acid that fizzes and scrubs without physical brushing (though brushing still helps).

Are they better than plain baking soda? Marginally. They're more convenient. They come in individual tablets so there's no measuring. And they rinse out cleaner — no residual taste. But they cost about $1-2 per tablet, which adds up if you're cleaning monthly.

I keep a pack around for convenience but baking soda is my regular method. Same result, fraction of the cost.

The Vinegar Method

Some people swear by white vinegar. I used to use this before switching to baking soda. Mix 1/4 cup of white vinegar per liter of warm water. Fill the bladder. Soak 30 minutes. Scrub. Rinse multiple times.

Vinegar is a decent mild acid that kills some mold and bacteria. It's not as effective as bleach for serious mold but it's gentler on the bladder material. The main downside is the smell — even after thorough rinsing, you might get a faint vinegar taste for the first use or two.

Honestly? Baking soda is easier, equally effective for routine cleaning, and doesn't leave any taste. I don't bother with vinegar anymore.

Don't Forget the Drinking Tube and Bite Valve

Here's where most people slack off. You clean the bladder itself and ignore the tube. But the tube is narrow, dark, and stays damp — it's a mold highway. And the bite valve has all sorts of nooks and crannies where biofilm builds up.

Tube Cleaning

A tube cleaning brush (comes with most cleaning kits, or buy one separately — they're like $5) is the right tool. Thread it through the tube and scrub back and forth a few times. If you don't have a tube brush, running cleaning solution through the tube multiple times and then flushing with clean water is the next best thing.

Or just disconnect the tube and drop it in a glass of cleaning solution (baking soda or bleach mix) for 30 minutes. Works great.

Bite Valve

Pull the bite valve off the tube (most just pull straight off). Soak it in the cleaning solution separately. Use a small brush or even a Q-tip to get into the groove where your teeth grip. That groove collects bacteria like crazy. Rinse and reattach.

If the bite valve is chewed through, cracked, or permanently discolored — just replace it. They're $5-10 and it's not worth the risk. CamelBak, Osprey, and most brands sell replacement valves separately.

Drying: This Is Where Most People Fail

Cleaning your bladder perfectly means nothing if you store it wet. Moisture is what starts the mold cycle. You need to dry this thing completely before it goes into storage.

The problem is that hydration bladders are designed to be sealed and flexible — the interior surfaces collapse against each other, trapping moisture. You need to keep them propped open to allow airflow.

Methods That Work

  • Bladder drying hanger: The CamelBak cleaning kit includes a hanger that holds the bladder open while it hangs. This is the simplest solution. Hook it over a doorknob or a towel rack and let it air dry overnight.
  • Paper towel trick: Stuff a wad of paper towels inside the bladder and leave the mouth open. The paper towels absorb moisture and keep the walls separated. Cheap and effective. This is my method at the cabin in PA where I don't have a drying hanger.
  • Chopstick or whisk method: Stick a wooden chopstick or a kitchen whisk inside the bladder opening to prop the walls apart. Hang it upside down.
  • Open + fan: If you're in a hurry, prop the bladder open and aim a small fan at it. Dry in a couple hours.

Methods That Don't Work

  • Closing it up while damp: Obviously. But so many people do this.
  • Throwing it in a gear bag while wet: Same problem, plus now your gear bag smells like mildew too.
  • Putting it in the freezer: I've seen this recommended. The idea is that freezing prevents mold. Technically true — but it doesn't DRY the bladder. When it thaws, it's still wet. And now you have to deal with ice potentially damaging the material. Just dry it properly.

Storing It Right

Once completely dry, you have two storage options:

Option 1: Store it clean and dry. Fold it loosely (don't crease it hard), store in a drawer or on a shelf. When you're ready to use it again, give it a quick rinse before filling.

Option 2: Store it in the freezer. Once dry, fold it up and stick it in the freezer. Even if there's trace moisture you missed, freezing prevents any mold growth. This is actually a decent trick for long-term storage between camping seasons. I do this with my bladder from about November through March.

Never store a hydration bladder in direct sunlight. UV degrades the material and promotes algae growth if there's any residual moisture.

Prevention: The Real Strategy

Cleaning is fixing a problem. Prevention means not having the problem. Here's what I do now after learning the hard way:

  • Only put water in the bladder. Sports drinks, juice, anything with sugar — they feed mold and bacteria growth 10x faster than plain water. If you want electrolytes, use your water bottle. Keep the bladder water-only.
  • Drain it same day. When I get home from a hike or ride, the bladder gets drained and quick-rinsed before I take my boots off. Five minutes. Make it a habit.
  • Dry it every time. Paper towels inside, hang on a hook, done.
  • Deep clean monthly during active season. Takes 30 minutes including soak time. Set a reminder.
  • Replace the bite valve annually. It's the germiest part of the system and it's the cheapest to replace. New valve, fresh start.

When to Just Buy a New One

If you see black mold that won't come off with scrubbing. If there are cracks or peeling in the bladder material. If it smells even after thorough cleaning. If the tube is cloudy and discolored inside.

It's a $25-40 part. Your gut health is worth more than that. Sometimes the right answer is "throw it out and start over." No shame in it — I've replaced two bladders in 5 years because they were too far gone from neglect.

Just don't neglect the new one. Five minutes after each use. That's all it takes.

Seriously, Just Do the Five Minutes

I know this is the most boring article on the site. You'd rather read about power stations or bug out bags or overland builds. But here's reality — maintaining your gear is the unglamorous backbone of being prepared. A hydration bladder with mold in it is worse than no hydration bladder at all, because at least with nothing you'll bring water bottles.

Drain it. Rinse it. Dry it. Takes less time than brushing your teeth.

Your future self — the one drinking clean water from a trail source through a clean bladder while your buddy is gagging on whatever's growing in his — will thank you.
