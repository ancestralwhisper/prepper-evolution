Rice Is the King of Long-Term Food Storage

If I could only stockpile one food for long-term storage, it'd be white rice. No contest. And I say that not because I'm some rice evangelist — I say it because the math is overwhelming.

White rice stored properly lasts 25-30 years. It's about 1,600 calories per pound. It costs roughly $0.80 per pound in bulk. It pairs with literally anything. And my three kids will eat it without complaining, which — if you have kids — you know is the real test.

But here's where people mess up. They buy a 25 lb bag from Costco, throw it in the pantry, and call it "long-term food storage." That rice will last maybe 1-2 years before bugs, moisture, or rancidity gets to it. To actually hit that 25+ year shelf life, you need to do it right.

It's not complicated. It takes an afternoon and costs less than dinner at Applebee's. Let me walk you through it.

White Rice vs Brown Rice — This Matters

I need to get this out of the way because I see this mistake constantly.

White rice lasts 25-30 years when stored properly. The bran and germ have been removed during processing, which is what makes it shelf stable. No oils to go rancid. No moisture-rich layers for bugs to eat.

Brown rice lasts 6 months to maybe 1 year. The bran layer contains natural oils that go rancid relatively quickly no matter how you store it. Mylar bags and oxygen absorbers help, but you're still looking at 1-2 years max for brown rice. Maybe 3-5 if you freeze it first and then seal it perfectly.

I know brown rice is "healthier." But a 6-month shelf life isn't long-term storage. If you want the nutritional benefits of whole grains, store wheat berries instead — they last 30+ years and you grind them into flour when you're ready. But for rice? White rice. Period.

Also — don't store instant rice. It's pre-cooked and dried, which means it absorbs moisture faster and goes off sooner. Store regular long-grain white rice, jasmine rice, or basmati. They all store the same way.

The Three Enemies of Stored Rice

Your rice will fail if you don't defeat three things:

1. Oxygen

Oxygen allows aerobic bacteria to grow, causes oxidation that degrades nutrients, and — here's the big one — keeps insect eggs alive. Yeah. Most dry goods from the store already have insect eggs in them. Tiny, invisible, harmless... until those eggs hatch in your sealed container. Remove the oxygen and those eggs never hatch. Problem solved before it starts.

2. Moisture

Moisture content above 10% promotes mold and bacterial growth. Commercially packaged rice is usually around 12-14% moisture, which is fine for a pantry but not for 25-year storage. Mylar bags with oxygen absorbers reduce the moisture to safe levels by removing the oxygen that would otherwise interact with ambient moisture.

3. Light

UV light breaks down nutrients and can heat the contents of your container, creating condensation. Store your rice in opaque containers in a dark, cool location. A closet, basement shelf, or under-bed storage all work fine.

What You Need

Here's your shopping list. Total cost is roughly $30-50 depending on how much rice you're storing, which will cover enough supplies for 50-100+ lbs of rice:

  • White rice — buy in bulk from Costco, Sam's Club, or restaurant supply stores. 25 lb bags are usually the best price per pound.
  • 5-gallon mylar bags — get 7 mil or thicker. Thinner bags can get punctured by rice grains. You want bags sized to fit inside 5-gallon buckets.
  • 2000cc oxygen absorbers — one per 5-gallon bag. Buy them sealed and work fast when you open the package, because they start absorbing oxygen immediately from the air.
  • 5-gallon food-grade buckets with gamma seal lids — you can buy these at Lowe's, Home Depot, or get free ones from bakeries and restaurants (they get frosting and pickles in them). Make sure they're HDPE #2 food-grade plastic. The gamma seal lids snap on and then have a screw-off center that makes access easy later.
  • A clothes iron or flat iron — for sealing the mylar bags. A regular household iron on the highest setting works perfectly. Some people use a hair straightening iron and honestly that's easier.
  • A board or 2x4 — to seal the mylar against. You put the board under the opening of the bag and run the iron across it.

The Step-by-Step Process

This is so much simpler than people make it sound. I knocked out 100 lbs of rice in about 2 hours the first time, and I was being slow and careful.

Step 1: Set Up Your Station

Put the mylar bag inside the 5-gallon bucket. The bag should be bigger than the bucket — that extra mylar at the top is what you'll seal. Fold the edges of the bag over the rim of the bucket so it stays open while you pour.

Step 2: Pour the Rice

Fill the bag about 80-85% full. You need room at the top to seal it. For a 5-gallon bucket, that's roughly 33-36 lbs of white rice. Give or take.

Step 3: Drop in the Oxygen Absorber

Open your oxygen absorber package and work quickly. Drop one 2000cc absorber into the rice, pushing it down a few inches so it's surrounded by rice. If you're doing multiple bags at once, only open as many absorbers as you can use in 10-15 minutes. Seal the rest back in their original packaging.

Some people use two 500cc absorbers instead of one 2000cc. Same total absorption. Doesn't really matter.

Step 4: Push Out Air

Before sealing, push as much air out as you can. I press down on the rice gently to squeeze air up, then kind of hug the bag to force air out the top. You don't need to be perfect — the oxygen absorbers will handle the remaining oxygen. But the less air you start with, the faster and more completely the absorbers work.

Step 5: Seal the Mylar Bag

Lay the opening of the bag flat across your board or 2x4. Heat up your iron to the highest setting (cotton/linen). Run the iron slowly across the opening, applying firm pressure. Go about 2 inches from one edge, seal all the way across to about 2 inches from the other edge — leave a small gap.

Now squeeze out as much remaining air as you can through that gap. Then seal the gap. Done.

You want a solid seal — about 1/2 inch wide minimum. Run the iron over it twice to be sure. Give it a tug test when it cools — it should hold firm. If it peels apart, reseal it.

Step 6: Fold and Bucket

Fold the excess mylar down over the sealed rice and press the bag into the bucket. Put the gamma seal lid on. Label the bucket with the contents and date using a Sharpie.

Within a few hours, the oxygen absorbers will suck out the remaining oxygen and the mylar bag will shrink tight around the rice. If you tap on the bag the next day and it's firm like a vacuum-sealed brick, you know it worked. If it's still puffy and soft, you might have a leak in your seal or a bad absorber. Open it up and redo it.

Storage Location

Cool, dark, dry. That's the rule.

  • Temperature: Below 70F is ideal. Every 10 degrees cooler roughly doubles storage life. A 60F basement or closet interior is perfect. A hot garage in Texas? Your rice will degrade much faster.
  • Humidity: Low is better. If your storage area feels damp, use a dehumidifier or at minimum keep the buckets off the floor on pallets or shelves.
  • Light: Doesn't matter with opaque buckets and sealed mylar, but don't leave them in direct sunlight. It'll heat the contents.
  • Pests: Mice will chew through mylar bags if they can get to them. The 5-gallon bucket protects against rodents. Don't skip the bucket.

I keep mine on metal shelves in the basement of our NJ house. Stays around 60-65F year round. Some of it goes to the cabin in PA for the stash up there.

How Much Rice to Store

This depends on how many people you're feeding and for how long. A rough guideline:

  • 1 person, 1 year: ~300-400 lbs of rice (as a staple alongside other foods)
  • Family of 4, 3 months: ~150-200 lbs
  • Family of 4, 1 year: ~400-500 lbs

That sounds like a mountain of rice but it's only 8-12 five-gallon buckets for a year's supply for a family. They stack. It's very manageable space-wise.

Don't just store rice though. Rice alone will keep you alive but it's nutritionally incomplete. You need beans (together they form a complete protein), salt, cooking oil, multivitamins, and ideally a wider variety of stored foods. The Bay Leaf Myth and Other Bad Advice

People on the internet will tell you to put bay leaves in your rice to repel bugs. This is folk wisdom that doesn't actually work for long-term storage. Bay leaves might mildly deter some insects in an open pantry, but they don't kill insect eggs that are already in the rice. Oxygen absorbers do. That's the science. Skip the bay leaves.

Other things I've seen recommended that are unnecessary or counterproductive:

  • Diatomaceous earth mixed in rice: Not necessary if you're using oxygen absorbers. And now you have to wash DE off your rice before cooking. Why?
  • Freezing rice before sealing: Freezing does kill bugs and eggs. It's a good extra step if you want belt-and-suspenders. Freeze the rice for 72+ hours, let it come back to room temperature (so you don't get condensation in the bag), then seal. But oxygen absorbers alone are sufficient.
  • Dry ice in buckets: The old-school method. It works — the CO2 from dry ice displaces oxygen. But it's harder to source, harder to handle, and oxygen absorbers are cheaper and simpler. I don't bother with dry ice anymore.
  • Vacuum sealing without mylar: Vacuum seal bags alone won't cut it for long-term storage. They're not light-proof, they're not rodent-proof, and the seals can fail over years. Mylar + oxygen absorber + bucket is the standard for a reason.

Rotation and Inspection

If you store rice properly, you don't need to rotate it for decades. That's the whole point. But I still recommend checking your buckets once a year — look for chew marks from mice, check that lids are secure, make sure nothing is leaking.

When you do eventually open a bucket, the rice should smell neutral. No musty, rancid, or off smells. If it looks and smells fine, it's fine. Cook a small batch as a test if you're unsure.

The rice you're eating day-to-day should come from your pantry supply, not your long-term storage. Keep those buckets sealed until you need them.

But What About Cooking?

Storing rice is great. But if the power is out, how are you cooking it? Rice needs boiling water and 15-20 minutes of heat. You need either a camp stove, a rocket stove, a charcoal grill, or a wood fire. Don't forget this part of the plan.

Also — rice takes about 2 cups of water per cup of rice. If water is limited in your emergency, that water cost matters. Factor it into your water storage calculations.

Total Cost Breakdown

For 100 lbs of rice stored in 5-gallon buckets with mylar and oxygen absorbers:

  • 100 lbs white rice (Costco/Sam's): ~$40-50
  • 3x 5-gallon mylar bags: ~$8-12
  • 3x 2000cc oxygen absorbers: ~$8-10
  • 3x 5-gallon food-grade buckets with lids: ~$15-20 (or free from bakeries)

Total: roughly $70-90 for 160,000 calories of food that'll last a quarter century. That's about 5 cents per meal's worth of rice. You literally cannot beat that price for long-term food security.

Compare that to freeze-dried meals at $8-15 per pouch. Long-term food storage doesn't have to be expensive. It just has to be done right.

Just Do It

I think people put this off because it feels like a big project. It's not. Buy the rice, buy the mylar bags and absorbers, grab some buckets, and spend a Saturday afternoon packing it up. Put on a podcast. Have a beer. Make the kids help — they think the mylar bags are shiny space stuff and the heat sealing is exciting.

An afternoon. Fifty bucks. Twenty-five years of food security. That's about the best return on investment you'll ever get in prepping.
