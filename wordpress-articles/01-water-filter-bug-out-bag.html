Most People Get This Wrong

Here's what's up. You can survive three days without water at most in perfect text book conditions. But you're not building a bug out bag for perfect conditions are you? No, you're building it for the worst day of your life.

And yet I see people throw a $10 LifeStraw in their bag and call it done “prepared”.

That is not a water plan, that's just a hopium plan and hope is a terrible strategy when your family of five is hiking out of a situation and the only water source is a muddy creek off The West Rim trail.

I've tested four of the most popular water filtration options out there. Not in a lab, but on actual trails, at the cabin in PA, and working in the woods on mutual aid work. We have bottled water at work, but I still just wanted to see how some of these filters worked and the opportunity presented itself. So let me break down what actually works, what's overhyped, and what belongs in your bag right now.

The Four Contenders

We're looking at the Sawyer Squeeze, the LifeStraw Personal, the Katadyn BeFree, and Aquamira chlorine dioxide drops. Each one handles water treatment differently, and honestly? They all have a place, but ust not all in a bug out bag.

But before we get into the weeds, if you haven't figured out what else goes in your bag, take a stroll over to the Bug Out Bag Calculator first. Water filtration is one piece. You need the whole pie.

Sawyer Squeeze: The One I Actually Carry

I'll say it straight away. The Sawyer Squeeze is the best water filter for a bug out bag, period. And I don't say that because it's the most expensive or the fanciest I say it because it works every single time and it's stupid simple.

0.1 micron of absolute filtration. That means bacteria, protozoa, microplastics are eliminated. It'll filter 100,000 gallons before you need to replace it, which is basically never for a normal human being. The thing weighs 3 ounces. THREE.

What makes it really special is the flexibility. With it you can:

  • Screw it onto a standard water bottle (like a Smartwater bottle with a 28mm thread, the 1L ones thread right on)

  • Use it inline with a hydration bladder

  • Gravity feed it from a tree for camp filtration

  • Squeeze it through the included pouches

That last option is the default setup and it works great until the pouches fail. And yes at some point they will fail. The seams on Sawyer's included pouches are garbage and I've had two blow out on me. So here's what you do: buy a couple Smartwater 1L bottles and use those instead. Costs you $2 at a gas station and that solves your problem forever.

Flow rate is solid, but it's not the fastest thing in the world. You can fill a liter in about 30 seconds of squeezing, which is perfectly fine when you're thirsty. Good enough when you've got three kids asking "are we there yet" while you're trying to filter creek water.

The Catch

Backflushing. You're going to have to backflush the Sawyer regularly or the flow rate drops to a trickle. They do include a backflush syringe, so don't lose it. I keep mine rubber-banded to the filter in a quart ziplock bag. Some people forget to backflush and then complain the filter is slow. That's just operator error, not the filter.

Also, this matters in a bug out scenario. The Sawyer can't handle freezing temperatures, if the filter element freezes, the hollow fiber membranes can crack and you won't even know it. You should sleep with this thing in your sleeping bag, otherwise suffer the consequences of the invisible Winter cold.

LifeStraw: The Famous One That's Actually Limited

The LifeStraw has an incredible marketing team. They get it out there to the point where everyone knows about it. Your mom probably knows about it. It's the filter that shows up in every "survival kit" list on the internet.

It does one job and does the job well. It removes bacteria and parasites down to 0.2 microns. Newer Peak Series models even remove microplastics. It's inexpensive, lightweight, and basically indestructible.

But here's the one thing nobody talks about.

You have to drink directly through it like you would with a straw. Knelt down next to a creek on your hands and knees or you're dunking it in a bottle and sucking through it like a juice box.

You can't collect filtered water to carry with you and you can't gravity feed it or fill your kids' bottles. You can't use it to cook. Every sip requires your mouth on the tube, sucking water through it.

Is it good for a day hike? Yeah. For an actual bug out situation where you need to collect water for a family, keep moving, and maybe boil some for freeze-dried meals? It's a bottleneck. Literally.

I keep one in my glove box as a backup. The 2025 Sierra has plenty of room. But it's not my primary filter and it shouldn't be yours either.

Katadyn BeFree: The Ultralight Option

The Katadyn BeFree 1.0L is what the ultralight backpacking crowd swears by. 2 ounces. Insanely fast flow rate and the fastest of any filter I've tested. The EZ-Clean membrane just needs a quick shake to clean. No backflushing syringe to be misplaced or lost.

It's just a really good filter. I almost switched to it.

Almost.

There are two problems that killed it for me. First, the soft flask it comes with feels fragile. Not the "it'll break in a month" fragile, but "I don't trust this in a SHTF scenario" fragile. The threading is proprietary to Katadyn, so you can't just swap on a Smartwater bottle like you can with the Sawyer. You're locked into Katadyn's ecosystem.

Second reason and this is the big one - longevity. The BeFree is rated for 1,000 liters. The Sawyer Squeeze? 100,000 gallons. That's not my typo. The BeFree is a great backpacking filter that you'll replace every season or two and the Sawyer is a survival filter that you might never replace.

If your going to be using it for weekend tripos, the BeFree all day. For a bug out bag that might sit for months or years before you need it the Sawyer wins hands down.

Aquamira Drops: The Backup Nobody Carries (But Should)

Here's where it gets pretty interesting. Aquamira chlorine dioxide drops aren't a filter at all. They're a chemical water treatment in two little bottles (Part A and Part B) that you mix together and add to water.

The combination of these drops kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa including giardia and cryptosporidium. You know, the stuff that turns a great day into a day where you can't get off the toilet kind of day. Wait 15 minutes for bacteria, 30 minutes for crypto. It tastes slightly like pool water but nothing terrible and can be tolerated.

Why do I love these as a backup?

  • They weigh almost nothing, maybe 3 oz for both bottles

  • They handle VIRUSES, which no filter on this list does (filters remove bacteria and protozoa, not viruses)

  • They can't freeze-damage like a hollow fiber filter

  • Five-year shelf life stable

  • One set treats about 30 gallons

The downside is obviously you have to wait fifteen to thirty minutes before you can drink. If you're severely dehydrated that wait feels like forever and it still doesn't remove sediment, chemicals, or heavy metals. Muddy water treated with Aquamira is still muddy water. Just... clean muddy water.

My setup: Sawyer Squeeze as primary, Aquamira drops as backup. Total weight under 6 oz and a total cost under $50. That covers filtration AND purification.

So What Goes in the Bag?

My actual bug out bag water setup and the one that's sitting by my front door right now:

  • Sawyer Squeeze filter (primary)

  • Two 1L Smartwater bottles (they're the squeeze bottles, perfect thread match)

  • Aquamira drops (backup + virus protection)

  • 32 oz Nalgene (for treated water storage)

Total weight: maybe 12 oz empty and under a pound. That's your entire water system. Simple and Easy.

Some people ask about UV purifiers like the SteriPen, which works, but they need batteries, they're fragile, and if the glass tube breaks you're done. I'm not betting my family's hydration on a glass tube and AA batteries. It's just more things that can go wrong and break added to the equation.

You can always boil water as an option if you have fuel and time. But you won't always have either. A filter and chemical backup means you can drink NOW and worry about camp later. When prepping I like to keep things simple and as efficient as possible.

Common Mistakes I See

People overthink the filter and underthink the container. It really doesn't matter how good your filter is if you have nothing to put clean water in. Pack a collapsible water container or at minimum a couple Smartwater bottles. If space is an issue you can always get a collapsible water bottle like the CNOC Vesica 1L or the Evernew collapsible bottles.

People also buy a filter and never test it, take it out or use it. Filter some creek water and try it out. Make sure you know how to backflush it, how fast it flows, how the threading works. The middle of an emergency is not the time to read instructions and figure these things out.

And for the love of everything, check your filter before you need it. Sawyer recommends storing it slightly damp in the fridge, but I just make sure to run some water through mine every few months and that literally takes two minutes.

Bottom Line

Get a Sawyer Squeeze and some Aquamira drops and practice using both. Put them in your bag and move on to the next item on your list ✅

Water filtration is a solved problem, don't overspend or overcomplicate it. Just make sure you have it, because a $30 filter and a $12 bottle of drops is the difference between drinking safely and getting giardia in the worst possible situation.

And trust me I've seen what happens when people don't have clean drinking water. It's not hypothetical. It's ugly. Spend the forty bucks and get peace of mind.
