They're Not the Same Bag

I can't tell you how many times I've seen someone say "my bug out bag is in my truck" like that covers everything. It doesn't. A bug out bag and a get home bag serve completely different purposes, and if you're only carrying one, you've got a gap in your plan.

Short version: a get home bag gets you from wherever you are back to your house. A bug out bag gets you from your house to somewhere else. Two different directions. Two different scenarios. Two different bags.

I carry both. One lives in the 2025 Sierra. One lives by my front door. And honestly, building both cost me less than a set of tires.

The Get Home Bag: Your "I'm Not Home" Insurance

Think about where you spend most of your time that isn't your house. Work. The grocery store. Your kid's school. The job site. Now imagine something goes sideways — power grid failure, major storm, civil unrest, whatever — and you need to get home. Right now. Maybe your car works, maybe it doesn't. Maybe the roads are jammed. Maybe you're on foot.

That's what a get home bag is for.

When I'm doing mutual aid electrical work — could be California, Texas, Florida, Maine, wherever the storms hit — I'm sometimes hundreds of miles from home. But even on a normal day, my commute puts me 30-40 miles from my front door. If something happened and I had to walk that? We're talking 10-15 hours of steady walking, maybe more depending on terrain and conditions.

The get home bag is built for that window. Usually 12-24 hours. Get home, link up with your family, then figure out the next move from there.

What Goes In It

A GHB is lighter and simpler than a bug out bag. You're not planning to live out of it for days. You're planning to move fast and get home. Here's what's in mine — the one that lives behind the driver's seat of the Sierra:

  • Good walking shoes (I keep a pair of broken-in trail runners — you might be in work boots or dress shoes when things go sideways)
  • Water: two 16 oz water bottles + a Sawyer Squeeze filter
  • Food: 4 protein bars, 2 peanut butter packets, 1 Datrex emergency bar
  • Headlamp + spare batteries
  • Rain poncho (weighs nothing, changes everything in a storm)
  • Basic first aid kit — band-aids, gauze, ibuprofen, moleskin for blisters
  • Phone charger + small power bank
  • Local road map (yes, paper — your phone might be dead or jammed)
  • Cash — $200 in small bills
  • Multi-tool
  • Seasonal add: hat/gloves in winter, sunscreen in summer

Total weight is maybe 10-12 lbs. It fits in a regular backpack — nothing tacticool, nothing that screams "I'm a prepper." Just a normal-looking bag. Grey man all the way.

The walking shoes thing is underrated. I've watched guys try to hike 5 miles in steel-toe work boots during storm deployments. Their feet were destroyed. If you work in shoes you can't comfortably walk 10+ miles in, stash a pair of sneakers.

The Bug Out Bag: Your "We're Leaving Home" Kit

The bug out bag is a different animal. This is for when staying home isn't safe anymore. House fire. Flood. Chemical spill. Whatever forces you to grab a bag and go — on foot if necessary — to a predetermined location.

The BOB is bigger, heavier, and more complete. It needs to sustain you for 72 hours minimum, though I'd argue you should plan for longer. Way too many real disasters have lasted more than three days — I've personally worked storm recovery ops where people were displaced for weeks.

The Bug Out Bag Calculator on our site breaks down exactly what you need based on your family size and situation. But the core categories are:

  • Shelter (tarp, emergency bivvy, or lightweight tent)
  • Water (filter + chemical backup + containers — I covered this in the water filter article)
  • Food (3+ days of calorie-dense, lightweight food)
  • Fire (lighter, ferro rod, tinder)
  • First aid (more comprehensive than the GHB kit)
  • Navigation (compass, maps, GPS if you trust batteries)
  • Clothing (full change + weather appropriate layers)
  • Communications (radio, whistle, signal mirror)
  • Documents (copies of IDs, insurance, medical info in a waterproof bag)
  • Tools (knife, multi-tool, cordage, duct tape)

My BOB weighs about 30-35 lbs loaded. My wife's is about 25 lbs. The kids share a lighter setup between the two oldest. This isn't a "live in the woods for a month" bag — it's a "get to safety with what we need" bag.

Do You Actually Need Both?

Yes. And here's why.

If you only have a bug out bag at home and something happens while you're at work, you have nothing. You're improvising your way home with whatever's in your pockets. And if you only have a get home bag in your car and you need to evacuate your house? You're going to wish you had a bag with shelter, real food, and proper gear.

These two bags overlap in some areas — water filtration, basic first aid, fire — and that's fine. Some redundancy isn't waste, it's insurance. If you somehow end up with both bags (maybe you made it home and now need to leave), great. You've got double the supplies.

Here's what I'd actually prioritize if you're starting from zero:

Build the bug out bag first. It covers more scenarios for longer. Then build the get home bag — which is really just a stripped-down version with an emphasis on mobility.

Where They Live Matters

Get home bag: in your vehicle. Always. Mine sits behind the driver's seat in the Sierra. Some people keep theirs in the trunk. Some keep a smaller version at their desk at work. The point is it's where YOU are.

If you take public transit, this gets trickier. You probably want a smaller, lighter GHB you can carry daily — more like an EDC bag with some extra supplies. A 25L backpack with water, food, a rain layer, and comfortable shoes can cover a lot of scenarios without looking out of place on a train.

Bug out bag: at home, somewhere accessible. Not buried in the back of a closet under boxes. By the front door, in a coat closet, in the garage by the car — wherever you can grab it in 60 seconds and go. My family has practiced this. We can be out the door with bags, kids, and the dog in under three minutes. It's not graceful but it works.

The Vehicle as a Bridge

Here's something people miss. If you're bugging out from home, you're probably driving first and walking second (if at all). So your vehicle — in my case the Sierra — becomes part of the system. I keep extra water, a full-size first aid kit, a blanket, jumper cables, a tow strap, and tools in the truck separate from either bag.

Think of it as three tiers:

  • Tier 1: Get home bag (on your person or in your vehicle)
  • Tier 2: Bug out bag (at home, grab and go)
  • Tier 3: Vehicle supplies (supplements either bag when you're driving)

The vehicle stuff is great until it's not — if roads are blocked or your car is disabled, you're on foot with just your bag. So don't use the truck as an excuse to underbuild your actual bag. The bag needs to be self-sufficient.

A Quick Word on "Bugging In"

Honestly? For most realistic scenarios in NJ — storms, power outages, supply disruptions — you're better off staying home. Bugging in. Your house has way more resources than any bag you can carry. The bug out bag is for when staying home isn't an option. Fire, flood, chemical leak, mandatory evacuation.

That's a whole different conversation though. If you're building out your home supplies, check the Food Storage Calculator and Water Storage Calculator for figuring out how much to stockpile.

Common Mistakes

Building a 60-lb bug out bag you can't actually carry. I've seen this so many times. Guys loading up MOLLE packs with everything they own. You need to be able to wear this bag and walk 10 miles with it. If you can't, start cutting weight. Seriously.

Making the get home bag too small. A drawstring gym bag with a water bottle and a granola bar isn't a get home bag. It's a snack. If you might have to walk 10+ miles in bad conditions, plan for that.

Ignoring seasonal changes. A get home bag that works in July won't work in January in New Jersey. I swap out warm-weather and cold-weather layers twice a year.

Never practicing with either bag. Put them on. Walk somewhere. See how they feel. See what you forgot. See what you packed that you don't actually need. You'll learn more from one 5-mile walk with your bag than from reading a hundred articles.

Including mine.

Start Today

If you have nothing right now, do this: grab a regular backpack, throw in a water bottle, some protein bars, a rain poncho, a flashlight, comfortable shoes, and $100 cash. Put it in your car. Congratulations — you have a basic get home bag. It's not perfect but it's infinitely better than nothing.

Then go build your bug out bag properly with the calculator. Take your time on that one. But the GHB? That's a 20-minute project. Do it tonight.
