How I Mounted Starlink on My Overlanding Rig Without Hardwiring Anything

I'm going to say something that'll make the hardcore overlanding guys cringe: I put internet on my truck.

Not the garbage OnStar hotspot that barely loads a weather app. Real internet. Starlink-on-the-roof, streaming-movies-at-camp, kids-can-do-homework-in-the-tent kind of internet. And I did it without drilling a single hole, without hardwiring anything, and without permanently modifying my 2025 GMC Sierra 1500.

Look, I get it. Part of going out there is disconnecting. But I've got three kids, a wife who wants to know the weather's not about to turn ugly, and — honestly — after 16-hour days doing mutual aid electrical work in hurricane zones, I've learned that communication isn't a luxury. It's the thing that keeps you from making stupid decisions.

So here's the full build. Every part, every step, every dollar. The stuff that worked and the stuff I'm still figuring out.

Why Starlink on an Overlanding Rig

When GMC sells you a Sierra, they push OnStar pretty hard. $36 a month for their built-in wifi hotspot. And it's... fine. For checking email in a Walmart parking lot. But take that truck 30 miles outside of cell coverage — which is kind of the whole point of overlanding — and OnStar drops to nothing. Or worse, shows one bar and just spins. Forever.

I was paying $36 a month for something I couldn't use where I actually needed it.

Starlink is $50 a month. Fourteen bucks more. But it works everywhere. Well, almost everywhere — you need open sky, which on a roof rack you've got plenty of. And the Starlink wifi network shows up on the Sierra's infotainment system. So now my truck's big screen has real internet. Google Maps with traffic. Spotify without pre-downloading. OTA updates that don't take three days.

But the bigger thing? When I've got the OVS Bushveld roof top tent popped and the kids are on tablets while my wife and I sit by the fire — everybody's got wifi. At camp. In the middle of nowhere. That's worth $50 a month.

And when I'm home? I can use it as backup internet in the house. Or leave it on the truck in the driveway. Versatile in a way OnStar will never be.

The Setup Overview

Before I get into the step-by-step, here's the high-level picture so you know where this is going:

  • Dish: Starlink Standard (not the Mini — bigger antenna means better signal, and I had the roof space for it)
  • Location: Bolted to the roof rack crossbars
  • Cable route: Along rack → wire hider zip-tied to crossbar → rear window weather stripping → through cab-to-bed gap → grommet → into bed
  • Power & router: Mounted on a MOLLE panel in the truck bed using a house-style Starlink mount
  • Power source: Bed outlet while driving, EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra at camp
  • Hardwiring: None. Zero. Completely plug-and-play and removable.

That last part was non-negotiable for me. I've got arthritis in my right foot that makes crawling under dashboards and pulling wire through firewall grommets genuinely miserable. And honestly? I didn't want to void anything on a brand new truck. So everything here is bolt-on, plug-in, and reversible.

Roof Rack Install — RealTruck Ceros Low Profile

You need somewhere to put the dish. I went with the RealTruck Ceros Low Profile roof rack. Built-in wind fairing, sits low enough that it doesn't turn my Sierra into a billboard, and the crossbars give you solid mounting points for the Starlink, recovery gear, whatever else you want up there.

Install was straightforward — kit comes with everything and the instructions are decent. Took me about two hours alone, which would've been 90 minutes if my foot wasn't acting up. Climbing in and out of the bed, standing on a step stool, torquing bolts overhead. Not exactly arthritis-friendly. But it's a one-time deal.

The fairing matters more than people think. Without it, you get a constant low whistle at highway speeds that'll drive you insane on a 6-hour drive to the PA cabin. With the Ceros fairing, basically silent up to about 75.

And the low profile thing is real — I can still fit in my garage. Barely. But I can.

Mounting the Starlink Dish

The Starlink Standard comes with a kickstand mount for flat surfaces — your roof, a table, the ground. Some guys just set the dish on the rack when they get to camp.

I didn't want that. I wanted it bolted down and road-ready at all times. Set it and forget it.

The stock Starlink mount hardware actually works well for this. I bolted it to the rack crossbars with stainless hardware and it's solid. No wobble at highway speeds, no vibration noise, nothing. The dish sits flat and low on the rack — it almost looks like it belongs there.

But here's the thing about a white satellite dish on a dark truck. It looks... medical. Like you're driving an ambulance or something. So I grabbed the Starlink protective cover in black and the whole vibe changed. Now it just looks like part of the rack setup. People don't even notice it.

The cover also protects the dish face from tree branches, road debris, and whatever else. It doesn't affect signal at all — it's designed for permanent outdoor installation. Worth every penny just for the looks alone.

Cable Routing — The Clever Part

This is where most Starlink truck installs get ugly. Cable needs to get from the roof into the cab or bed, and most guys either drill a hole (no thanks) or run it through a cracked window (looks terrible, leaks).

I went a different route. Literally.

Cable runs along the roof rack rail inside a cable wire hider channel zip-tied to the rack. From there it drops down at the rear of the cab and tucks under the rear window weather stripping.

That weather stripping trick is the move. The rubber seal along the top of the rear window has just enough give to press the flat Starlink cable underneath it. From outside, you can barely see it. It's not pinching the cable, it's not breaking the weather seal in any meaningful way, and it looks factory.

From the weather stripping, the cable drops down between the cab and bed — that gap every truck has — and goes through a grommet into the bed. I'm still finalizing the grommet routing, honestly. It works but it's not as clean as I want it yet. I'll update this article when I've got the final solution dialed in.

But even now? You have to know where to look to spot the cable. This is a daily driver that goes overlanding, not a dedicated expedition vehicle. I don't want it looking like a mobile command center.

MOLLE Panels & Swing Case Integration

Once the cable's in the bed, it needs somewhere to go. And this is where the MOLLE panels come in.

I installed MOLLE panels on both bed sidewalls. These things are surprisingly good for the price. Solid steel, powder coated, and they use the existing stake pocket holes for mounting — so again, no drilling. The install took maybe 20 minutes per side even with me moving slow because of the foot.

Now here's where it got interesting. I already had a RealTruck swing case mounted on the driver side — that's my tool and recovery gear storage. The swing case has a locking latch that mounts to the bed wall, and the MOLLE panel sits right over that area.

So I had to cut a hole.

About 7 inches by 3 inches, right where the swing case latch catches. Grabbed the multitool with a metal cutting blade and went at it. Not pretty, but functional. The edges are clean enough and you can't see them when the panel's mounted because the swing case covers that section.

And here's a bonus I didn't plan for — with the swing case closed against the MOLLE panel, you can still mount small pouches and accessories on the rest of the panel above and below the case. So you don't lose that storage surface just because the swing case is there. More real estate, not less.

Power Adapter & Router Mount — The Money Shot

This is the part I'm most proud of. And it's the simplest part of the whole build.

The Starlink Standard has two pieces that need to live in your vehicle: the power adapter and wifi router. Most people toss them in a bin or velcro them to something. I wanted them mounted clean and secure.

Found this house-style Starlink mount bracket designed for mounting the power adapter and router to a wall. Snaps both units into place. Reviews showed people already using them in RVs and truck builds, so I grabbed one and mounted it to the MOLLE panel on the passenger side bed wall.

Looks factory. The bracket bolts to the MOLLE webbing, power adapter and router snap in, everything's tidy. No loose cables flopping around when you take a turn.

And the power situation is dead simple. Two scenarios:

While driving: The power adapter plugs into the 120V outlet in the truck bed. The Sierra has one from the factory. Truck's running, Starlink's running. Internet on the highway.

At camp: Truck's off, bed outlet's dead. Unplug and plug into my EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra. The dish only pulls 50-75 watts average — DELTA 3 Ultra runs it for days. With solar feeding the EcoFlow during the day, Starlink is basically free power at camp.

No inverter. No fuse tap. No 10-gauge wire through the firewall. Just a plug.

And if I ever want to pull the whole system out — cabin in PA, lend it, sell the truck — I unbolt the dish, pull the cable, unsnap the bracket, gone. Try that with a hardwired setup.

The OnStar vs Starlink Math

Let's talk money because I know someone's going to bring it up.

OnStar Hotspot Starlink Standard
Monthly cost $36/mo $50/mo
Hardware cost Included with truck ~$299 (dish + router)
Works off-grid Only with cell signal Yes — satellite direct
Speed 5-15 Mbps (when it works) 50-200 Mbps typical
Camp coverage Barely reaches outside cab Whole camp site
Infotainment compatible Built in Yes — connects via wifi
Use at home/cabin No Yes
Portable/removable No Yes

The difference is $14 a month. That's two gas station coffees. For satellite internet that works in places where your phone shows "No Service" and OnStar shows nothing at all.

I'm not anti-OnStar. It works fine within cell range. But I bought a truck to go places that aren't within cell range. At a dispersed site in the Poconos or running a forest road in West Virginia, OnStar is a $36 paperweight. Starlink doesn't care where you are as long as it can see sky.

If you're only doing established campgrounds with cell service, save your money. But if you're going where we go — especially with a family — the extra $14 isn't even a question. Check Starlink availability for your area here.

What's Next

This build is about 90% done. Here's what I'm still working on:

  • Grommet routing: The cable path from the cab-bed gap into the bed needs to be finalized. It works but it's not clean enough for me yet. I want a proper waterproof grommet so I'm not thinking about it every time it rains.
  • Speed testing: I want real numbers from real locations. Highway speeds while driving, camp speeds at various sites, performance in heavy tree cover vs. open meadow. I'll add a speed test section to this article once I've got enough data.
  • Cold weather performance: Haven't had it through a real cold snap yet. The dish has a built-in heater for snow melt, which draws more power. Need to see what that does to my EcoFlow consumption on a winter camping trip.
  • Long-term durability: Bugs, road grime, tree sap, UV exposure — how does the dish hold up after 6 months on the roof? A year? Will report back.

If you're building something similar, use the vehicle profile tool to check your payload with all this added weight. Maybe 60-70 pounds total for the Starlink stuff and MOLLE panels on top of rack weight — but if you're already running a roof top tent and loaded bed, every pound matters.

Worried about aero drag? The fuel and range planner can show you the impact. Short answer: the Ceros fairing does most of the work and the dish is flat enough that it's not a meaningful drag adder.

Bottom Line

I sold my Jeep Rubicon during COVID and went without a real overlanding rig for years. Honda Accord, then a Canyon with problems, now this Sierra. Building this truck back up the right way — piece by piece — has been one of the better things I've done for my head.

The Starlink addition changed how my family uses the truck. My wife went from tolerating overnight trips to actually suggesting them. Kids have wifi, she can check in with her parents, I can pull up weather radar when the sky looks sketchy. Nobody's cut off.

Is it "cheating" at overlanding? Some guys on the forums think so. But those guys don't have three kids and they probably weren't running generators in 100-degree heat after Hurricane Helene so people could keep their insulin cold. Connectivity saves lives. Having it available doesn't mean you have to use it — but when you need it, you need it.

Total hardware cost — dish, cover, mount bracket, wire hider — under $400 plus $50 a month. Works at the PA cabin, dispersed sites in the Pine Barrens, on the highway, in my driveway. Minus the $36 OnStar hotspot, the real monthly cost is $14.

That's the easiest mod decision I've made on this build.
