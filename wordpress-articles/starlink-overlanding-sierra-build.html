How I Mounted Starlink on My Overlanding Rig Without Hardwiring Anything

I'm going to say something that'll make the hardcore overlanding guys cringe: I put internet on my truck. Oh, did I hit a nerve?

Not the garbage OnStar hotspot that barely loads a weather app. Real internet. Starlink on the roof. The kids can stream movies or play with their Nintendo Switch or their other handhelds. And I did it without drilling a single hole, without hardwiring anything, and without permanently modifying my 2025 GMC Sierra 1500. Well, I lied. I did drill two holes in the starlink power adapter and wifi mount, but I'll touch on that a bit later.

I am totally for getting out there and being disconnected. In fact, I don't think there is anybody that loves that more than me. Going adventuring is all about disconnecting. But I've got three kids, a wife who wants to know the weather's not about to turn ugly, and honestly after 16-hour days at work, I've learned that communication isn't a luxury. It's the thing that keeps you from making stupid decisions.

So here's the full build. Every part, every step, every dollar. The stuff that worked and the stuff I'm still figuring out.

Why Starlink on an Overlanding Rig

When GMC sells you a Sierra, they push OnStar pretty hard (and that dang warranty) and be sure to give you 3 months free. It costs $36 a month for their built-in wifi hotspot, which is just okay for checking email in a Walmart parking lot. But take that truck 30 miles outside of cell coverage, which is kind of the whole point of overlanding and OnStar gets slower than it is already.

I was paying $36 a month for something I couldn't use where I actually needed it.

So, I cancelled OnStar and got Starlink which is $50 a month. Fourteen bucks more. But it works everywhere. Well, almost everywhere - you need an open sky, which on a roof rack you've got plenty of. And I just had to connect my infotainment system to the Starlink wifi. There is one caveat to this. I can't connect my phone to the infotainment system simultaneously to use Apple carplay while connected to the Starlink with my phone. I had to plug my phone into the usb on my dash in order for that to connect all together. I'll be messing around with that a little more over the next few days though.

But the bigger thing? When I've got the OVS Bushveld roof top tent popped and the kids are on tablets while my wife and I sit by the fire and everybody's got wifi. At camp, in the middle of nowhere. That's totally worth it.

And when I'm at home and not actually working a storm? I can use it as backup internet in the house. Or leave it on the truck in the driveway. Much more versatile in a way OnStar will never be.

Roof Rack Install - RealTruck Ceros Low Profile

I needed somewhere to put the dish. I went with the RealTruck Ceros Low Profile roof rack. Built-in wind fairing, sits low enough that it doesn't turn my Sierra into a billboard, and the crossbars give me solid mounting points for the Starlink, a rugged storage case I can store my Devos LightRanger 4000 in and some chairs. I might even mount two storage cases up there so I have one for recovery gear.

The install was straightforward, the kit comes with everything and the instructions are were okay. Took me about four hours alone, which would've been 60 minutes if my foot wasn't acting up. Climbing in and out of the bed, standing on a step stool, torquing bolts overhead. Not exactly arthritis-friendly. But it's a one-time deal.

The fairing matters more than people think. Without it, you get a constant low whistle at highway speeds that'll drive you insane on a 6-hour drive to the PA cabin. With the Ceros fairing, basically silent up to about 55mph. I'm still experimenting with it and take back roads to work so I barely get over 55mph.

Mounting the Starlink Dish

The Starlink Standard comes with a kickstand mount for flat surfaces for your roof, a table, or the ground. Some people just set the dish on the rack when they get to camp if they like to pack up each trip.

I didn't want that. I wanted it bolted down and road-ready at all times in a semi-permanent location. Set it and forget it.

The Starlink roof rack mount hardware actually works perfect for this. I bolted it to the rack crossbars with stainless hardware and it's solid. It doesn't wobble, there is no noise or vibration. The dish sits slightly angled up at an 8 degree angle and low on the rack to the point it almost looks like it belongs there.

But here's the thing about a white satellite dish on a dark truck. It stand out like a sore thumb. Like you're driving an ambulance or something. So I grabbed the Starlink protective cover in black and the whole vibe changed. Now it just looks like part of the rack setup. People don't even notice it. Out of sight, out of mind.

The cover also protects the dish face from hail, tree branches, road debris, and whatever else. It doesn't affect signal at all and it's designed for permanent outdoor installation. The silicone cover doesn't effect the satellite signals at all so for it to blend in with my truck is worth every penny.

Cable Routing - The Clever Part

This is where most Starlink truck installs get ugly. The cable needs to get from the roof into the cab or bed, and most people either drill a hole (no thank you) or run it through a third brake light (potential leaks).

I literally took this a different approach, especially if I need to disconnect it and move it.

The cable runs along the roof rack rail zip tied to tiny little slits that were already on the rack. When I got to the back of the cab I ran it down between the cab and the bed, into and along the bed and behind the molle panel to the mounting location.

That weather stripping trick is the move. The rubber seal along the top of the rear window has just enough give to press the round Starlink cable into it. From outside, you can barely see it. It's not pinching the cable, it's not breaking the weather seal in any meaningful way, and it looks factory.

From the weather stripping, the cable drops down between the cab and bed between that gap every truck has and goes through a grommet into the bed. I'm still finalizing the grommet routing, honestly. It works but it's not as clean as I want it yet. I'll update this article when I've got the final solution dialed in. I might get a really sticky wire hider and mount it to the underside of the tonnaue cover so its up and out of the way and looks cleaner.

But as of now you would have to know where to look to spot the cable. This is a daily driver that goes overlanding, not a dedicated expedition vehicle. I don't want it looking like a mobile command center. That middle grommet that you see and the one to the right of it is where I drilled two vertical holes in each and zip tied the wire to it just to take some of the pressure off the grommet on the left.

MOLLE Panels & Swing Case Integration

Once the cable's in the bed, it needs somewhere to go and this is where the MOLLE panels come in.

I installed MOLLE panels on both bed sidewalls. These things are surprisingly good for the price. Solid steel, powder coated, and they use the existing stake pocket holes for mounting â€” so again, no drilling. The install took maybe 20 minutes per side even with me moving slow because of the foot.

Now here's where it got interesting. I already had a RealTruck swing case mounted on the driver side - that's my tool and recovery gear storage. The swing case has a locking latch that mounts to the bed wall, and the MOLLE panel sits right over that area.

So I had to use a multi tool and cut a few spots on the molle panel to get it to fit.

About 7 inches by 3 inches, right where the swing case latch catches. Grabbed the multitool with a metal cutting blade and went at it. Not pretty, but functional. The edges are clean enough and you can't see them when the panel's mounted because the swing case covers that section.

And here's the bonus, with the swing case closed you can still mount small pouches and accessories on the rest of the panel directly behind the case. So you don't lose that storage surface just because the swing case is there. More real estate, not less.

Power Adapter & Router Mount â€” The Money Shot

This is the part I'm most proud of. And it's the simplest part of the whole build.

The Starlink Standard has two pieces that need to live in your vehicle: the power adapter and wifi router. Most people toss them in a bin or velcro them to something. I wanted them mounted clean and secure.

I was originally looking around the net for a 3d printed Starlink molle mount, but instead I found this house-style Starlink mount bracket designed for mounting the power adapter and router to a wall. It snaps both units into place and keeps them with a little security lip. Reviews showed people already using them in RVs, so I grabbed one and mounted it to the MOLLE panel on the passenger side bed wall.

Looks factory. The bracket bolts to the MOLLE webbing, power adapter and router snap in, everything's tidy and there are no loose cables flopping around when you take a turn. I'm going to mount some other gear to the molly, so eventually I'll wind up cleaning the wires a bit more. I have a small molly pouch I can mount there to tuck them in.

And the power situation is dead simple. Two scenarios:

While driving: The power adapter plugs into the 120V outlet in the truck bed. My Sierra has one from the factory and the Starlink plugged into it. When the truck is running, the Starlink's running. Internet on the highway.

At camp: When the truck is off and the bed outlet's dead. Just unplug it from the truck outlet and plug into my EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra. The dish only pulls 50-75 watts average and the DELTA 3 Ultra runs it for days. With solar feeding the EcoFlow during the day, Starlink is basically free power at camp.

No inverter. No fuse tap. No 10-gauge wire through the firewall. Just a simple plug.

And if I ever want to pull the whole system out at the cabin in PA, lend it, sell the truck I can simply unbolt the dish, pull the cable, unsnap the bracket, gone. Try that with a hardwired setup.

The difference is $14 a month. That's two gas station coffees, a protein bar and a yogurt. For satellite internet that works in places where your phone shows "No Service" and OnStar shows nothing at all.

I'm not anti-OnStar. It works fine within cell range. But I bought a truck to go places that aren't within cell range. At a dispersed site in the PA mountains or running a forest road in West Virginia, OnStar is a $36 paperweight. Starlink doesn't care where you are as long as it can see sky.

If you're only doing established campgrounds with cell service, save your money. But if you're going where we go especially with a family, the extra $14 isn't even a question. Check Starlink availability for your area here.

What's Next

This build is about 90% done. Here's what I'm still working on:

  • Grommet routing: The cable path from the cab-bed gap into the bed needs to be finalized. It works but it's not clean enough for me yet. I want a proper waterproof grommet so I'm not thinking about it every time it rains.

  • Speed testing: I want real numbers from real locations. Highway speeds while driving, camp speeds at various sites, performance in heavy tree cover vs. open meadow. I'll add a speed test section to this article once I've got enough data.

  • Cold weather performance: Haven't had it through a real cold snap yet. The dish has a built-in heater for snow melt, which draws more power. Need to see what that does to my EcoFlow consumption on a winter camping trip and Sprint is right around the corner. Unless old man Winter snaps back with one last blast.

  • Long-term durability: Bugs, road grime, tree sap, UV exposure and how does the dish hold up after 6 months on the roof? A year? Will definitely be reporting back.

If you're building something similar, use the vehicle profile tool to check your payload with all this added weight. Maybe 60-70 pounds total for the Starlink stuff and MOLLE panels on top of rack weight, but if you're already running a roof top tent and loaded bed, every pound matters.

Worried about aero drag? The fuel and range planner can show you the impact. Short answer: the Ceros fairing does most of the work and the dish is flat enough that it's not a meaningful drag adder.

Bottom Line

I sold my Jeep Rubicon during COVID to an engineer that gave me an offer I couldn't refuse and went without a real overlanding rig for years. I had a 2007 Honda Accord, then a 2023 Canyon with problems, now this Sierra. Building this truck back up the right piece by piece has been one of the better things I've done for my mind.

The Starlink addition changed how my family uses the truck. My wife went from tolerating overnight trips to actually suggesting them. Kids have wifi, she can check in with her parents, I can pull up weather radar when the sky looks sketchy and write an article or two for this site before bed. Nobody's cut off.

Is it "cheating" at overlanding? Some guys on the forums think so. But perhaps those guys don't have three kids and they probably weren't running generators in 100-degree heat after Hurricane Helene so people could keep their insulin cold. Connectivity saves lives. Having it available doesn't mean you have to use it but it's insurance and when you need it, you need it.

Total hardware cost: dish, cover, mobile mount, wire hider under $400 plus $50 a month. Works at the PA cabin, dispersed sites in the Pine Barrens, on the highway, in my driveway. Minus the $36 OnStar hotspot, the real monthly cost is $14.

That's the easiest mod decision I've made on this build.

I'll keep updating this article as I finalize the cable routing, run speed tests at different locations, and put some real miles on the setup. Bookmark it and check back.
