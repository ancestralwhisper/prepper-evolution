Family Prepping With Kids: What Nobody Tells You

I have a cabin in North Central Pennsylvania and I frequent it quite often. Sometimes I take a solo weekend trip up there, other times I go with my family, sometimes my brother in law and I go together to do some work, sometimes he goes with his family. It's a mutual investment between my brother in law and I with the same idea in mind – to get out into nature and unplug from the chaotic world around us.

I took my family up there for New Years Eve in December of 2021. We all enjoy hiking and like to find new places to discover and check out the scenery. On New Years Day we traveled to Morris Township, PA to go for a hike on a new trail. The temperature was about 50 degrees Fahrenheit, which was quite warm for that time of year. There was overcast and it was misting out. We were about 2.5 miles into the hike and there was a tree with 4 trail blazers on it and continued on the trail path. We trekked a mile up the mountain on a single track and got to a widened flat spot where we could take a quick break and I could take my phone out to check AllTrails to verify we were going in the right direction. I discovered we were supposed to take the yellow trail blazer, which was a hard right at the intersection instead of following the orange trail blazer straight up the mountain. We turned around and headed back down the mountain toward the trail intersection. I was at the vanguard, my three kids were behind me and my wife took the back.





As we descended I heard a sound that was not something I thought I would ever hear at that time of year and not one I wanted to hear in the middle of a hike with my family for that matter. It was the sound of a rattle. Yes, a rattlesnake on the edge of the single track coiled up in the dead leaves well within striking distance of my kids. I must have just missed it with my trekking pole, however my oldest son almost struck it with his trekking pole which set the rattler off. I stopped dead in my tracks and calmly told them not to make any sudden moves and to not be scared. I told Caiden to slowly raise his trekking pole up slowly and gently move it away from the rattlesnake. As soon as he did that the rattling slowed and stopped. I had to take a quick picture of it of course and we slowly moved away from it and went our way as did that rattlesnake.

That was the moment that made me realize things could have gone really wrong, really quickly and I started prepping more like a dad instead of just an ordinary prepper. And to be honest, everything I thought I knew went straight out the window that day.



Here's a little background on me. I'm not former military and I'm no tactical operator. I'm a regular guy from New Jersey with three kids, a wife who thankfully doesn't think I'm crazy for doing this, and our cabin up in PA that we use as much for practice as we do for family weekends. I've been a lineman for a power company for 22 years and have seen a lot of wild and crazy things. I've been sent to California, Texas, Florida, Long Island, New York, Maine and all over New Jersey for mutual aid and disaster restoration. I've seen what happens when infrastructure fails and people aren't ready. I wasn't ready for that rattlesnake on the first day of January while hiking and it took me by surprise. Life throws things at you that you do not expect and the same goes for my line of work and things I've seen out in the field.

Being very much of a prepper my entire life I was solely focused on prepping for myself. When I got married my prepping focus shifted to my wife and I and when we had kids I had to adapt to that as well. With that being said, over the years this is what I've figured out so far.

Your Family Is Not a Squad

Stop prepping like it is. I did.

You watch youtube videos, read blogs, and buy gear. And almost all of that content is recorded or written by one guy, for one guy. He's got one good pack and one good rifle. His plan is to move fast, move light, and survive. That's all well and good for him. Now throw a kid who needs to pee every 45 minutes, another one who won't eat anything that isn't chicken nuggets or Goldfish, and a third who just learned what tornadoes are and is now terrified of wind.

That's not a squad. That's a family. And this is the difference that matters more than most prepper content will ever tell you.

At that point your gear list changes. The entirety of your timeline changes. Your planning of any routes change. The way you think about communications changes. And maybe the biggest and most important thing that changes is your mental model. You're not optimizing for YOUR survival any longer. You're optimizing for the slowest, the most emotional, and the least experienced person in your group. And that person is four feet tall and wants their blanket. They want to be a kid and think like a kid and do what kids do.

I spent way too long building out my own setup before I realized I was basically prepping for a solo camping trip while my wife and kids had nothing tailored for them. Gear that works for a 250 pound adult doesn't work for a kid. A headlamp that fits your head falls off theirs if it's not adjusted properly. A water bottle they can't open is useless and a bag rated to 20 degrees means nothing if they kick it off at 2 in the morning because they always seem to kick it off at 2 am.

The Mira Safety CM-2M gas masks I purchased for them when they were smaller do not fit anymore, so I had to buy new adult size masks for them. I was planning and prepping with the current times for the immediate unknown at that time. What I should have done was bought the kids sized masks and the adult size masks at the same time knowing they would have eventually grown out of them. That's the thing with kids vs. adults. Adults have stopped growing while kids are still growing. I buy them a decent pair of sneakers, not the most expensive ones, because I know that in 3 months that sneaker may not fit any more and I'll be buying them another pair. These are just some of the dynamics that have to be taken into consideration.

My kids can carry their own bug out bag if they wanted to. I still have to take into consideration their size, how much they should be carrying and how much they would actually be able to carry in a prolonged trek if they do, but I don't let them yet. Most of the time they have a pack with the items they bring for themselves like their ipads, some chewing gum or candy, their headsets, fidget spinners or whatever else they grab.

What we do have is a family pack that has the essentials and gear in that. I want to share something with you that'll give you some food for thought. If you fill a glass of water up and hold it for a few seconds it's really no big deal, you think you can actually hold it all day. If you hold a glass of water for a prolonged period of time I can guarantee it will become uncomfortable. If you try to hold that glass of water all day you can bet that your arm is going to be extremely fatigued and tired of holding that simple glass of water. The same example is how I think when I buy gear for my kids BOB's, which is why it's just a family pack.

When I was doing an inventory check on mine and my wife's bug out bags and had everything neatly laid out on the table she was looking over our things and she said to me, “These things are great if we need them, but what about the kids”. That hit me in the gut harder than the feeling when I lose my wallet. I always thought that she would have the kids end of things taken care of supply wise with the mom mentality. It never dawned on me until that moment that the scope of things goes deeper than that prepping for a family. There is much more involved on every aspect of prepping than I ever thought about previously.

The Stuff Nobody Puts on the Checklist

Every bug out bag list on the internet has the same basic stuff. A water filter, a fire starter, a first aid kit, paracord, and a knife. And yeah, you need all of those essential survival things. However, there is a problem and here's what none of those lists include - the stuff that actually keeps a family functional when things go wonky.

A stuffed animal. I'm deadly serious. Whatever your child sleeps with, that thing weighs maybe four ounces and that simple thing could buy you four hours of a calm child. Four hours of a kid who can finally sleep instead of crying. Four hours where you're not managing a meltdown while also trying to set up shelter or figure out your next move or all of the other things going through your mind that you're trying to process. That stuffed animal is operational gear and I will die on this hill.

More comfort items like coloring books and crayons or even a deck of UNO cards. Something that is going to keep kids occupied during down time or waiting. Here's what people don't think about: most emergency situations involve A LOT of waiting. You're waiting for the storm to pass, waiting for the road to clear, waiting at a shelter, waiting for information. Adults can sit with uncertainty and children get bored very quickly and cannot. And a bored, scared kid makes everything harder for everyone.

On the medical end is children's Tylenol, children's Benadryl and if your kid takes any prescriptions. And I'm not talking adult doses you'll try to split – I'm talking actual children's medications in children's dosages. This sounds obvious but I guarantee half the people reading this don't have specific meds for their kids in their emergency supplies.

You need to pack snacks they will actually eat. Not your freeze dried Mountain House (which honestly isn't too bad) and not MREs. Snacks your kids already eat at home. Granola bars they like. Crackers they recognize. Fruit snacks. Whatever they eat and like, because a stressed kid will not eat unfamiliar food. Well, my little guy in an exception, he'll try just about anything, but most kids they just won't. And then you've got a hungry, stressed kid and that's just worse.

You have to think about all of their school documents, immunization records, copies of birth certificates, insurance cards. If you're evacuating across state lines, you may need to prove these are your children. That's a sentence I hate writing but it's real and that can happen.

One Summer we jumped in the minivan and decided to head up to the Pocono's to camp for the weekend. There was some light thunderstorms that rolled through later in the evening as the kids were all sleeping and my wife and I were relaxing just before we fell asleep. She heard a noise outside the tent and said something about it and I told her it was just the thunder she was hearing. Then I heard it too and a deep throaty growl and some rustling. I thought it was a bear, so I started making loud noises with my voice. By this time the kids were all up, scared and wondering what was going on. I grabbed my flashlight and unzipped the tent to take a peak and seen a juvenile raccoon grunting and growling shaking a Pringles can back and forth. The sounds were heard were from the can hitting the metal fire pit ring and making a metal clinking sound. One of the kids must have left their Pringles cans somewhere I didn't notice before bed and the raccoon sniffed it out and got its head too far in the tube. It finally got it off and scurried away. We all started laughing a bit afterwards. I went out and grabbed the Pringles can and saved it. That can now lives in our family kit and I keep it there with some other items that could fit inside so it's not just wasted space. I am saving that because it's now a family memento that is not only a conversation piece, but something I could break out and tell the story over to take their minds off of anything that may be going on in the world around us.

Teaching Kids Without Terrifying Them

This is the part I've screwed up more than once.

There's a line in the sand between "prepared" and "paranoid" and kids can feel which side you're on. They pick up on everything and they're always listening even when you think they're not. The tone of your voice when you check the weather. The way you react when the power flickers (well my wife anyway. I know what's happening when the power flickers). Whether this stuff feels like a fun family project or a countdown to something terrible.

So here's what at least works in my house. We go camping a lot and I never once have told my kids "we're practicing for an emergency." We're just camping. I'm teaching them to build a fire because fires are cool. We're filtering water from the creek because that's what you do when you're out in the woods. We're figuring out how to set up a tarp because it might rain and we don't want to get wet.

Every single one of those is a survival skill, but the kids don't know that. They think they're having fun, which they are, but they're also building skills and confidence that will matter if things ever get serious.

One weekend we were up at the cabin and I was teaching my son how to build a Paiute deadfall trap. The trap was all set and done and he asked what it was for. I told him it's for catching small animals in case you need some protein. He responded and said, “yeah, but when we go camping you already have all the food in the cooler that we eat”. I then had to explain to him that sometimes people get lost in the woods and can't figure out how to get out. They run out of food and don't know how to catch their own food until they figure out how to get out of the woods and that this is a skill you can take with you and actually use it in case he was ever in that situation.

The family communication plan is another thing and it has to be simple enough for a kid to remember when they're scared. It can't be a laminated card with 47 steps. It has to be something like: if something happens at school, who picks you up. If we get separated, where do we meet. If you can't reach Mom, call Grandma. Three things. Maybe four. That's it. It has to be stupid simple and easy to remember.

And here's what needs to happen. You have to let them mess it up during practice. Let the fire go out. Let them pack their bag wrong and realize they forgot something on a camping trip when the stakes are zero. Those small failures teach more than any lecture you'll ever give. At work I'll watch the apprentices in the air. I'm not going to yell at them for making a mistake unless they're putting themselves in absolute danger where they could have a flash with secondary or primary wires. But, I'll calmly watch them make general mistakes and allow them to make the mistakes. Why? I'm not trying to be an ass, but if I tell exactly what to do every step of the way they are not going to remember it. If they unknowingly make a mistake now and are not thinking about their moves 5 steps ahead and it totally screws them, then they have to now deal with it and figure out how to make it work, or cut some wire out and do it all over. It's a learning curve, except when I was an apprentice not only would they let me make the mistake, if anybody made a mistake you'd have to buy the guys on the crew lunch. It hit you mentally and it hit you in your wallet and that's just the way it was.

The Bug Out Bag Problem

I've written about family bug out bags before and the single biggest takeaway is that a family of five cannot function with five individual bags packed like five solo operators would. 15 minutes into a hike I can almost bet you that one of them is going to ask my wife or I to carry their bag for them. A couple of hours into the hike I can almost guarantee you that my youngest son is going to ask if he can go on my shoulders.

Everybody, especially kids having their own bags doesn't work. A seven year old can carry maybe five pounds before they're miserable. Your spouse might have a different fitness level than you. And if you're honest about it, which most preppers aren't, your on foot range with kids is maybe two or three miles before someone's done as I pointed out before.

So your vehicle becomes your mobile bug out command system. Period. At least for the first move. I drive a 2025 Sierra and I've been building it out specifically with the family in mind. Not with a bunch of tactical bolt on things. I've been building it out with the family in mind. I'm talking about organized storage that actually makes sense, supplies we can get to without unpacking everything, and enough fuel range to get to our cabin without stopping. I want it to feel and be like a basecamp where ever we go. That means for me it has to be easy to set up and break down. Things need their own dedicated home on the truck for where they get stored.

Transitioning from prepping for myself to family and camping and overlanding changed my approach - turning my daily driver into a fully adaptable command center. I've EMP shielded my rig, I have a spacious rooftop tent for the family, and a 95L fridge keeping food fresh, the focus is now on keeping the family safe, dry, and as comfortable as possible.

The brutally honest truth that nobody in the prepper community wants to say out loud... if you have kids under 10, bugging out on foot for any real distance is a fantasy. You should plan around your vehicle and have a backup plan if the vehicle doesn't work. But your primary plan should assume you're driving, because you will be.

By the way for those of you who have any children that need a car seat, a booster, or a stroller. Whelp, you need one in your bug out plan. I know that sounds like a ridiculous thing to mention in a survival context but try carrying a 35 pound kid for two miles and then tell me the stroller was a dumb thing to plan around.

The Emotional Weight Not Talked About

Here's where it gets gritty.

Prepping as a single person is totally exciting. All the new gear, new skills, all the "what if" scenarios that feel like a video game you're strategizing for. Prepping as a parent? It's heavy. Because every scenario you plan for, you're picturing your kids in it. And that changes everything about how it sits in your head.

When I watch prepper related movies like “The Road” or “The Book of Eli” it is a really bitter pill to swallow when I have to think about a scenario like that and mix my own kids into the picture. When I am going through the gear I set there and think to myself If something were to happen to my wife and I, would they make it? And I go down that dark rabbit hole nobody ever wants to go down and never hopes to go down. There is nothing exciting about that at all in any way.

And then there's the conversation between you and your spouse that goes something like: "What if it happens and only one of us is home?" That conversation sucks. But you need to have it. Who grabs the kids from school? Where do you meet? What if the phones don't work? What if one of you is at work 40 miles away? Yeah, that's me.

We had the conversation and have our plans in place for different situations, whether it be hunkering down at home or bugging out and had that conversation with my brother in law as well, so we have multi family prep plans.

One other thing and I think this might be the most important thing in this entire article is balance. You can prep yourself into a hole where every family dinner has a shadow cast over it. Where every vacation you plan you're also scouting routes. Where your kids start to feel like something bad is always about to happen.

You can't let it get to that point where it's noticeable all the time. Don't do that.

You have to maintain a sense of balance. Most people know the work/life balance, but we need to have the work/life/prep balance. Prep on Tuesday night after bedtime one day during the week. Enjoy your life the rest of the time. Go to the baseball game on Saturday and don't think about your water supply. Take your kids camping and let it just be camping sometimes. The whole point of prepping is so your family gets to keep living a normal life, even when things aren't normal. If prepping has become the vampire of operating the normal life, you've lost the plot.

One Last Thing

I'd like to close this up with a small piece of advice for all the other dads or moms out there. Prepping is the single most reliant thing you can do for your family. It builds stability, peace of mind, and practicality in your life and your children's lives. A bug out bag is insurance you'll have what you need in the time that's calling for it. As we continue to prep we also adapt to our circumstances in life and we evolve into better individuals as we gain more knowledge and skills. So keep prepping and in the process help your community and neighbors, even if they don't know you're a prepper. Help them discover prepping in their own way, because everybody is really a prepper – it's just a matter of what kind of prepper they are and the extent they will go with their preps.
