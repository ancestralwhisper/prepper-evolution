Your Family Is Not a Squad

Stop prepping like it is.

Look, I get it. You watch videos, read blogs, buy gear. And almost all of that content is written by one guy, for one guy. One pack. One rifle. One plan. Move fast, move light, survive. Cool. Now add a kid who needs to pee every 45 minutes, another one who won't eat anything that isn't chicken nuggets or Goldfish crackers, and a third who just learned what tornadoes are and is now terrified of wind.

That's not a squad. That's a family. And the difference matters more than most prepper content will ever tell you.

Your gear list changes. Your timeline changes. Your route changes. The way you communicate changes. And maybe the biggest thing — your mental model has to change. You're not optimizing for YOUR survival. You're optimizing for the slowest, most emotional, least experienced person in your group. And that person is four feet tall and wants their blanket.

I spent way too long building out my own setup before I realized I was basically prepping for a solo camping trip while my wife and kids had nothing tailored to them. Gear that works for a 200-pound adult doesn't scale down to a kid. A headlamp that fits your head falls off theirs. A water bottle they can't open is useless. A sleeping bag rated to 20 degrees means nothing if they kick it off at 2am because they always kick it off at 2am.

The Stuff Nobody Puts on the Checklist

Every bug out bag list on the internet has the same stuff. Water filter. Fire starter. First aid kit. Paracord. Knife. And yeah, you need all of that. But here's what none of those lists include — the stuff that actually keeps a family functional when things go sideways.

A stuffed animal. I'm dead serious. Whatever your kid sleeps with, that thing weighs maybe four ounces and it buys you four hours of a calm child. Four hours of a kid who sleeps instead of crying. Four hours where you're not managing a meltdown while also trying to set up shelter or figure out your next move. That stuffed animal is operational gear. I will die on this hill.

Coloring books and crayons. A deck of cards. Something — anything — to keep kids occupied during the waiting. Because here's what people don't think about: most emergency situations involve a LOT of waiting. Waiting for the storm to pass. Waiting for the road to clear. Waiting at a shelter. Waiting for information. Adults can sit with uncertainty. Kids cannot. And a bored, scared kid makes everything harder for everyone.

Children's Tylenol. Children's Benadryl. Whatever prescriptions your kids take. Not adult doses you'll try to split — actual children's medications in children's dosages. This sounds obvious but I guarantee half the people reading this don't have kid-specific meds in their emergency supplies.

Snacks they will actually eat. Not your freeze-dried Mountain House (which honestly isn't bad). Not MREs. Snacks your kids already eat at home. Granola bars they like. Crackers they recognize. Fruit snacks. Whatever. Because a stressed kid will not eat unfamiliar food. They just won't. And then you've got a hungry, stressed kid and that's — yeah, that's worse.

School documents. Immunization records. Copies of birth certificates. Insurance cards. If you're evacuating across state lines, you may need to prove these are your children. That's a sentence I hate writing but it's real.

Teaching Kids Without Terrifying Them

This is the part I've screwed up more than once.

There's a line between "prepared" and "paranoid" and kids can feel which side you're on. They pick up on everything. The tone of your voice when you check the weather. The way you react when the power flickers. Whether this stuff feels like a fun family project or a countdown to something terrible.

So here's what actually works — at least in my house. We camp. A lot. And I never once have told my kids "we're practicing for an emergency." We're just camping. We're learning to build a fire because fires are cool. We're filtering water from the creek because that's what you do when you're in the woods. We're figuring out how to set up a tarp because it might rain and we don't want to get wet.

Every single one of those is a survival skill. The kids don't know that. They think they're having fun. And they are having fun. But they're also building skills and confidence that will matter if things ever get serious.

The family communication plan is another thing. And it has to be simple enough for a kid to remember when they're scared. Not a laminated card with 47 steps. Something like: if something happens at school, who picks you up. If we get separated, where do we meet. If you can't reach Mom, call Grandma. Three things. Maybe four. That's it.

And here's a thing — let them mess up during practice. Let the fire go out. Let them pack their bag wrong and realize they forgot something on a camping trip when the stakes are zero. Those small failures teach more than any lecture you'll ever give.

The Bug Out Bag Problem

I've written about family bug out bags before and the single biggest takeaway is this: a family of five cannot function with five individual bags packed like five solo operators.

It doesn't work. A seven-year-old can carry maybe five pounds before they're miserable. Your spouse might have a different fitness level than you. And if you're honest about it — which most preppers aren't — your on-foot range with kids is maybe two or three miles before someone's done.

So your vehicle IS your bug out system. Period. At least for the first move. I drive a 2025 Sierra and I'm building it out specifically with the family in mind. Not with a bunch of tactical bolt-ons. With kid seats, organized storage that actually makes sense, supplies we can get to without unpacking everything, and enough fuel range to get to our cabin without stopping.

The honest truth that nobody in the prepper community wants to say out loud: if you have kids under 10, bugging out on foot for any real distance is a fantasy. Plan around your vehicle. Have a backup plan if the vehicle doesn't work, sure. But your primary plan should assume you're driving. Because you will be.

Car seats and boosters, by the way. If your kid needs one, you need one in your bug out plan. I know that sounds like a ridiculous thing to mention in a survival context but try carrying a 35-pound kid for two miles and then tell me the car seat was a dumb thing to plan around.

The Emotional Weight Nobody Talks About

Here's where it gets real.

Prepping as a single person is kind of exciting. New gear, new skills, "what if" scenarios that feel like a video game you're strategizing for. Prepping as a parent? It's heavy. Because every scenario you plan for, you're picturing your kids in it. And that changes everything about how it sits in your head.

The other thing — and I think this might be the most important thing in this entire article — is balance. You can prep yourself into a hole where every family dinner has a shadow over it. Where every vacation you're also scouting routes. Where your kids start to feel like something bad is always about to happen.

Don't do that.

Prep on Tuesday night after bedtime. Enjoy Wednesday. Go to the soccer game on Saturday and don't think about your water supply. Take your kids camping and let it just be camping sometimes. The whole point of prepping is so your family gets to keep living a normal life, even when things aren't normal. If the prepping is stealing the normal life, you've lost the plot.
