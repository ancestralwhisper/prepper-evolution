72 Hours Is a Lie

I'm going to say something that FEMA won't. 72 hours is not enough.

It never has been. Not once in any major disaster I've seen — and I've worked mutual aid electrical restoration after hurricanes in Florida and Texas, ice storms in Maine, fires in California. Not a single one of those events was cleaned up in three days. Most lasted a week minimum. Some stretched into months.

Hurricane Maria. Puerto Rico was without power for ELEVEN MONTHS. Hurricane Harvey — Houston was underwater for weeks. Even smaller, more localized events regularly leave people without power, water, or access to stores for 7 to 14 days.

So yeah, 72 hours is a great starting point. But if you stop there, you're building half a house. Let's build the whole thing.

The 14-Day Mindset Shift

Going from 72 hours to 14 days isn't just "buy more stuff." The whole approach changes. At 72 hours, you're thinking about grabbing a bag and surviving. At 14 days, you're thinking about sustainment. Comfort. Sanitation. Morale. Mental health.

Because here's what nobody tells you about being stuck without power for two weeks: it's boring. And boring with stressed-out kids in a dark house is a special kind of miserable.

My family of five — me, my wife, three kids — could run on our current setup for well beyond 14 days. But it took time to build. You don't have to do this all at once. Start with the 72-Hour Kit Builder and then scale up from there.

Water: The Non-Negotiable

One gallon per person per day. That's the standard recommendation and honestly it's barely enough — it doesn't account for cooking, cleaning, or hot weather. But let's use it as a baseline.

Family of five, 14 days: 70 gallons.

That's a lot of water. You're not stacking bottled water cases for that. Here's how I handle it:

  • 14 gallons in 1-gallon jugs (rotated every 6 months) — that's your immediate, grab-and-drink supply
  • Two 7-gallon Reliance Aqua-Tainer jugs — sturdy, stackable, have a spigot
  • WaterBOB bathtub liner (fills your tub with 100 gallons when you get warning of an incoming event)
  • Sawyer Squeeze filter + Aquamira drops for treating additional water sources
  • Two cases of bottled water for the kids — because they're kids and they drink from water bottles

Food: Two Weeks Worth

For 14 days, you need a mix of no-cook foods (days 1-3, when you might not have a stove or your routines are disrupted), simple-cook foods (days 4-7, when you've figured out your cooking situation), and real meals (days 8-14, because morale matters and nobody wants protein bars for two straight weeks).

No-Cook / Grab-and-Eat

  • Peanut butter — jars, not packets. You're at home, weight doesn't matter like it does in a bug out bag
  • Crackers (rotate these — they go stale)
  • Canned tuna/chicken
  • Granola bars and protein bars
  • Dried fruit and nuts
  • Canned fruit in syrup (the kids love this)

Simple-Cook (just add boiling water or heat)

  • Instant oatmeal
  • Ramen noodles (yeah, I said it — they're lightweight, cheap, and my kids eat them)
  • Mac and cheese boxes
  • Instant rice and beans (separately or those Knorr rice side packs)
  • Freeze-dried meals like Mountain House 14-Day Supply
  • Soup — canned, condensed, whatever. Hot soup on day 9 of a power outage is a morale bomb

Real Meals (canned goods that are actual food)

  • Canned chili
  • Canned beef stew
  • Canned pasta (Chef Boyardee — judge me, my kids are happy)
  • Spam (high calorie, shelf stable for years)
  • Canned vegetables — green beans, corn, mixed veggies
  • Canned beans — black beans, kidney beans, chickpeas

For a complete breakdown of what canned goods give you the most bang for the buck, check out the best canned food for survival guide.

Cooking Without Grid Power

You need a way to cook that doesn't depend on your stove (which might be electric or might need electricity to ignite). Options:

  • Propane camp stove (Coleman 2-burner is the standard — have 4-6 propane canisters)
  • Butane single burner (great for indoor use with ventilation)
  • Charcoal grill (you probably already have one)
  • Solo Stove or similar wood-burning camp stove

I have a Coleman propane stove and a butane burner. Between the two, we can cook hot meals for weeks. Total investment: maybe $60.

Power and Light

This is where I geek out a little because I've lived this one.

My primary home backup power is the EcoFlow DELTA 3 Ultra — 3,072Wh of LiFePO4 battery with 3,600W output. It'll run my fridge for about 24-30 hours on a single charge, and with the two 220W solar panels I have, I can top it off during the day and basically run indefinitely as long as we get some sun.

But you don't need a $1,500 power station for a 14-day kit. Here's a tiered approach:

Minimum (under $50)

  • LED lanterns — get 3-4 of them, one per room you'll actually use
  • Headlamps for each family member
  • AA/AAA batteries — lots of them
  • A battery-powered or hand-crank radio (NOAA weather band is non-negotiable)
  • Power banks for phone charging

Better ($200-500)

  • Everything above, plus a mid-size power station (300-500Wh range) for phone/device charging and maybe running a small fan
  • A 100W portable solar panel to recharge the station

Best ($1,000+)

  • Large power station (2,000Wh+) that can run a fridge, medical devices, router
  • 200-400W of solar panels
  • Automatic transfer switch for seamless home backup

Our Solar Power Calculator can help you figure out exactly what size system you need based on what you want to keep running. And the full breakdown of power station vs generator helps you decide which path makes sense for your situation.

Sanitation — The Part Nobody Wants to Talk About

If your water is off or your sewer system is compromised (which happens in floods), you need a plan for this. For 14 days. For five people.

I'll spare you the romance. Here's what you need:

  • 5-gallon bucket with a snap-on toilet seat lid (Luggable Loo or similar — $20)
  • Heavy-duty trash bags (lots)
  • Kitty litter or enzyme-based waste treatment powder
  • Hand sanitizer — big bottles
  • Baby wipes (even if you don't have a baby — they're the closest thing to a shower you'll get)
  • Feminine hygiene products
  • Toilet paper — more than you think you need
  • Trash bags for waste disposal

This isn't glamorous. It's not Instagram-worthy. But two weeks without a working toilet is how diseases start spreading. Handle it before it handles you.

Medical and First Aid

Your 72-hour first aid kit covers cuts and headaches. A 14-day kit needs to account for longer-term medical needs:

  • 30-day supply of all prescription medications (talk to your doctor about getting an extra fill)
  • OTC meds: ibuprofen, acetaminophen, antihistamine, anti-diarrheal, antacid
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotic ointment
  • Larger wound care supplies — Israeli bandages, QuikClot, butterfly closures
  • Thermometer
  • SPF lip balm and sunscreen (sounds small, matters a lot after day 5)
  • Moleskin (if you're doing any walking)
  • Any infant/child-specific meds your kids need

The prescription medication thing is big. If someone in your family depends on daily meds — blood pressure, insulin, thyroid, whatever — a 14-day disruption without them can be genuinely dangerous. Get ahead of this.

Communications

  • NOAA weather radio (battery or crank powered)
  • FRS/GMRS radios — a pair for family communication if cell towers are down
  • Paper list of emergency contacts (phone numbers, out-of-state contact, rally points)
  • Local maps — paper, not phone

Cell towers typically have 4-8 hours of battery backup. After that, if the grid is down, so is your cell service. Plan for that.

Morale and Comfort

Fourteen days is a long time. Especially with kids. Don't neglect this stuff:

  • Card games, board games, books
  • Coloring books and crayons for younger kids
  • Downloaded movies/shows on tablets (if you have power to charge them)
  • Comfort items — stuffed animals, blankets, whatever makes your kids feel safe
  • Coffee and tea (I am not functional without coffee, full stop)
  • Candy or treats — a bag of Jolly Ranchers costs a dollar and buys goodwill for days

This isn't soft. This is realistic. Morale in a prolonged emergency is a survival tool. I've seen grown men break down on day 4 of a deployment not because of danger, but because of boredom, uncertainty, and discomfort. Give your family things that maintain normalcy.

Security

Not going to go deep on this because it's a sensitive topic, but 14 days of grid-down means some level of security awareness. Lock your doors. Know your neighbors. Have a plan for if someone tries to enter your home. A bright flashlight is as much a defensive tool as anything else — 1,000 lumens in someone's face at 2 AM is extremely disorienting.

The Master List

Here's the full 14-day emergency kit list, consolidated:

Water

  • 70+ gallons stored water (1 gal/person/day)
  • Water filtration (Sawyer Squeeze + Aquamira)
  • WaterBOB or similar bathtub bladder

Food

  • 3 days of no-cook food per person
  • 11 days of cook-able food per person (canned, dried, freeze-dried)
  • Camp stove + fuel
  • Manual can opener (don't forget this)
  • Paper plates, plastic utensils, paper towels

Power and Light

  • LED lanterns (3-4)
  • Headlamps (one per person)
  • Batteries (lots)
  • Power station + solar panels (if budget allows)
  • Power banks for phones
  • NOAA weather radio

Sanitation

  • Emergency toilet setup
  • Trash bags, cleaning supplies
  • Hygiene basics

Medical

  • Extended first aid kit
  • 30-day prescription meds
  • OTC medications

Documents

  • Copies of IDs, insurance, medical records in waterproof container
  • Cash in small bills ($500+ recommended)

Morale

  • Entertainment for all ages
  • Comfort items
  • Coffee/tea

What This Costs

If you're starting from scratch, a basic 14-day kit for a family of four costs roughly $300-500. That sounds like a lot until you compare it to one night in a hotel during an evacuation — which can run $200+ per night if rooms are even available. And they usually aren't.

Spread it out over a few months. Buy one category each paycheck. By the end of the quarter, you're set.

Or just run the 72-Hour Kit Builder to get started, then keep building from there. You don't have to do everything today. You just have to start today.

One More Thing

Test your kit. Pick a weekend. Turn off the breakers. Don't go to the store. Live out of your supplies for 48 hours. You'll find out real fast what you forgot, what you need more of, and what you packed that you never touched.

It's also, weirdly, kind of fun. The kids treat it like camping. My wife tolerates it. I learn something every time.

Fourteen days of preparedness isn't paranoia. It's what people who've lived through real emergencies wish they'd had. I've seen it firsthand. Don't be the family scrambling for batteries at Walmart after the warning drops. Be the family that's already set.
