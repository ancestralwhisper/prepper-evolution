### 1. Bug Out Bag Weight Calculator

**[OPEN on the BOB Calculator page]**

Most bug out bag lists on the internet are written by people who've never actually tried to carry one. You end up with a 65-pound pack, and two miles in, you're done. You're not bugging out — you're a liability.

The BOB calculator here fixes that. The rule is simple: your loaded pack should weigh no more than 20 to 25 percent of your body weight. If you're 180 pounds, that's 36 to 45 pounds max. If you're 130 pounds, you're working with about 32 pounds. That's less than you think once you add everything.

Here's how to use it. First thing — put in your body weight up top. That sets your personal carry limit. The calculator will track against that number the whole time.

**[scroll down to categories]**

Then you work through the categories. Water, food, shelter, fire, navigation, first aid, tools, comms, documents, clothing. Each item has a checkbox and a weight. Check what you own or what you're planning to carry. The total updates live as you go.

A few things I want you to pay attention to. The water section — you need 2 liters minimum carried on you, plus a filter. The filter is what keeps you going after that runs out. Don't skip it. And in the first aid section, there's a tourniquet listed. That's not optional. That's the one piece of kit that's saved more lives than anything else in that bag.

**[scroll to weight summary]**

Watch this number at the top. When it goes yellow, you're getting close. When it goes red, you're over and you need to start cutting. The calculator flags what's heaviest so you know where to look first.

When you're happy with the list, you can export it to PDF. Print it, put it in your bag, put one on the fridge. Your family should know what's in there.

The link to every item on the list goes to the actual product I'd buy. Not the most expensive version. The one that actually works.

### 2. Water Storage Calculator

**[OPEN on the Water Storage Calculator page]**

FEMA says one gallon per person per day. That's the survival floor. That's drinking water and that's it. It doesn't cover cooking, hygiene, or flushing a toilet. It keeps you alive. It doesn't keep you functional.

The real number is closer to 2 gallons per person per day if you want to actually live like a human being for a few days while the situation gets sorted out.

Here's how to use the calculator. Start with household size — how many adults, how many kids. Kids drink less but that gap closes fast if they're sick or it's hot. Then pick your duration target. 72 hours is the minimum anyone should have. Two weeks is where you actually start to feel comfortable about your situation.

**[show the living situation selector]**

The living situation section matters. If you're in an apartment, the calculator switches its recommendations. A 55-gallon barrel isn't happening in a third-floor apartment. You're looking at stackable 5-gallon jugs, a WaterBOB for the tub, and a good filter. The calculator adjusts the container list to what actually fits.

If you've got a house with a garage, you can go bigger. Two 55-gallon barrels on a basic rack setup — that's a month of water for a family of four. About $100 in barrels, a bung wrench, and a hand pump. Not complicated.

The output shows you your total target in gallons, what container setup gets you there, and the rotation schedule so your stored water stays clean. Water you stored two years ago and forgot about is not an asset. Rotation is the whole game.

Run it once, write down the number, go buy what you need. That's it.

### 3. Food Storage Calculator

**[OPEN on the Food Storage Calculator page]**

Here's the thing about food storage — most people either way overthink it or they do nothing because they don't know where to start. Both of those end the same way.

The calculator gives you a real number based on your actual household. Not some generic FEMA list. Your people, your calories, your duration goal.

**[walk through inputs]**

Put in your household size. Adults, kids, teenagers if applicable. The calorie estimates adjust — teenagers eat more than you think, young kids eat less. Then set your duration. 72 hours is a start. 3 months is where I'd like everyone to be. One year is the long game.

**[show living situation selector]**

Same as the water calculator — if you're in an apartment, the recommendations shift. You don't have room for 400 pounds of rice in 5-gallon buckets. The calculator knows that and adjusts for calorie density. Peanut butter, olive oil, pasta — things that pack a lot of calories into a small footprint.

**[scroll to output section]**

The output breaks down what to buy, how much of each thing, total weight, estimated cost, and shelf life. White rice is the backbone — 3,400 calories per pound, 25-year shelf life in sealed Mylar bags. Beans are second. Together, rice and beans cover the calorie math for almost any duration at a price that doesn't destroy your budget.

The rotation schedule at the bottom is important. First in, first out. Date everything when you put it away. Check your canned goods every year. Sealed bulk staples every three to five years.

Do this once, buy it in stages over a few months, and you're done. You never have to think about it again until you need it.

### 4. Solar & Power Station Calculator

**[OPEN on the Solar Power Calculator page]**

A power outage in February isn't a camping trip. You're running your heat blower, you're keeping medication refrigerated, you're charging phones so you can get information, and you might be running a CPAP at night. That's a real power budget and you need to know what it is before you buy anything.

The calculator builds your load from scratch based on your actual devices and how long you run them each day.

**[walk through device inputs]**

Add your devices from the list — fridge, phone chargers, lights, medical equipment, router, whatever you're actually running. Set the daily hours for each. The watt-hours column updates as you go. That's your daily energy need.

**[show location/ZIP section]**

Put in your ZIP code. This matters. The calculator pulls your region's peak sun hours — how many usable hours of solar generation you get per day. Someone in Arizona gets way more solar production than someone in upstate New York in January. The panel recommendation adjusts for that.

**[show living situation for apartment cap]**

If you're in an apartment, the calculator caps the recommendations. You're not running a 400-watt solar array off your balcony. It shifts to suggest smaller, more practical setups — a single 100-watt panel on a window mount paired with a mid-size power station.

**[scroll to recommendations]**

The output matches you to a power station size and a solar panel count. It shows charge time from solar alone, backup generator runtime if you add one, and which specific products I'd actually buy for that setup.

Run this before you buy a power station. A lot of people buy based on marketing and end up either underpowered or overpaying for capacity they never use.

### 11. Field Failure Diagnostic

**[OPEN on Field Failure Diagnostic page]**

Your water filter is pulling slow. Your stove won't light. Your solar panel stopped charging. You're in the field, you don't have a manual, and you need to figure out what's wrong right now.

### 13. Deadstock — Supply Lifecycle Manager

**[OPEN on Deadstock page]**

Deadstock is a simple concept with a hard number at the end of it. The day your household runs out of its own supplies and becomes dependent on outside help — that's your Deadstock date.

Most people have never calculated it. This tool does it for them.

**[walk through the 6 categories]**

You track six categories: water, food, medical supplies, power, communications, and gear. Each one asks how much you have and how much you use. It calculates how many days that supply lasts.

**[show the result date]**

The output is a date — the day you go from self-sufficient to dependent. For most households that haven't prepped at all, it's somewhere between 3 and 7 days. Water runs out fastest. Food usually lasts longest because people have more in their pantry than they think.

**[show the weakest link highlight]**

The tool flags your shortest timeline as your critical gap. That's where you put your energy first. Doesn't matter if you have 6 months of food if you run out of water in 4 days.

This isn't about fear. It's about knowing your actual number so you can do something about it.

### 16. Overland Load Balancer

**[OPEN on Load Balancer page]**

You can be under your payload limit and still have a dangerous load. If everything heavy is on the roof and in the back, you've got a high center of gravity and a rear-heavy axle distribution. The truck's rated for the weight — it's just not happy about where it is.

The Load Balancer catches that.

**[show vehicle pull from profile]**

Load it from your Vehicle Profile. That brings in your vehicle specs and the gear you've already logged.

**[show placement zones]**

Assign each piece of gear to a placement zone — roof front, roof rear, truck bed forward, truck bed rear, cab, tow hitch. Weight placement matters for both axle loading and CG height.

**[show live numbers]**

The live display shows total loaded weight versus GVWR, front-to-rear axle split, roof load versus rating, and a visual representation of how balanced the load is. Ideal front-to-rear for most trucks is roughly 60/40.

**[show warnings]**

Red warnings show up if your roof is overloaded, if your axle split is out of safe range, or if you've exceeded payload. These aren't theoretical — overloaded suspension is a handling and tire problem on the trail before it's a mechanical one.

### 17. RigSafe Overland Configurator

**[OPEN on RigSafe page]**

Rooftop tents are great until someone puts one on a roof it doesn't belong on. Most people install based on the RTT's weight rating and call it done. That's not the whole calculation.

RigSafe runs three chains: static rating, on-road dynamic rating, and off-road dynamic rating. Static is just the weight. On-road adds 1.5x for braking, acceleration, and cornering. Off-road adds a 3x dynamic factor for drops and impacts. Most tents that are technically within the RTT's static rating fail the off-road dynamic check.

**[walk through the configurator]**

Pick your rack system — we've got major brands in there. Add your RTT. The tool knows their weights and ratings. Add occupants if you're sleeping in it. Toggle your vehicle payload status.

**[show three-chain result]**

The three-chain result tells you where you stand. Green on all three is what you want. Yellow means you're in the caution zone and you should probably lose some weight somewhere. Red means don't go off-road with that setup.

**[show garage clearance check]**

There's also a garage clearance check at the bottom. A lot of people never think about whether they can get into their garage with a tent on the roof until the day they try.

The guided tour walks you through every section with plain-English explanations if this is your first time through.

### 23. Barter & Trade Value Estimator

**[OPEN on Barter Estimator page]**

Most people think about barter in post-collapse terms like they're playing a video game — gold, ammo, cigarettes, done. The reality from actual economic collapses — Venezuela, Yugoslavia, Argentina, post-Katrina New Orleans — is more nuanced than that.

What's valuable shifts by phase. Early emergency, day one through thirty, it's consumables and practical tools. Extended crisis, month one through twelve, it's production capacity and skills. Long-term collapse, beyond a year, it's durable goods, medicine, and knowledge.

**[show item database]**

100-plus items and 30-plus skills rated across all three phases. The ratings pull from historical data, not theory. Antibiotics are high-value in all three phases. A flat-screen TV is worthless by phase two.

**[show ROI column]**

The ROI column is the useful one. It shows you which items have the best barter value per dollar spent now, while everything is cheap and available. Fuel stabilizer, water purification tablets, quality hand tools, generic over-the-counter medications — these are cheap now and extremely valuable later.

**[show skills section]**

Don't skip the skills section. Medical skills, mechanics, electrical work, farming, and teaching are consistently high-value in every historical case study. Skills weigh nothing, don't expire, and can't be taken from you.

### 24. SITREP — Ops Deck Readiness Report

**[OPEN on SITREP page]**

SITREP stands for Situation Report. In the military it's how you communicate your current status. Here it's a scored readiness assessment against a specific scenario.

**[show scenario selector]**

Pick your scenario. Grid-down extended outage, 72-hour bug-out, wildfire evacuation, or pandemic lockdown. Each one weights the scoring categories differently. Grid-down is heavily power and food. Wildfire evac is mobility and go-bag readiness. Bug-out is vehicle, fuel, and comms.

**[show connected tools section]**

If you've already filled out your Vehicle Profile, Power System Builder, Water Calculator, or Food Storage Calculator, SITREP reads that data automatically. The sources panel shows what's connected and what's still manual.

The more tools you've filled in, the more accurate your SITREP becomes. If you haven't connected anything, you're entering estimates. If you have, it's using your real numbers.

**[walk through the scored domains]**

The report scores six domains: mobility, power, shelter, water, food, and communications. Each one gets a score from 0 to 100 and a letter grade. Your overall readiness score is the weighted average based on the scenario selected.

**[show the gap section]**

The gap section is what you act on. It tells you exactly which domain is dragging your score and the specific gaps within it. Not "improve your water supply" — the actual number of days short you are and what it would take to close that gap.

Run this for each scenario. They'll surface different gaps. Most people are weak in comms and strong in food. Most people have more water than they think but zero water treatment options. SITREP will tell you.
